\begin{abstract}
Evaluating Large Language Model (LLM) variants---quantized checkpoints, fine-tuned snapshots, LoRA adaptations---is prohibitively expensive, yet existing representational similarity metrics (e.g., CKA) provide no formal link to downstream performance.
We propose \textbf{PRISM} (Proxy Risk Inference via Structural Mapping), a geometric framework that derives a rigorous, closed-form upper bound on the risk discrepancy $|\mathcal{R}_T - \mathcal{R}_P|$ between a target and proxy model.
By decomposing feature discrepancy over orthogonal alignments into scale mismatch ($\Delta\rho$) and shape mismatch ($1 - \Omega_W$), combined with a second-moment-adjusted head bound, we obtain a closed-form upper bound computable from a single forward pass.
The choice of alignment $W$ governs a fundamental trade-off between feature-bound tightness and head-bound fidelity: the Procrustes solution $W^*$ maximizes shape alignment via the nuclear norm, while the identity alignment ($W = I$, yielding the trace similarity $\Omega_{\mathrm{tr}}$) preserves head coherence in coordinate-preserving regimes.
We instantiate the framework for post-training quantization (GGUF, GPTQ, AWQ, BitsAndBytes), where the bound reduces to a pure geometric distortion measure, and for fine-tuning (SFT, LoRA), where it isolates catastrophic forgetting in the backbone geometry.
Extensive experiments across Llama, Qwen, Mistral, and DeepSeek demonstrate strong rank correlation (Spearman's $\rho > 0.9$) between our geometric metric and actual performance degradation.
By leveraging high alignment as a variance-reduction guarantee, we further show that full-benchmark performance (e.g., 14{,}000 MMLU tasks) can be accurately forecast from a 500-sample calibration set, reducing evaluation compute by over 95\%.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}

\paragraph{The evaluation bottleneck in modern LLMs.}
Large Language Models (LLMs) have achieved remarkable success across diverse tasks, from multiple-choice reasoning to open-ended generation~\cite{grattafiori2024llama, qwen2025qwen3}.
As these models scale, their post-training lifecycle---from quantization (e.g., GPTQ, AWQ, GGUF) to parameter-efficient fine-tuning (e.g., LoRA, SFT)---generates numerous model variants, yet evaluating how each variant affects downstream performance remains prohibitively expensive.
A single comprehensive evaluation on benchmarks such as MMLU (14{,}000 tasks) can require hours of GPU time per variant, creating a severe bottleneck for practitioners who need to diagnose quality degradation, select among compression configurations, or monitor training dynamics across the model lifecycle.

\paragraph{Existing geometric metrics lack formal risk guarantees.}
Practitioners seeking to circumvent this bottleneck currently face an unsatisfying choice: either run full benchmark suites for every variant, or resort to post-hoc representational similarity metrics such as CKA~\cite{kornblith2019similarity} and SVCCA~\cite{raghu2017svcca}.
These Frobenius-norm-based metrics measure geometric resemblance between internal representations, but provide no formal guarantee that high similarity translates to small performance gaps.
A quantized model may exhibit high CKA similarity to its full-precision parent yet suffer significant accuracy degradation; conversely, a fine-tuned model may show moderate geometric drift while preserving task performance.
This disconnect between geometric similarity and functional risk leaves practitioners without principled tools for predicting when a model variant is safe to deploy.

\paragraph{PRISM: an exact geometric decomposition linking alignment to risk.}
We address this gap by proposing \textbf{PRISM} (Proxy Risk Inference via Structural Mapping), a geometric framework that derives a rigorous, computable bound linking representational alignment to functional risk.
Our key observation is that modern Transformer-based LLMs share a common structural property: a deep non-linear feature extractor (the backbone) followed by a single linear prediction head (\texttt{lm\_head}) that projects representations onto the vocabulary space.
This architecture, combined with the \emph{Linear Representation Hypothesis}~\cite{moschella2022relative, park2023linear}---that models within the same lineage learn feature manifolds related by a near-isometric linear transformation---enables an \emph{exact} geometric analysis of risk discrepancy.
Specifically, we show that for any orthogonal alignment $W$ on the Stiefel manifold, the feature discrepancy decomposes exactly into two interpretable components: \emph{scale mismatch} ($\Delta\rho$), capturing activation magnitude shifts, and \emph{shape mismatch} ($1 - \Omega_W$), capturing structural distortion of the feature manifold.
The choice of $W$ governs a fundamental trade-off: the Procrustes solution $W^*$ maximizes $\Omega_W$ to the nuclear-norm similarity $\Omega$---provably the tightest possible feature bound---while the identity alignment $W = I$ yields the trace similarity $\Omega_{\mathrm{tr}}$ and preserves head coherence when the prediction head is shared.
Combined with a second-moment-adjusted bound on head discrepancy, this yields a closed-form upper bound on risk discrepancy $|\mathcal{R}_T - \mathcal{R}_P|$ that is directly computable from a single forward pass on a small calibration set.

\paragraph{Why Large Language Models.}
Although our framework mathematically applies to any model adopting the feature-extractor-plus-linear-head paradigm, we focus empirical validation exclusively on LLMs for four reasons.
First, LLMs face the most severe \emph{evaluation bottleneck}: the post-training ecosystem generates dozens of quantized and fine-tuned variants per base model, each requiring expensive benchmark evaluation.
Second, the backbone-plus-linear-head decomposition is architecturally exact---the \texttt{lm\_head} is a single linear projection, and under standard LoRA fine-tuning it remains frozen ($H_T = H_P$), isolating performance changes entirely in the backbone geometry.
Third, LLM activations exhibit well-documented \emph{outlier channels} and \emph{scale collapse} phenomena during quantization~\cite{dettmers2022gpt3, xiao2023smoothquant}, which our decomposition into $\Delta\rho$ and $\Omega_W$ can precisely characterize.
Fourth, our framework naturally unifies both point-wise classification (e.g., MMLU logit scoring) and sequence-level autoregressive generation (e.g., language modeling loss) under a single bound, since teacher-forcing allows all token-level features to be extracted in a single forward pass.

\paragraph{Contributions.}
Our main contributions are as follows:
\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{A Unified Geometric Framework for Risk Bounding.} We establish a rigorous theoretical bridge linking the internal representational geometry of a proxy model to the functional risk of a target model. For any orthogonal alignment $W$, we derive an exact decomposition of feature discrepancy into scale mismatch ($\Delta\rho$) and shape mismatch ($1 - \Omega_W$), providing a closed-form, computable bound on $|\mathcal{R}_T - \mathcal{R}_P|$. Our framework naturally unifies point-wise classification and sequence-level autoregressive generation under the same geometric constraints.

    \item \textbf{The $\delta$--$\gamma$ Trade-off and Canonical Alignments.} We identify a fundamental trade-off governed by the choice of $W$: the Procrustes solution $W^*$ maximizes $\Omega_W$ to the nuclear-norm similarity $\Omega$, yielding the tightest feature bound (provably tighter than Frobenius-based metrics such as CKA), while the identity alignment $W = I$ yields the trace similarity $\Omega_{\mathrm{tr}}$ and preserves head coherence. We show that $W = I$ is the physically motivated choice in coordinate-preserving regimes (quantization, LoRA), providing a fully decoupled decomposition of scale, shape, and head effects.

    \item \textbf{Principled Diagnostics for Quantization and Fine-Tuning.} We operationalize our theory across two high-value LLM lifecycle stages. In post-training quantization (GGUF, GPTQ, AWQ, BitsAndBytes), the identity alignment avoids overfitting to quantization noise and yields a pure geometric distortion measure. In fine-tuning (SFT, LoRA), geometric drift serves as a label-free monitor for catastrophic forgetting, with the alignment choice adapted to the fine-tuning protocol: $W = I$ for frozen-head LoRA, $W^*$ for full-parameter SFT.

    \item \textbf{Strong Empirical Correlation.} Extensive experiments across modern LLMs (Llama, Qwen, Mistral, DeepSeek) demonstrate that our geometric bound serves as a highly predictive surrogate for actual performance degradation, achieving Spearman's $\rho > 0.9$ across quantization formats and fine-tuning protocols, establishing linear geometric alignment as a robust, compute-efficient proxy for model evaluation.

    \item \textbf{Compute-Efficient Performance Estimation via Control Variates.} By leveraging the high geometric alignment ($\Omega_W \approx 1$) between target and proxy models, the pointwise Lipschitz bounds underlying our framework guarantee small per-sample loss differences, which in turn yield low variance via Popoviciu's inequality. We demonstrate that full-benchmark performance (e.g., 14{,}000 MMLU tasks) can be accurately forecast from a 500-sample calibration set, reducing evaluation compute by over 95\% with strict statistical guarantees.
\end{enumerate}

\section{Related Work}

\paragraph{Representational Similarity Metrics.}
Comparing the internal representations of neural networks is a long-standing problem.
SVCCA~\cite{raghu2017svcca} projects activations onto their top singular vectors before computing canonical correlations, while CKA~\cite{kornblith2019similarity} measures alignment through kernel-based Hilbert--Schmidt independence.
Both metrics rely on Frobenius-norm statistics, which conflate rotational misalignment with scale differences and provide no formal connection to downstream task performance: two models can exhibit high CKA yet diverge significantly in functional risk, or vice versa.
The \emph{Linear Representation Hypothesis}~\cite{moschella2022relative, park2023linear} offers a structural explanation---models trained on similar data converge to linearly alignable manifolds---but existing work uses this insight only for post-hoc interpretability.
Our framework makes this hypothesis \emph{constructive}: for any orthogonal alignment $W$, we derive an exact decomposition that separates scale mismatch ($\Delta\rho$) from shape mismatch ($1-\Omega_W$) and directly bounds the risk gap $|\mathcal{R}_T - \mathcal{R}_P|$. We further show that the Procrustes alignment $W^*$ maximizes $\Omega_W$ to the nuclear norm---provably tighter than Frobenius-based metrics---while the identity alignment yields a trace similarity that cleanly decouples geometric distortion from head effects.

\paragraph{Post-Training Quantization and Its Evaluation.}
Post-training quantization (PTQ) has become the dominant approach for deploying LLMs under memory constraints.
GPTQ~\cite{frantar2022gptq} uses approximate second-order information to minimize layer-wise reconstruction error, AWQ~\cite{lin2024awq} preserves salient weight channels identified by activation magnitudes, and round-to-nearest methods in GGUF and BitsAndBytes~\cite{dettmers2022gpt3} offer simpler but less controlled compression.
A common bottleneck across all these methods is \emph{quality assessment}: determining how much a quantized variant has degraded requires running full perplexity or accuracy evaluations on large benchmarks, which can be as expensive as the quantization process itself.
Existing diagnostics are limited to indirect proxies such as layer-wise reconstruction loss or weight quantization error, neither of which accounts for the nonlinear accumulation of errors through the full network.
PRISM addresses this gap by providing a \emph{generation-free} geometric diagnostic: our bound decomposes quantization-induced degradation into scale collapse ($\Delta\rho$, capturing activation clipping from outlier channels~\cite{xiao2023smoothquant}) and structural distortion ($1-\Omega_{\mathrm{tr}}$), both computable from a single forward pass on a small calibration set without any label or generation.

\paragraph{Catastrophic Forgetting in Continual and Parameter-Efficient Fine-Tuning.}
When LLMs are sequentially fine-tuned---whether through full SFT or parameter-efficient methods such as LoRA~\cite{hu2022lora}---they risk catastrophic forgetting of pre-training knowledge.
Classical remedies operate in weight space: EWC~\cite{kirkpatrick2017overcoming} penalizes changes to important parameters via a diagonal Fisher approximation, while replay-based methods retain a buffer of old data.
These weight-space constraints are rigid: they penalize any parameter movement equally regardless of whether the underlying feature geometry is preserved.
Our framework offers a complementary, \emph{representation-space} perspective.
We monitor forgetting through the geometric drift of the feature manifold ($\Omega_W$, $\Delta\rho$) relative to the frozen base model, which naturally permits global rotations and rescalings that do not affect downstream risk.
Notably, under standard LoRA fine-tuning where the prediction head is frozen ($H_T = H_P$), our bound isolates forgetting entirely in the backbone geometry, providing a label-free diagnostic that decouples manifold distortion from head-specific effects.

\paragraph{Performance Forecasting for Foundation Models.}
The prohibitive cost of evaluating LLM variants has motivated several forecasting approaches.
Scaling laws~\cite{kaplan2020scaling, hoffmann2022training} predict aggregate loss as a power-law function of compute and data, but cannot capture variant-specific degradation (e.g., the effect of a particular quantization format on a specific benchmark).
Weak-to-strong generalization~\cite{burns2023weak} explores using smaller models to supervise larger ones, but targets label generation rather than risk estimation.
PRISM introduces a distinct paradigm: \emph{Geometric Control Variates}.
Rather than extrapolating from scaling trends, we exploit the fact that a geometrically aligned proxy ($\Omega_W \approx 1$) produces per-sample losses that are highly correlated with those of the target.
Our unified bound guarantees that the variance of the loss difference $\ell_T(x) - \ell_P(x)$ is controlled by the geometric terms, enabling accurate estimation of full-benchmark performance from a small calibration set with strict statistical guarantees via Popoviciu's inequality.

\section{A Geometric Framework for Proxy Performance Estimation}
\label{sec:geometric_foundation}

We derive a rigorous upper bound on the risk discrepancy $|\mathcal{R}_T - \mathcal{R}_P|$ between a target model $T$ and a proxy model $P$, grounded in their representational geometry. We first formalize the backbone-plus-linear-head architecture, then state our main theorem linking geometric alignment to functional risk, and finally extend the bound to autoregressive generation.

\subsection{Problem Setup}
\label{subsec:problem_setup}

\paragraph{The Backbone-Plus-Linear-Head Architecture.}
We consider a \emph{Target Model} $T$ (e.g., full-precision) and a \emph{Proxy Model} $P$ (e.g., quantized or fine-tuned variant), both with hidden dimension $d$. Each model $M \in \{T, P\}$ decomposes as:
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:model_decomp}
f_M(x) = \mathrm{softmax}\!\big(\phi_M(x) \cdot H_M\big),
\end{equation}
where $\phi_M: \mathcal{X} \to \mathbb{R}^{d}$ is the \emph{feature extractor} (Transformer backbone), producing a feature vector $z_M = \phi_M(x)$, and $H_M \in \mathbb{R}^{d \times V}$ is the \emph{prediction head} (\texttt{lm\_head}), projecting features onto the vocabulary of size $V$. We denote the columns of $H_M$ (token embeddings) as $h_{M,1}, \ldots, h_{M,V} \in \mathbb{R}^d$.

\paragraph{Risk via Cross-Entropy.}
For a data distribution $\mathcal{D}$ over input-label pairs $(x, y)$, the \emph{risk} of model $M$ is:
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:ce_def}
\mathcal{R}_M = \mathbb{E}_{(x,y) \sim \mathcal{D}}\!\Big[\ell\!\big(\phi_M(x) \cdot H_M,\; y\big)\Big], \quad \text{where} \quad \ell(v, y) = -v_y + \log\!\sum_{j=1}^{V} e^{v_j}
\end{equation}
is the cross-entropy loss, with logits $v = \phi_M(x) \cdot H_M \in \mathbb{R}^V$ and ground-truth index $y$.

\paragraph{The Linear Alignment Hypothesis.}
Following the Linear Representation Hypothesis~\cite{moschella2022relative, park2023linear}, we posit that models within the same lineage learn feature manifolds related by a near-isometric linear transformation: there exists an orthogonal $W \in \mathcal{O}(d) = \{W \in \mathbb{R}^{d \times d} \mid W^\top W = I_d\}$ such that
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:alignment_hypothesis}
\phi_T(x) \approx \phi_P(x)\, W, \quad \forall x \in \mathcal{X}.
\end{equation}

\paragraph{Notation.}
Given $n$ samples, we collect features into $Z_T, Z_P \in \mathbb{R}^{n \times d}$. We define the RMS feature scale $\rho_M = \frac{1}{\sqrt{n}}\|Z_M\|_F$ and the empirical second-moment matrix $\Sigma_P = \frac{1}{n} Z_P^\top Z_P$.


\subsection{The Unified Risk Bound}
\label{subsec:unified_bound}

We derive a computable upper bound on $|\mathcal{R}_T - \mathcal{R}_P|$ in three steps; full proofs are in Appendix~\ref{app:detailed_proofs}.

\paragraph{Step 1: Decomposing the risk gap.}
Introducing an intermediate \emph{hybrid} risk $\mathcal{R}_{P \to T} = \mathbb{E}[\ell(\phi_P(x) W \cdot H_T, y)]$ that evaluates the target's head on the proxy's aligned features, the triangle inequality gives (Appendix~\ref{app:risk_decomp}):
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:risk_decomp}
|\mathcal{R}_T - \mathcal{R}_P| \;\le\; \underbrace{\big|\mathcal{R}_T - \mathcal{R}_{P \to T}\big|}_{\text{Feature alignment error } \delta} \;+\; \underbrace{\big|\mathcal{R}_{P \to T} - \mathcal{R}_P\big|}_{\text{Head discrepancy } \gamma}.
\end{equation}

\paragraph{Step 2: Bounding the feature alignment error $\delta$.}
The cross-entropy loss is Lipschitz in $z$ with constant $K_{\mathrm{feat}} = \max_{j,k} \|h_{T,j} - h_{T,k}\|_2$ (the maximum pairwise distance between token embeddings in $H_T$; see Appendix~\ref{app:kfeat}). Combined with Jensen's inequality, for any orthogonal alignment $W \in \mathcal{O}(d)$:
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:delta_lipschitz}
\delta(W) \;\le\; K_{\mathrm{feat}} \sqrt{\frac{1}{n}\|Z_T - Z_P W\|_F^2}.
\end{equation}
Expanding the squared Frobenius norm and using the orthogonality of $W$ (i.e., $\|Z_P W\|_F = \|Z_P\|_F$) yields an exact decomposition of the alignment residual into scale and shape components:

\begin{proposition}[Exact Geometric Decomposition]
\label{prop:exact_decomposition}
For any $W \in \mathcal{O}(d)$:
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:exact_equality}
\frac{1}{n} \| Z_T - Z_P W \|_F^2
\;=\;
\underbrace{(\rho_T - \rho_P)^2}_{\text{\rm Scale Mismatch } (\Delta\rho)^2}
\;+\;
\underbrace{2 \rho_T \rho_P \big( 1 - \Omega_W \big)}_{\text{\rm Shape Mismatch}},
\end{equation}
where $\Omega_W(Z_T, Z_P)$ is the \emph{alignment-dependent similarity}:
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:omega_W_def}
\Omega_W(Z_T, Z_P) \;:=\; \frac{\operatorname{Tr}(Z_T^\top Z_P\, W)}{\|Z_T\|_F \, \|Z_P\|_F}.
\end{equation}
\end{proposition}

\noindent This is an exact identity for every orthogonal $W$: feature discrepancy decomposes into a scale gap ($\Delta\rho$) and a shape gap governed by $\Omega_W$.
Substituting into \eqref{eq:delta_lipschitz}:
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:delta_main}
\delta(W) \;\le\; K_{\mathrm{feat}} \sqrt{(\rho_T - \rho_P)^2 + 2\rho_T \rho_P(1 - \Omega_W)}.
\end{equation}

\paragraph{Step 3: Bounding the head discrepancy $\gamma$.}
The cross-entropy loss is Lipschitz with respect to logits with constant $K_{\mathrm{pred}} \le \sqrt{2}$. Defining the head misalignment $\Delta H_W = W H_T - H_P$ and projecting onto the empirical second-moment matrix (Appendix~\ref{app:head_bound}):
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:gamma_main}
\gamma(W) \;\le\; K_{\mathrm{pred}} \cdot \|\Sigma_P^{1/2}(W H_T - H_P)\|_F.
\end{equation}
The second-moment weighting $\Sigma_P^{1/2}$ ensures that only misalignment within the \emph{active subspace} of the data is penalized; disagreement in the null space of $\Sigma_P$ has zero cost.

\paragraph{Main Result.}
Combining Eqs.~(\ref{eq:risk_decomp}), (\ref{eq:delta_main}), and (\ref{eq:gamma_main}):

\begin{theorem}[Unified Risk Bound]
\label{thm:unified_bound}
For any orthogonal alignment $W \in \mathcal{O}(d)$:
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:unified_bound_eq}
\begin{aligned}
|\mathcal{R}_T - \mathcal{R}_P| \;\;\le\;\; & \underbrace{K_{\mathrm{feat}} \sqrt{ (\rho_T - \rho_P)^2 + 2\rho_T \rho_P \big( 1 - \Omega_W(Z_T, Z_P) \big) }}_{\text{\rm Geometric Alignment Error } \delta(W)} \\
& + \underbrace{K_{\mathrm{pred}} \cdot \| \Sigma_P^{1/2} (W H_T - H_P) \|_F}_{\text{\rm Second-Moment-Weighted Head Error } \gamma(W)}.
\end{aligned}
\end{equation}
\end{theorem}

\noindent
The bound holds for every orthogonal $W$ and decomposes the risk gap into three independently measurable quantities---scale mismatch ($\Delta\rho$), shape mismatch ($1 - \Omega_W$), and head misalignment---whose relative importance depends on $W$.
The tightest overall bound is $\inf_{W \in \mathcal{O}(d)}[\delta(W) + \gamma(W)]$, a non-convex problem on the Stiefel manifold without a closed-form solution in general (Appendix~\ref{app:joint_opt}).
Two canonical choices of $W$ are of particular importance:

\begin{remark}[Procrustes Optimality and the Nuclear Norm]
\label{rem:procrustes}
For the feature term $\delta(W)$ alone, the optimal alignment is the solution to the classical \emph{Orthogonal Procrustes problem}: $W^* = V U^\top$, obtained from the SVD $Z_T^\top Z_P = U \Sigma V^\top$. Under this choice:
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:omega_def}
\Omega_{W^*} \;=\; \frac{\|Z_T^\top Z_P\|_*}{\|Z_T\|_F\,\|Z_P\|_F} \;=:\; \Omega,
\end{equation}
where $\|\cdot\|_*$ denotes the nuclear norm. The Procrustes Similarity $\Omega$ is the maximum of $\Omega_W$ over all $W \in \mathcal{O}(d)$ and yields the tightest possible feature bound; it is provably tighter than Frobenius-based similarities such as CKA (Appendix~\ref{app:tightness}).
However, $W^*$ enters the head term as $\gamma(W^*) = K_{\mathrm{pred}}\|\Sigma_P^{1/2}(W^* H_T - H_P)\|_F$, which can be inflated when the rotation $W^*$ is applied to a nearly preserved head ($H_T \approx H_P$).
\end{remark}

\begin{remark}[The Identity Alignment and the Trace Similarity]
\label{rem:identity_alignment}
The identity alignment $W = I$ yields the \emph{trace similarity}:
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:omega_tr}
\Omega_I \;=\; \frac{\operatorname{Tr}(Z_T^\top Z_P)}{\|Z_T\|_F\,\|Z_P\|_F} \;=:\; \Omega_{\mathrm{tr}}.
\end{equation}
Since $\operatorname{Tr}(Z_T^\top Z_P) \le \|Z_T^\top Z_P\|_*$, we have $\Omega_{\mathrm{tr}} \le \Omega$ in general, so the feature bound under $W = I$ is no tighter than under $W^*$.
However, $W = I$ preserves head coherence: $\gamma(I) = K_{\mathrm{pred}}\|\Sigma_P^{1/2}(H_T - H_P)\|_F$, which vanishes when the prediction head is shared or preserved.
When the cross-moment matrix $Z_T^\top Z_P$ is symmetric positive semidefinite---as it approximately is for mild perturbations such as quantization---the trace and nuclear norm coincide ($\Omega_{\mathrm{tr}} = \Omega$), and both alignments yield the same feature bound (Appendix~\ref{app:trace_specialization}).

In the two primary application regimes of this paper---post-training quantization (where $H_T \approx H_P$ and $W^* \approx I$) and frozen-head fine-tuning ($H_T = H_P$, so $\gamma = 0$ for any $W$)---the identity alignment $W = I$ is the \emph{physically motivated} choice: it preserves the original coordinate basis and yields a fully decoupled decomposition of scale, shape, and head effects (Section~\ref{sec:applications}).
\end{remark}


\subsection{Geometric Interpretation}
\label{subsec:interpretation}

Theorem~\ref{thm:unified_bound} reveals three sufficient conditions for a proxy to reliably estimate target risk:

\paragraph{Scale Matching ($\rho_T \approx \rho_P$).}
Divergence in activation magnitude directly amplifies the geometric error term. In post-training quantization, aggressive bit-width reduction clips activation outliers~\cite{dettmers2022gpt3, xiao2023smoothquant}, shrinking $\rho_Q < \rho_F$. Our bound isolates this \emph{scale collapse} as a primary and independently measurable source of degradation.

\paragraph{Shape Alignment ($\Omega_W \to 1$).}
The shape mismatch term is governed by $\Omega_W$, which captures how well the relational structure of the feature manifold is preserved under alignment $W$. A drop in $\Omega_W$ signals that the relative arrangement of token representations has been corrupted---what we term \emph{structural distortion}. The Procrustes alignment $W^*$ maximizes $\Omega_W$ to the nuclear-norm similarity $\Omega$ (Remark~\ref{rem:procrustes}), yielding the tightest possible feature bound; Frobenius-based similarities yield provably looser residuals, and metrics such as CKA---which use a different normalization---are not directly substitutable (Appendix~\ref{app:tightness}). In coordinate-preserving regimes, the identity alignment yields the trace similarity $\Omega_{\mathrm{tr}}$ (Remark~\ref{rem:identity_alignment}), which measures structural distortion in the original basis.

\paragraph{Head Coherence ($\|\Sigma_P^{1/2}(W H_T - H_P)\|_F \to 0$).}
The head error quantifies how differently the two prediction heads interpret aligned features, weighted by the empirical second-moment $\Sigma_P$ so that only misalignment within the active subspace of the data contributes.
The choice of $W$ enters this term directly: the Procrustes solution $W^*$ can \emph{inflate} $\gamma$ by rotating a nearly preserved head ($H_T \approx H_P$), whereas $W = I$ yields $\gamma(I) = K_{\mathrm{pred}}\|\Sigma_P^{1/2}(H_T - H_P)\|_F$, which vanishes naturally when the heads are preserved.
Under standard LoRA fine-tuning~\cite{hu2022lora} where the \texttt{lm\_head} is frozen ($H_T = H_P$), this term vanishes for any $W$, isolating all performance change in the backbone geometry.
In post-training quantization, where the head is typically retained in FP16, the identity alignment avoids manufacturing an artificial head discrepancy from quantization noise (Section~\ref{subsec:quantization}).


\subsection{Extension to Autoregressive Generation}
\label{subsec:ar_extension}

The framework above treats each evaluation as a single feature-to-loss mapping. We now show that it extends naturally to sequence-level autoregressive generation, unifying both point-wise classification and language modeling under a single bound.

\paragraph{Autoregressive Risk.}
For a sequence $(c, y)$ drawn from $\mathcal{D}$, where $c$ denotes the context (prompt) and $y = (y_1, \ldots, y_{|y|})$ the target continuation, the autoregressive risk is:
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:ar_risk}
\mathcal{R}_M^{\mathrm{AR}} = \mathbb{E}_{(c, y) \sim \mathcal{D}} \left[ \frac{1}{|y|} \sum_{t=1}^{|y|} \ell\!\big(\phi_M(c, y_{<t}) \cdot H_M,\; y_t\big) \right],
\end{equation}
where $\phi_M(c, y_{<t}) \in \mathbb{R}^d$ is the feature vector at position $t$, encoding the context $c$ and all preceding target tokens $y_{<t}$.

\paragraph{From Tokens to Matrices.}
Under teacher forcing, all token-level features $\{\phi_M(c, y_{<t})\}_{t=1}^{|y|}$ are extracted in a single forward pass---each token contributes an independent feature-loss pair to the average. Collecting all $N = \sum_{(c,y)} |y|$ token-level features into a single matrix $Z_M^{\mathrm{AR}} \in \mathbb{R}^{N \times d}$, we apply Theorem~\ref{thm:unified_bound} directly:

\begin{corollary}[Autoregressive Risk Bound]
\label{cor:ar_bound}
Let $Z_T^{\mathrm{AR}}, Z_P^{\mathrm{AR}} \in \mathbb{R}^{N \times d}$ be the token-level feature matrices collected under teacher forcing. Then for any $W \in \mathcal{O}(d)$:
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:ar_bound}
|\mathcal{R}_T^{\mathrm{AR}} - \mathcal{R}_P^{\mathrm{AR}}| \;\le\; K_{\mathrm{feat}} \sqrt{(\rho_T^{\scriptscriptstyle\mathrm{AR}} - \rho_P^{\scriptscriptstyle\mathrm{AR}})^2 + 2\,\rho_T^{\scriptscriptstyle\mathrm{AR}}\,\rho_P^{\scriptscriptstyle\mathrm{AR}}\!\big(1 - \Omega_W^{\mathrm{AR}}\big)} + K_{\mathrm{pred}} \,\big\|\Sigma_P^{\mathrm{AR}\,1/2}(W H_T - H_P)\big\|_F,
\end{equation}
where $\rho^{\mathrm{AR}}$, $\Omega_W^{\mathrm{AR}}$, and $\Sigma_P^{\mathrm{AR}}$ are computed over the token-level matrices $Z^{\mathrm{AR}}$.
\end{corollary}

\section{Instantiating the Framework}
\label{sec:applications}

The Unified Risk Bound (Theorem~\ref{thm:unified_bound}) provides a general mechanism to estimate functional divergence between any two models. As discussed in Remarks~\ref{rem:procrustes}--\ref{rem:identity_alignment}, the choice of orthogonal alignment $W$ controls a fundamental trade-off between feature-bound tightness ($\delta$) and head-bound fidelity ($\gamma$). This trade-off, combined with the physical nature of each application, gives rise to two distinct regimes:
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{The Coordinate-Preserving Regime ($W = I$):} When the proxy and target share a nearly identical coordinate basis---as in post-training quantization and frozen-head fine-tuning (LoRA)---the feature space undergoes element-wise perturbation without global rotation. In this regime, the identity alignment $W = I$ is the physically motivated choice: it avoids manufacturing spurious head discrepancy from numerical noise, and yields a fully decoupled decomposition of scale ($\Delta\rho$), shape ($1 - \Omega_{\mathrm{tr}}$), and head effects. The trace similarity $\Omega_{\mathrm{tr}}$ (Eq.~\ref{eq:omega_tr}) measures structural distortion directly in the original coordinate frame.
    \item \textbf{The Rotational Regime ($W^* \in \mathcal{O}(d), W^* \not\approx I$):} When training dynamics induce global basis rotations---as in full-parameter fine-tuning---the Procrustes alignment $W^*$ is required to absorb arbitrary rotations. Here $\Omega$ (nuclear-norm similarity) measures the residual geometric drift that rotations alone cannot explain.
\end{itemize}
We instantiate these two regimes below for their most practically relevant scenarios, and additionally develop a control variate method for compute-efficient evaluation.

\subsection{Quantization Quality Estimation}
\label{subsec:quantization}

We designate the full-precision model as Target ($T = F$) and the quantized variant as Proxy ($P = Q$). Three conditions specific to post-training quantization motivate the identity alignment $W = I$:
\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{Coordinate-Basis Preservation:} Standard PTQ introduces element-wise numerical noise (rounding, clipping) without global rotation of the feature space.
    Unlike fine-tuning, where gradient updates can induce arbitrary basis changes, quantization perturbs each activation channel independently.
    The optimal Procrustes solution $W^*$ would absorb these small, non-symmetric perturbations by fitting a slight rotation---effectively \emph{overfitting to quantization noise}---while the identity alignment $W = I$ faithfully reflects the in-place distortion.

    \item \textbf{Head Stability ($H_Q \approx H_F$):} Modern protocols (GPTQ, AWQ) retain the prediction head in FP16. With $W = I$, the head term reduces to $\gamma(I) = K_{\mathrm{pred}}\|\Sigma_P^{1/2}(H_F - H_Q)\|_F \approx 0$, correctly reflecting the physical invariance. In contrast, using $W^*$ would yield $\gamma(W^*) = K_{\mathrm{pred}}\|\Sigma_P^{1/2}(W^* H_F - H_Q)\|_F$, artificially inflating the head error by rotating an otherwise preserved head.\footnote{Empirically, $B(I)$ is consistently 3--10$\times$ tighter than $B(W^*)$ across all quantization formats tested, precisely because the spurious head rotation dominates the bound when using $W^*$.}

    \item \textbf{Interpretability:} Setting $W = I$ produces a fully decoupled diagnostic: scale ($\Delta\rho$) captures activation magnitude shifts, shape ($1 - \Omega_{\mathrm{tr}}$) captures structural distortion in the original coordinate frame, and head effects vanish. No component is contaminated by a compensatory rotation.
\end{enumerate}
Under $W = I$, the feature alignment error uses the trace similarity $\Omega_{\mathrm{tr}}$ (Eq.~\ref{eq:omega_tr}), and the risk gap reduces to a pure geometric distortion measure:
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:quant_bound}
|\mathcal{R}_F - \mathcal{R}_Q| \;\;\lessapprox\;\; K_{\mathrm{feat}} \sqrt{ \underbrace{(\rho_F - \rho_Q)^2}_{\text{\rm Scale Collapse}} + \underbrace{2\rho_F \rho_Q \big(1 - \Omega_{\mathrm{tr}}(Z_F, Z_Q)\big)}_{\text{\rm Structural Distortion}} }.
\end{equation}
This establishes $\Omega_{\mathrm{tr}}$ and $\Delta\rho$ as generation-free diagnostics: \emph{scale collapse} ($\rho_Q < \rho_F$) captures activation clipping from aggressive bit-width reduction~\cite{dettmers2022gpt3, xiao2023smoothquant}, while a drop in $\Omega_{\mathrm{tr}}$ signals corruption of the relational geometry between tokens in the original basis. Practitioners can rank quantization configurations purely from feature statistics on a small calibration set.

\paragraph{Relation to the Nuclear Norm.}
When $Z_F^\top Z_Q$ is symmetric positive semidefinite---as it approximately is for mild quantization---the trace and nuclear norm coincide: $\operatorname{Tr}(Z_F^\top Z_Q) = \|Z_F^\top Z_Q\|_*$, and thus $\Omega_{\mathrm{tr}} = \Omega$. As quantization noise grows (lower bit-widths, aggressive clipping), the cross-moment matrix develops asymmetric perturbations, causing $\Omega_{\mathrm{tr}} < \Omega$.
This gap is a feature, not a limitation: $\Omega_{\mathrm{tr}}$ detects asymmetric distortions that the nuclear norm---by optimizing over rotations---would mask (Appendix~\ref{app:trace_specialization}).


\subsection{Geometric Monitoring of Catastrophic Forgetting}
\label{subsec:forgetting}

We define the frozen base model as Target ($T = \theta_0$) and the model at fine-tuning step $t$ as Proxy ($P = \theta_t$). The appropriate alignment choice depends on the fine-tuning protocol.

\paragraph{LoRA Fine-Tuning ($W = I$).}
Under standard LoRA fine-tuning~\cite{hu2022lora}, low-rank updates are applied only to attention and MLP projection matrices ($W_q, W_k, W_v, W_o, W_{\mathrm{up}}, W_{\mathrm{down}}$), while the \texttt{lm\_head} remains frozen ($H_t = H_0$). With $H_T = H_P$, the head discrepancy $\gamma$ vanishes for \emph{any} alignment $W$, so the choice of $W$ affects only the feature term.
We adopt $W = I$ for the same interpretability rationale as in quantization: LoRA's low-rank perturbations modify the feature manifold without inducing a coherent global rotation, and the identity alignment measures backbone drift directly in the original coordinate basis:
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:lora_bound}
\text{Forgetting}_{\mathrm{LoRA}}(t) \;\;\le\;\; K_{\mathrm{feat}} \sqrt{ (\rho_0 - \rho_t)^2 + 2\rho_0 \rho_t \big( 1 - \Omega_{\mathrm{tr}}(Z_0, Z_t) \big) }.
\end{equation}
This provides a label-free diagnostic that cleanly separates scale drift ($\Delta\rho$) from structural distortion ($1 - \Omega_{\mathrm{tr}}$), without any compensatory rotation masking the true geometric change.

\paragraph{Full-Parameter SFT ($W = W^*$).}
When all parameters---including the prediction head---are updated, training dynamics can induce substantial global basis rotations, placing the model in the rotational regime. Here the full Procrustes alignment $W^*$ is required to absorb arbitrary rotations, and the nuclear-norm similarity $\Omega$ measures the residual geometric drift that rotations alone cannot explain:
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:forgetting_bound}
\begin{aligned}
\text{Forgetting}_{\mathrm{SFT}}(t) \;\;\le\;\; & \underbrace{K_{\mathrm{feat}} \sqrt{ (\rho_0 - \rho_t)^2 + 2\rho_0 \rho_t \big( 1 - \Omega(Z_0, Z_t) \big) }}_{\text{\rm Backbone Geometric Drift}} \\
& + \underbrace{K_{\mathrm{pred}} \cdot \| \Sigma_t^{1/2} (W^* H_0 - H_t) \|_F}_{\text{\rm Head Divergence}}.
\end{aligned}
\end{equation}
The head divergence term is now non-negligible and captures how the updated prediction head diverges from the base model's head, projected through the Procrustes alignment.


\subsection{Compute-Efficient Estimation via Geometric Control Variates}
\label{subsec:control_variates}

The high geometric alignment ($\Omega_{\mathrm{tr}} \approx 1$ or $\Omega \approx 1$, depending on the regime) between target and proxy implies that the per-sample loss difference $\Delta\ell(x) = \ell_T(x) - \ell_P(x)$ has low variance. To make this precise, note that the Lipschitz analysis underlying Theorem~\ref{thm:unified_bound} provides a \emph{pointwise} bound on each sample (before taking expectations). For a chosen alignment $W$ (either $I$ or $W^*$):
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:pointwise_bound}
|\Delta\ell(x)| \;\le\; K_{\mathrm{feat}} \|\phi_T(x) - \phi_P(x) W\|_2 \;+\; K_{\mathrm{pred}} \|\phi_P(x) \cdot \Delta H_W\|_2 \;\le\; B,
\end{equation}
where $\Delta H_W = W H_T - H_P$ and $B = K_{\mathrm{feat}} \max_{x \in \mathcal{D}_{\mathrm{cal}}} \|\phi_T(x) - \phi_P(x) W\|_2 + K_{\mathrm{pred}} \max_{x \in \mathcal{D}_{\mathrm{cal}}} \|\phi_P(x) \cdot \Delta H_W\|_2$ is the worst-case per-sample bound over the calibration set. Since $\Delta\ell(x) \in [-B, B]$, Popoviciu's inequality on bounded random variables gives:
\begin{equation}
\mathrm{Var}(\Delta\ell) \;\le\; B^2.
\end{equation}
When $\Omega_W \approx 1$ and $\Delta\rho \approx 0$, the per-sample residuals $\|\phi_T(x) - \phi_P(x) W\|_2$ are uniformly small, yielding a tight $B$ and thus low variance. This justifies treating the risk difference as a stable bias rather than random noise, and motivates the following estimator.

\paragraph{The Control Variate Estimator.}
We estimate the target's risk on a large test set $\mathcal{D}_{\mathrm{test}}$ using a small calibration set $\mathcal{D}_{\mathrm{cal}}$:
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:cv_estimator}
\hat{\mathcal{R}}_T(\mathcal{D}_{\mathrm{test}}) = \mathcal{R}_P(\mathcal{D}_{\mathrm{test}}) + \underbrace{\big(\mathcal{R}_T(\mathcal{D}_{\mathrm{cal}}) - \mathcal{R}_P(\mathcal{D}_{\mathrm{cal}})\big)}_{\text{Calibrated Bias } \hat{\Delta}}.
\end{equation}
The proxy risk $\mathcal{R}_P(\mathcal{D}_{\mathrm{test}})$ serves as a low-variance baseline (cheap to compute), and $\hat{\Delta}$ corrects for the systematic offset using only $|\mathcal{D}_{\mathrm{cal}}|$ evaluations of the expensive target. The geometric bound guarantees that $\hat{\Delta}$ is estimated with narrow confidence intervals when $\Omega_W$ is high.


\subsection{Other Applications}
\label{subsec:other_applications}

The framework extends naturally beyond the two regimes above. We briefly outline three additional applications that follow directly from Theorem~\ref{thm:unified_bound}:
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Out-of-Distribution (OOD) Detection:} For in-distribution data, the alignment $\phi_T(x) \approx \phi_P(x) W^*$ holds by construction. When an input $x'$ falls outside the training distribution, this linear relationship breaks down, and the per-sample residual $\|\phi_T(x') - \phi_P(x') W^*\|_2$ spikes. This residual serves as a theoretically grounded anomaly score without requiring the target model's logits.
    \item \textbf{Cross-Scale Coefficient Transfer:} When a small proxy ($P$) and a large target ($T$) maintain high $\Omega_W$ along a hyperparameter search path (e.g., merging coefficient $\alpha$), our bound guarantees that the optimal $\alpha$ found on the proxy transfers to the target with bounded regret. This enables using small models to tune hyperparameters for large models.
    \item \textbf{Label-Free Production Monitoring:} Since $\Omega_W$ and $\Delta\rho$ depend only on feature matrices---not ground-truth labels---they can serve as online monitors for deployed models, detecting semantic degradation or distribution shifts in real time.
\end{itemize}
We leave detailed empirical validation of these applications to future work.

\section{Conclusion and Future Work}
\label{sec:conclusion}

We presented PRISM, a unified geometric framework that establishes a rigorous, computable link between representational alignment and functional risk. For any orthogonal alignment $W$, the bound decomposes the risk gap $|\mathcal{R}_T - \mathcal{R}_P|$ into scale collapse ($\Delta\rho$), structural distortion ($1 - \Omega_W$), and head divergence as independently measurable diagnostic quantities.
A fundamental $\delta$--$\gamma$ trade-off governs the choice of $W$: the Procrustes solution $W^*$ maximizes $\Omega_W$ to the nuclear-norm similarity $\Omega$ (the tightest feature bound), while the identity alignment $W = I$ yields the trace similarity $\Omega_{\mathrm{tr}}$ and preserves head coherence.

We instantiated this framework for two critical LLM lifecycle stages---post-training quantization and frozen-head fine-tuning (LoRA), where $W = I$ yields a fully decoupled diagnostic, and full-parameter SFT, where the Procrustes alignment absorbs global rotations---and demonstrated that geometric control variates enable accurate risk forecasting from small calibration sets with strict statistical guarantees.

\paragraph{Limitations and Future Work.}
Several promising directions remain:
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Dynamic Trajectory Alignment:} Our current formulation validates static (prefill) feature manifolds. Extending to multi-step generation trajectories would enable proxy estimation for long Chain-of-Thought reasoning and agentic workflows.
    \item \textbf{Cross-Modal Generalization:} The Linear Representation Hypothesis extends beyond text. Investigating PRISM's applicability to vision transformers and cross-modal models (e.g., CLIP) remains a natural next step.
\end{itemize}

\section{Proof of the Unified Risk Bound (Theorem~\ref{thm:unified_bound})}
\label{app:detailed_proofs}

We provide the complete proof of Theorem~\ref{thm:unified_bound}, deriving the bound on $|\mathcal{R}_T - \mathcal{R}_P|$ for any orthogonal alignment $W \in \mathcal{O}(d)$. The proof proceeds through five self-contained steps: risk decomposition via triangle inequality, Lipschitz analysis of cross-entropy with respect to features, exact geometric decomposition of the alignment residual, second-moment-adjusted head bound, and final assembly. We additionally derive the Procrustes optimality result (Remark~\ref{rem:procrustes}) as a corollary of Step~3.

\subsection{Step 1: Risk Decomposition via Triangle Inequality}
\label{app:risk_decomp}

\paragraph{Setup.}
Recall that the risk of model $M$ is $\mathcal{R}_M = \mathbb{E}_{(x,y)}[\ell(\phi_M(x) \cdot H_M, y)]$, where $\ell$ is the cross-entropy loss (Eq.~\ref{eq:ce_def}). For any orthogonal alignment $W \in \mathcal{O}(d)$ that maps proxy features toward target features (i.e., $\phi_T(x) \approx \phi_P(x) W$), we introduce an intermediate \emph{hybrid risk}:
\begin{equation}
\mathcal{R}_{P \to T} := \mathbb{E}_{(x,y)}\!\Big[\ell\!\big(\phi_P(x)\, W \cdot H_T,\; y\big)\Big].
\end{equation}
This hybrid term evaluates the \emph{target's head} $H_T$ on the \emph{proxy's aligned features} $\phi_P(x) W$, serving as a bridge between $\mathcal{R}_T$ and $\mathcal{R}_P$.

\paragraph{Decomposition.}
By the triangle inequality on absolute values:
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:app_triangle}
|\mathcal{R}_T - \mathcal{R}_P| \;\le\; \underbrace{\big|\mathcal{R}_T - \mathcal{R}_{P \to T}\big|}_{\text{Feature Alignment Error } \delta} \;+\; \underbrace{\big|\mathcal{R}_{P \to T} - \mathcal{R}_P\big|}_{\text{Head Discrepancy } \gamma}.
\end{equation}
The first term $\delta$ measures how much the \emph{features} differ (through the same head $H_T$); the second term $\gamma$ measures how much the \emph{heads} differ (on the same aligned features). We bound each term separately in the following subsections.


\subsection{Step 2: Bounding the Feature Alignment Error $\delta$}
\label{app:kfeat}

\paragraph{Lipschitz Property of Cross-Entropy with Respect to Features.}
We bound $\delta$ by showing that the cross-entropy loss is Lipschitz continuous in the feature vector $z$. Define the composition $g_T(z, y) := \ell(z \cdot H_T, y)$. Our goal is to find the tightest constant $K_{\mathrm{feat}}$ such that:
\begin{equation}
|g_T(z_1, y) - g_T(z_2, y)| \le K_{\mathrm{feat}} \|z_1 - z_2\|_2, \quad \forall z_1, z_2, y.
\end{equation}

\paragraph{Computing the Gradient.}
The logit vector is $v = z \cdot H_T \in \mathbb{R}^V$, and the cross-entropy loss is $\ell(v, y) = -v_y + \log \sum_{j=1}^{V} e^{v_j}$. The gradient with respect to the logits is:
\begin{equation}
\frac{\partial \ell}{\partial v} = \hat{p} - e_y,
\end{equation}
where $\hat{p} = \mathrm{softmax}(v) \in \mathbb{R}^V$ and $e_y$ is the one-hot vector at index $y$. Applying the chain rule to obtain the gradient with respect to features:
\begin{equation}
\nabla_z \ell = H_T (\hat{p} - e_y) = \sum_{j=1}^{V} (\hat{p}_j - \delta_{jy})\, h_{T,j},
\end{equation}
where $h_{T,j}$ denotes the $j$-th column of $H_T$ (the embedding vector for token $j$).

\paragraph{Simplex Polarization.}
We now derive a tight bound on $\|\nabla_z \ell\|_2$ by exploiting the probability simplex constraint $\sum_j \hat{p}_j = 1$. Expanding the gradient for true class $y$:
\begin{equation}
\nabla_z \ell = (\hat{p}_y - 1)\, h_{T,y} + \sum_{j \neq y} \hat{p}_j\, h_{T,j}.
\end{equation}
Since $1 - \hat{p}_y = \sum_{j \neq y} \hat{p}_j$, we can rewrite:
\begin{equation}
\nabla_z \ell = -\Big(\sum_{j \neq y} \hat{p}_j\Big) h_{T,y} + \sum_{j \neq y} \hat{p}_j\, h_{T,j} = \sum_{j \neq y} \hat{p}_j\, (h_{T,j} - h_{T,y}).
\end{equation}
This reveals that the gradient is a \emph{convex combination} of the pairwise differences between incorrect-class embeddings and the correct-class embedding. Taking the norm:
\begin{equation}
\|\nabla_z \ell\|_2 \le \sum_{j \neq y} \hat{p}_j \|h_{T,j} - h_{T,y}\|_2 \le \max_{j \neq y} \|h_{T,j} - h_{T,y}\|_2 \cdot \underbrace{\sum_{j \neq y} \hat{p}_j}_{\le\, 1}.
\end{equation}
Taking the maximum over all possible true classes $y$:
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:kfeat_tight}
K_{\mathrm{feat}} = \max_{j,k} \|h_{T,j} - h_{T,k}\|_2.
\end{equation}

\paragraph{Remark.} This bound depends on the \emph{relative distances} between token embeddings, not their absolute magnitudes. A uniform shift $H_T \to H_T + c\mathbf{1}^\top$ does not change $K_{\mathrm{feat}}$. A naive Cauchy--Schwarz bound gives $K_{\mathrm{feat}}^{\mathrm{naive}} = \sqrt{2}\|H_T\|_2$, which is substantially looser.

\paragraph{Applying the Lipschitz Bound.}
Using the Lipschitz property with $z_1 = \phi_T(x)$ and $z_2 = \phi_P(x) W$ for any orthogonal alignment $W \in \mathcal{O}(d)$:
\begin{equation}
\begin{aligned}
\delta(W) &= \big|\mathcal{R}_T - \mathcal{R}_{P \to T}\big| = \Big|\mathbb{E}\big[g_T(\phi_T(x), y) - g_T(\phi_P(x) W, y)\big]\Big| \\
&\le \mathbb{E}\Big[|g_T(\phi_T(x), y) - g_T(\phi_P(x) W, y)|\Big] \\
&\le K_{\mathrm{feat}} \cdot \mathbb{E}_x\!\big[\|\phi_T(x) - \phi_P(x) W\|_2\big].
\end{aligned}
\end{equation}

Applying Jensen's inequality ($\mathbb{E}[\|X\|] \le \sqrt{\mathbb{E}[\|X\|^2]}$):
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:delta_bound}
\delta(W) \le K_{\mathrm{feat}} \cdot \sqrt{\mathbb{E}_x\!\big[\|\phi_T(x) - \phi_P(x) W\|_2^2\big]} = K_{\mathrm{feat}} \cdot \sqrt{\frac{1}{n}\|Z_T - Z_P W\|_F^2}.
\end{equation}



\subsection{Step 3: Exact Geometric Decomposition}
\label{app:procrustes}

We prove Proposition~\ref{prop:exact_decomposition}: the alignment residual decomposes exactly into scale mismatch and shape mismatch for any orthogonal alignment $W$.

\paragraph{Expansion.} Expanding the squared Frobenius norm for any $W \in \mathcal{O}(d)$:
\begin{equation}
\begin{aligned}
\|Z_T - Z_P W\|_F^2 &= \operatorname{Tr}\!\big[(Z_T - Z_P W)^\top (Z_T - Z_P W)\big] \\
&= \|Z_T\|_F^2 + \|Z_P W\|_F^2 - 2\operatorname{Tr}(Z_T^\top Z_P W).
\end{aligned}
\end{equation}
Since $W$ is orthogonal, it preserves the Frobenius norm: $\|Z_P W\|_F^2 = \operatorname{Tr}(W^\top Z_P^\top Z_P W) = \operatorname{Tr}(Z_P^\top Z_P) = \|Z_P\|_F^2$, where we used the cyclic property of trace and $W^\top W = I_d$. Therefore:
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:procrustes_expand}
\|Z_T - Z_P W\|_F^2 = \|Z_T\|_F^2 + \|Z_P\|_F^2 - 2\operatorname{Tr}(Z_T^\top Z_P W).
\end{equation}

\paragraph{Scale--Shape Decomposition.}
Dividing by $n$ and recalling $\rho_M = \frac{1}{\sqrt{n}}\|Z_M\|_F$ (so $\frac{1}{n}\|Z_M\|_F^2 = \rho_M^2$) and $\Omega_W = \operatorname{Tr}(Z_T^\top Z_P W)/(\|Z_T\|_F\|Z_P\|_F)$ (Eq.~\ref{eq:omega_W_def}):
\begin{equation}
\frac{1}{n}\operatorname{Tr}(Z_T^\top Z_P W) = \Omega_W \cdot \rho_T \rho_P.
\end{equation}
Therefore:
\begin{equation}
\frac{1}{n}\|Z_T - Z_P W\|_F^2 = \rho_T^2 + \rho_P^2 - 2\,\Omega_W\,\rho_T\rho_P.
\end{equation}
Adding and subtracting $2\rho_T\rho_P$ completes the square:
\begin{equation}
= (\rho_T^2 - 2\rho_T\rho_P + \rho_P^2) + 2\rho_T\rho_P - 2\,\Omega_W\,\rho_T\rho_P = \underbrace{(\rho_T - \rho_P)^2}_{\text{Scale Mismatch}} + \underbrace{2\rho_T\rho_P(1 - \Omega_W)}_{\text{Shape Mismatch}}.
\end{equation}
This completes the proof of Proposition~\ref{prop:exact_decomposition}. \qed

\paragraph{Assembling the Feature Bound.}
Combining Eq.~(\ref{eq:delta_bound}) with Proposition~\ref{prop:exact_decomposition}:
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:delta_final}
\delta(W) \le K_{\mathrm{feat}} \sqrt{(\rho_T - \rho_P)^2 + 2\rho_T\rho_P(1 - \Omega_W)}.
\end{equation}

\paragraph{Procrustes Optimality (Remark~\ref{rem:procrustes}).}
The Procrustes alignment $W^*$ maximizes $\Omega_W$ over $\mathcal{O}(d)$, yielding the tightest feature bound. Since the first two terms in Eq.~(\ref{eq:procrustes_expand}) are independent of $W$, maximizing $\Omega_W$ is equivalent to:
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:procrustes_max}
W^* = \arg\max_{W \in \mathcal{O}(d)} \operatorname{Tr}(Z_T^\top Z_P W).
\end{equation}
Let $Z_T^\top Z_P = U \Sigma V^\top$ be the SVD with $\Sigma = \mathrm{diag}(\sigma_1, \ldots, \sigma_d)$, $\sigma_i \ge 0$. Setting $R = V^\top W U \in \mathcal{O}(d)$:
\begin{equation}
\operatorname{Tr}(Z_T^\top Z_P W) = \operatorname{Tr}(\Sigma R) = \sum_{i=1}^{d} \sigma_i R_{ii}.
\end{equation}
Since $|R_{ii}| \le 1$ for any orthogonal $R$ and $\sigma_i \ge 0$, this is maximized by $R = I_d$, giving:
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:W_optimal}
W^* = V U^\top, \qquad \max_{W \in \mathcal{O}(d)} \operatorname{Tr}(Z_T^\top Z_P W) = \sum_{i=1}^{d} \sigma_i = \|Z_T^\top Z_P\|_*,
\end{equation}
where $\|\cdot\|_*$ denotes the nuclear norm. Hence $\Omega_{W^*} = \|Z_T^\top Z_P\|_* / (\|Z_T\|_F\|Z_P\|_F) = \Omega$, recovering Eq.~(\ref{eq:omega_def}).



\subsection{Step 4: Bounding the Head Discrepancy $\gamma$}
\label{app:head_bound}

\paragraph{Lipschitz Property with Respect to Logits.}
The head discrepancy measures how much the predictions differ when two different ``effective heads'' ($W H_T$ vs.\ $H_P$) operate on the same proxy features. Let $z = \phi_P(x)$ denote the (unaligned) proxy feature. Then for any $W \in \mathcal{O}(d)$:
\begin{equation}
\gamma(W) = \big|\mathcal{R}_{P \to T} - \mathcal{R}_P\big| = \Big|\mathbb{E}\!\big[\ell(z \cdot W H_T, y) - \ell(z \cdot H_P, y)\big]\Big|.
\end{equation}
To bound this, we use the Lipschitz property of cross-entropy with respect to \emph{logits}. The gradient of $\ell$ with respect to $v$ is $\nabla_v \ell = \hat{p} - e_y$, with norm:
\begin{equation}
\|\nabla_v \ell\|_2 = \|\hat{p} - e_y\|_2.
\end{equation}
Since $\hat{p}$ lies on the probability simplex, $\|\hat{p}\|_2^2 \le \|\hat{p}\|_1^2 = 1$, and therefore $\|\hat{p} - e_y\|_2^2 = \|\hat{p}\|_2^2 - 2\hat{p}_y + 1 \le 2 - 2\hat{p}_y \le 2$. The supremum $\sqrt{2}$ is approached as the model's predicted distribution concentrates on an incorrect class ($\hat{p}_y \to 0$). Thus $K_{\mathrm{pred}} \le \sqrt{2}$.

\paragraph{Deriving the Second-Moment Projection.}
Define the head misalignment matrix $\Delta H_W = W H_T - H_P \in \mathbb{R}^{d \times V}$. The logit difference for a single sample is $z \cdot \Delta H_W = \phi_P(x)(W H_T - H_P)$, and:
\begin{equation}
\begin{aligned}
\gamma(W) &\le \mathbb{E}\!\big[|\ell(z \cdot W H_T, y) - \ell(z \cdot H_P, y)|\big] \\
&\le K_{\mathrm{pred}} \cdot \mathbb{E}_z\!\big[\|z \cdot \Delta H_W\|_2\big].
\end{aligned}
\end{equation}
Applying Jensen's inequality on the concave square root:
\begin{equation}
\big(\mathbb{E}[\|z \cdot \Delta H_W\|_2]\big)^2 \le \mathbb{E}\!\big[\|z \cdot \Delta H_W\|_2^2\big].
\end{equation}
Expanding the squared norm using the trace operator:
\begin{equation}
\begin{aligned}
\mathbb{E}\!\big[\|z \cdot \Delta H_W\|_2^2\big] &= \mathbb{E}\!\Big[\operatorname{Tr}\!\big(\Delta H_W^\top z^\top z\, \Delta H_W\big)\Big] \\
&= \operatorname{Tr}\!\Big(\Delta H_W^\top \underbrace{\mathbb{E}[z^\top z]}_{\Sigma_P} \Delta H_W\Big) \\
&= \|\Sigma_P^{1/2} \Delta H_W\|_F^2.
\end{aligned}
\end{equation}
Taking the square root:
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:gamma_final}
\gamma(W) \le K_{\mathrm{pred}} \cdot \|\Sigma_P^{1/2}(W H_T - H_P)\|_F.
\end{equation}

\paragraph{Interpretation.} The second-moment weighting $\Sigma_P^{1/2}$ projects the head error onto the \emph{active subspace} of the data. If the heads disagree only in directions orthogonal to the feature distribution (the null space of $\Sigma_P$), this disagreement has zero cost---it does not affect any prediction. This is strictly tighter than the spectral bound $\gamma(W) \le K_{\mathrm{pred}} \|W H_T - H_P\|_2 \cdot \mathbb{E}[\|z\|_2]$, which assumes worst-case alignment between features and head error.


\subsection{Step 5: Assembling the Unified Bound}
\label{app:assembly}

Combining the risk decomposition (Eq.~\ref{eq:app_triangle}), the feature bound (Eq.~\ref{eq:delta_final}), and the head bound (Eq.~\ref{eq:gamma_final}), for any $W \in \mathcal{O}(d)$:
\begin{equation}
\begin{aligned}
|\mathcal{R}_T - \mathcal{R}_P| &\le \delta(W) + \gamma(W) \\
&\le K_{\mathrm{feat}} \sqrt{(\rho_T - \rho_P)^2 + 2\rho_T\rho_P(1 - \Omega_W)} + K_{\mathrm{pred}} \cdot \|\Sigma_P^{1/2}(W H_T - H_P)\|_F.
\end{aligned}
\end{equation}
This completes the proof of Theorem~\ref{thm:unified_bound}. \qed



\section{Tightness of Nuclear Norm over Frobenius Norm}
\label{app:tightness}

We show that the nuclear-norm-based Procrustes Similarity $\Omega$ yields a strictly tighter alignment residual than its Frobenius-based counterpart $\Omega_F$, and clarify the relationship to CKA.

\paragraph{Statement.}
Define the Frobenius-based similarity $\Omega_F := \|Z_T^\top Z_P\|_F / (\|Z_T\|_F \|Z_P\|_F)$. For any feature matrices $Z_T, Z_P$:
\begin{equation}
\Omega_F \le \Omega,
\end{equation}
with equality if and only if the cross-moment matrix $Z_T^\top Z_P$ has at most one nonzero singular value (i.e., rank $\le 1$).

\paragraph{Proof.}
Let $\sigma_1, \ldots, \sigma_d$ be the singular values of $Z_T^\top Z_P$. Then:
\begin{equation}
\|Z_T^\top Z_P\|_F = \sqrt{\sum_i \sigma_i^2}, \qquad \|Z_T^\top Z_P\|_* = \sum_i \sigma_i.
\end{equation}
By the Cauchy--Schwarz inequality applied to the vectors $(\sigma_1, \ldots, \sigma_d)$ and $(1, \ldots, 1)$:
\begin{equation}
\|Z_T^\top Z_P\|_F = \sqrt{\sum_i \sigma_i^2} \le \sum_i \sigma_i = \|Z_T^\top Z_P\|_*,
\end{equation}
since $\|\mathbf{x}\|_1 \ge \|\mathbf{x}\|_2$ for any non-negative vector. Dividing both sides by $\|Z_T\|_F \|Z_P\|_F$ gives $\Omega_F \le \Omega$.

\paragraph{Consequence for Risk Bounding.}
The alignment residual using $\Omega_F$ is:
\begin{equation}
(\rho_T - \rho_P)^2 + 2\rho_T\rho_P(1 - \Omega_F) \ge (\rho_T - \rho_P)^2 + 2\rho_T\rho_P(1 - \Omega).
\end{equation}
Therefore, substituting $\Omega_F$ into the unified bound produces a \emph{strictly looser} upper bound on risk discrepancy. The nuclear norm $\Omega$ is the tightest possible metric because it exactly solves the Procrustes optimization---any other norm-based similarity applied to the same alignment residual either underestimates the alignment or fails to correspond to a well-defined geometric minimization.

\paragraph{Relation to CKA.}
Linear CKA~\cite{kornblith2019similarity} is defined as $\mathrm{CKA}(Z_T, Z_P) = \|Z_T^\top Z_P\|_F^2 / (\|Z_T^\top Z_T\|_F \cdot \|Z_P^\top Z_P\|_F)$, which differs from $\Omega_F^2 = \|Z_T^\top Z_P\|_F^2 / (\|Z_T\|_F^2 \|Z_P\|_F^2)$ in its denominator. The two denominators are related by the inequality $\|Z_M^\top Z_M\|_F \le \|Z_M\|_F^2 = \operatorname{Tr}(Z_M^\top Z_M)$ (since $\|A\|_F \le \operatorname{Tr}(A)$ for any positive semidefinite $A$, by Cauchy--Schwarz on the eigenvalues), which gives $\mathrm{CKA} \ge \Omega_F^2$. Hence CKA can overestimate alignment relative to $\Omega_F$, and it is not directly substitutable into our Procrustes residual. More fundamentally, CKA is not derived from any alignment optimization and therefore lacks the operational meaning of $\Omega$, which exactly solves the Orthogonal Procrustes problem.

\section{The Trace Specialization ($W = I$)}
\label{app:trace_specialization}

We characterize the identity alignment $W = I$ as a specialization of Proposition~\ref{prop:exact_decomposition} and analyze when it coincides with or diverges from the Procrustes-optimal bound.

\paragraph{Feature Decomposition under $W = I$.}
Setting $W = I$ in Proposition~\ref{prop:exact_decomposition}, the alignment-dependent similarity (Eq.~\ref{eq:omega_W_def}) reduces to $\Omega_I = \operatorname{Tr}(Z_T^\top Z_P)/(\|Z_T\|_F\|Z_P\|_F) =: \Omega_{\mathrm{tr}}$ (Eq.~\ref{eq:omega_tr}), and the feature residual becomes:
\begin{equation}
\frac{1}{n}\|Z_T - Z_P\|_F^2
= (\rho_T - \rho_P)^2 + 2\rho_T\rho_P\big(1 - \Omega_{\mathrm{tr}}\big).
\end{equation}

\paragraph{Ordering of Similarities.}
Since the Procrustes solution maximizes $\operatorname{Tr}(Z_T^\top Z_P W)$ over $\mathcal{O}(d)$, we have:
\begin{equation}
\operatorname{Tr}(Z_T^\top Z_P) \;\le\; \max_{W \in \mathcal{O}(d)} \operatorname{Tr}(Z_T^\top Z_P W) \;=\; \|Z_T^\top Z_P\|_*,
\end{equation}
which gives $\Omega_{\mathrm{tr}} \le \Omega$. Equality holds if and only if $W^* = I$, i.e., $Z_T^\top Z_P$ is symmetric positive semidefinite (all singular vectors already aligned).

\paragraph{When does $\Omega_{\mathrm{tr}} = \Omega$?}
Let $Z_T^\top Z_P = U\Sigma V^\top$ be the SVD. Then $W^* = VU^\top$ and $\Omega_{\mathrm{tr}} = \Omega$ iff $VU^\top = I$, i.e., $U = V$. This holds when $Z_T^\top Z_P$ is symmetric (hence SPSD, since its singular values are non-negative). In post-training quantization with mild noise, the cross-moment matrix $Z_F^\top Z_Q \approx Z_F^\top Z_F$ is approximately SPSD, so $\Omega_{\mathrm{tr}} \approx \Omega$. As quantization becomes more aggressive, asymmetric perturbations cause $\Omega_{\mathrm{tr}}$ to fall below $\Omega$, and this gap reflects genuine asymmetric distortion that a rotation-invariant metric would mask.

\paragraph{Full Bound under $W = I$.}
Combining the feature and head terms:
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:bound_identity}
|\mathcal{R}_T - \mathcal{R}_P|
\;\le\;
K_{\mathrm{feat}} \sqrt{(\rho_T - \rho_P)^2 + 2\rho_T\rho_P(1 - \Omega_{\mathrm{tr}})}
\;+\;
K_{\mathrm{pred}}\,\|\Sigma_P^{1/2}(H_T - H_P)\|_F.
\end{equation}
In the quantization regime ($H_T \approx H_P$), the head term vanishes and the bound depends solely on the geometric distortion terms $\Delta\rho$ and $\Omega_{\mathrm{tr}}$.

\paragraph{Relation to CKA.}
The trace similarity $\Omega_{\mathrm{tr}}$ relates to linear CKA as follows. Recall that linear CKA uses the Frobenius norm of the cross-moment in its numerator: $\mathrm{CKA} = \|Z_T^\top Z_P\|_F^2 / (\|Z_T^\top Z_T\|_F \cdot \|Z_P^\top Z_P\|_F)$. While $\Omega_{\mathrm{tr}}$ and CKA both avoid explicit rotational alignment, they differ in two key respects: (i)~$\Omega_{\mathrm{tr}}$ uses the trace (sum of diagonal entries of $Z_T^\top Z_P$) rather than the Frobenius norm, which is sensitive to the sign structure of the cross-moment; (ii)~$\Omega_{\mathrm{tr}}$ has a direct operational meaning as the alignment residual under $W = I$, whereas CKA lacks a corresponding optimization interpretation. Both $\Omega_{\mathrm{tr}}$ and $\Omega_F$ are bounded above by $\Omega$ (since $\operatorname{Tr}(M) \le \|M\|_*$ and $\|M\|_F \le \|M\|_*$ for any matrix $M$), but $\Omega_{\mathrm{tr}}$ and $\Omega_F$ do not have a fixed ordering relative to each other (where $\Omega_F$ is the Frobenius similarity from Appendix~\ref{app:tightness}).


\section{Joint Optimization on the Stiefel Manifold}
\label{app:joint_opt}

We analyze the joint minimization of the risk bound over all orthogonal alignments and show why a closed-form solution does not exist in general.

\paragraph{The Joint Objective.}
The tightest bound requires solving:
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:joint_obj}
W_{\mathrm{opt}} = \arg\min_{W \in \mathcal{O}(d)}
\Big[
\underbrace{K_{\mathrm{feat}} \sqrt{\tfrac{1}{n}\|Z_T - Z_P W\|_F^2}}_{\delta(W)}
\;+\;
\underbrace{K_{\mathrm{pred}}\,\|\Sigma_P^{1/2}(W H_T - H_P)\|_F}_{\gamma(W)}
\Big].
\end{equation}
Unlike the standard Procrustes problem (which minimizes $\delta$ alone and admits the closed-form solution $W^* = VU^\top$), the addition of $\gamma(W)$ introduces a quadratic dependence on $W$ through the head term: expanding $\gamma(W)^2$ yields a term $\operatorname{Tr}(H_T^\top W^\top \Sigma_P W H_T)$, where $\Sigma_P$ is ``sandwiched'' between $W^\top$ and $W$ and cannot be simplified via $W^\top W = I$ unless $\Sigma_P \propto I$.

\paragraph{Why No Closed-Form Solution Exists.}
The feature term $\delta(W)$ is concave in $\operatorname{Tr}(Z_T^\top Z_P W)$ (since it involves the square root of a linear-minus-trace expression), while the head term $\gamma(W)$ is convex in $W$. Their sum is neither convex nor concave on $\mathcal{O}(d)$, precluding a simple variational characterization. This structure is related to the \emph{Weighted Orthogonal Procrustes Problem} (WOPP), which is known to lack closed-form solutions in general~\cite{gower2004procrustes}.

\paragraph{Special Case: Isotropic Features.}
If the proxy feature distribution is isotropic ($\Sigma_P \approx \lambda I$ for some $\lambda > 0$), the quadratic head term simplifies:
\begin{equation}
\operatorname{Tr}(H_T^\top W^\top (\lambda I) W H_T)
= \lambda\,\operatorname{Tr}(H_T^\top H_T),
\end{equation}
which is independent of $W$. The joint objective then reduces to a standard Procrustes problem with a modified cross-moment matrix:
\begin{equation}
M_{\mathrm{joint}} = \alpha\, Z_T^\top Z_P + \beta\, H_T H_P^\top,
\end{equation}
where $\alpha = K_{\mathrm{feat}}$ and $\beta = K_{\mathrm{pred}}\lambda$. The closed-form solution $W_{\mathrm{iso}}^* = V_J U_J^\top$ (from the SVD $M_{\mathrm{joint}} = U_J \Sigma_J V_J^\top$) simultaneously aligns both features and heads. However, the isotropic assumption is unrealistic for LLM representations, which exhibit well-documented anisotropy---including representation degeneration~\cite{ethayarajh2019contextual} and outlier channels~\cite{dettmers2022gpt3, xiao2023smoothquant}.

\paragraph{Numerical Solution via Riemannian Gradient Descent.}
For the general (anisotropic) case, $W_{\mathrm{opt}}$ can be approximated numerically via Riemannian gradient descent (RGD) on the Stiefel manifold. The Euclidean gradient of a squared surrogate $J(W) = \alpha\|Z_T - Z_P W\|_F^2 + \beta\|\Sigma_P^{1/2}(WH_T - H_P)\|_F^2$ is:
\begin{equation}
\nabla_W J = -2\alpha\, Z_P^\top Z_T + 2\beta\, \Sigma_P W H_T H_T^\top - 2\beta\, \Sigma_P H_P H_T^\top,
\end{equation}
which is projected onto the tangent space of $\mathcal{O}(d)$ at $W$ and followed by a retraction (e.g., QR decomposition or polar decomposition) to maintain orthogonality. The isotropic solution $W_{\mathrm{iso}}^*$ serves as an effective warm-start initialization, placing the iterate within the basin of attraction of the global minimum and reducing convergence to a small number of steps.

\paragraph{Practical Irrelevance for This Paper's Regimes.}
Although the Riemannian approach yields the theoretically tightest bound, it entangles the scale, shape, and head components: $W_{\mathrm{opt}}$ compromises feature alignment to compensate for head error, destroying the clean physical interpretation of each term. In the two primary regimes of this paper:
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Quantization ($H_T \approx H_P$):} The head term $\gamma$ is negligible for any $W$, so $W_{\mathrm{opt}} \approx W^* \approx I$ and all three candidates ($I$, $W^*$, $W_{\mathrm{opt}}$) yield nearly identical bounds. The identity alignment is preferred for its interpretability.
    \item \textbf{LoRA ($H_T = H_P$):} The head term vanishes identically for any $W$, so the joint problem reduces to the standard Procrustes problem. Again, $W = I$ is preferred for diagnostic clarity.
\end{itemize}
The Riemannian formulation becomes relevant when comparing models with \emph{both} rotated features and divergent heads (e.g., cross-model comparison or full-parameter SFT with head updates). We leave detailed empirical investigation of this regime to future work.
