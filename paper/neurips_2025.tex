\begin{abstract}
Efficient machine learning increasingly relies on \emph{proxy models}---such as smaller checkpoints, quantized variants, or historical snapshots---to estimate the behavior of expensive target models. However, the theoretical conditions under which a proxy accurately reflects a target's performance landscape remain poorly understood. In this work, we propose a unified geometric framework linking \emph{linear representational alignment} to \emph{functional risk proximity}. We derive an exact decomposition of feature discrepancy using the nuclear norm of the cross-covariance matrix, introducing the \emph{Procrustes Similarity Coefficient} ($\Omega$) as a computable, generation-free metric for alignment. We further bound prediction head discrepancy via spectral operator norms. We demonstrate the versatility of this framework across four distinct high-value scenarios: (1) \textbf{Cross-Scale Model Merging}, where we prove that optimal coefficients can be transferred from small to large models with bounded regret; (2) \textbf{Quantization Quality Estimation}, enabling prediction of performance degradation without ground-truth labels; (3) \textbf{Geometric Monitoring of Catastrophic Forgetting}, providing a lightweight regularizer for continual learning; and (4) \textbf{Out-of-Distribution Detection}, utilizing multi-view geometric consistency to identify anomalies. Our results establish linear geometric alignment as a rigorous, sufficient condition for functional performance transfer.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}

\paragraph{The evaluation bottleneck in modern LLMs.}
Large Language Models (LLMs) have achieved remarkable success across diverse tasks, from multiple-choice reasoning to open-ended generation~\cite{grattafiori2024llama, qwen2025qwen3}.
As these models scale, their post-training lifecycle---from quantization (e.g., GPTQ, AWQ, GGUF) to parameter-efficient fine-tuning (e.g., LoRA, SFT)---generates numerous model variants, yet evaluating how each variant affects downstream performance remains prohibitively expensive.
A single comprehensive evaluation on benchmarks such as MMLU (14{,}000 tasks) can require hours of GPU time per variant, creating a severe bottleneck for practitioners who need to diagnose quality degradation, select among compression configurations, or monitor training dynamics across the model lifecycle.

\paragraph{Existing geometric metrics lack formal risk guarantees.}
Practitioners seeking to circumvent this bottleneck currently face an unsatisfying choice: either run full benchmark suites for every variant, or resort to post-hoc representational similarity metrics such as CKA~\cite{kornblith2019similarity} and SVCCA~\cite{raghu2017svcca}.
These Frobenius-norm-based metrics measure geometric resemblance between internal representations, but provide no formal guarantee that high similarity translates to small performance gaps.
A quantized model may exhibit high CKA similarity to its full-precision parent yet suffer significant accuracy degradation; conversely, a fine-tuned model may show moderate geometric drift while preserving task performance.
This disconnect between geometric similarity and functional risk leaves practitioners without principled tools for predicting when a model variant is safe to deploy.

\paragraph{PRISM: an exact geometric decomposition linking alignment to risk.}
We address this gap by proposing \textbf{PRISM} (Proxy Risk Inference via Structural Mapping), a geometric framework that derives a rigorous, computable bound linking representational alignment to functional risk.
Our key observation is that modern Transformer-based LLMs share a common structural property: a deep non-linear feature extractor (the backbone) followed by a single linear prediction head (\texttt{lm\_head}) that projects representations onto the vocabulary space.
This architecture, combined with the \emph{Linear Representation Hypothesis}~\cite{moschella2022relative, park2023linear}---that models within the same lineage learn feature manifolds related by a near-isometric linear transformation---enables an \emph{exact} geometric analysis of risk discrepancy.
Specifically, by formulating alignment as an Orthogonal Procrustes problem on the Stiefel manifold, we decompose feature discrepancy into two interpretable components: \emph{scale mismatch} ($\Delta\rho$), capturing activation magnitude shifts, and \emph{shape mismatch} ($1 - \Omega$), capturing structural distortion of the feature manifold, where $\Omega$ is the Procrustes Similarity defined via the nuclear norm of the cross-covariance matrix.
Unlike CKA's Frobenius-based formulation, this nuclear-norm decomposition is provably the tightest bound for rotational alignment, explicitly isolating structural distortion from prediction head errors.
Combined with a covariance-adjusted bound on head discrepancy, this yields a closed-form upper bound on risk discrepancy $|\mathcal{R}_T - \mathcal{R}_P|$ that is directly computable from a single forward pass on a small calibration set.

\paragraph{Why Large Language Models.}
Although our framework mathematically applies to any model adopting the feature-extractor-plus-linear-head paradigm, we focus empirical validation exclusively on LLMs for four reasons.
First, LLMs face the most severe \emph{evaluation bottleneck}: the post-training ecosystem generates dozens of quantized and fine-tuned variants per base model, each requiring expensive benchmark evaluation.
Second, the backbone-plus-linear-head decomposition is architecturally exact---the \texttt{lm\_head} is a single linear projection, and under standard LoRA fine-tuning it remains frozen ($H_T = H_P$), isolating performance changes entirely in the backbone geometry.
Third, LLM activations exhibit well-documented \emph{outlier channels} and \emph{scale collapse} phenomena during quantization~\cite{dettmers2022gpt3, xiao2023smoothquant}, which our decomposition into $\Delta\rho$ and $\Omega$ can precisely characterize.
Fourth, our framework naturally unifies both point-wise classification (e.g., MMLU logit scoring) and sequence-level autoregressive generation (e.g., language modeling loss) under a single bound, since teacher-forcing allows all token-level features to be extracted in a single forward pass.

\paragraph{Contributions.}
Our main contributions are as follows:
\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{A Unified Geometric Framework for Risk Bounding.} We establish a rigorous theoretical bridge linking the internal representational geometry of a proxy model to the functional risk of a target model. We derive an exact decomposition of feature discrepancy via the nuclear norm (Procrustes Similarity $\Omega$), providing a strictly tighter bound than Frobenius-based metrics such as CKA. Crucially, our framework naturally unifies point-wise classification and sequence-level autoregressive generation under the same geometric constraints.

    \item \textbf{Exact Decomposition via Nuclear Norm.} Unlike prior post-hoc metrics that rely on Frobenius bounds, we derive an exact geometric decomposition of feature discrepancy using the nuclear norm. We prove this formulation provides the tightest bound for rotational alignment, explicitly isolating scale collapse ($\Delta\rho$) from structural distortion ($1 - \Omega$) and prediction head errors.

    \item \textbf{Principled Diagnostics for Quantization and Fine-Tuning.} We operationalize our theory across two high-value LLM lifecycle stages. In post-training quantization (GGUF, GPTQ, AWQ, BitsAndBytes), our framework mathematically characterizes feature scale collapse. In sequential fine-tuning (SFT, LoRA), geometric drift serves as a label-free monitor for catastrophic forgetting, decoupling backbone manifold geometry from head-specific adaptations.

    \item \textbf{Strong Empirical Correlation.} Extensive experiments across modern LLMs (Llama, Qwen, Mistral, DeepSeek) demonstrate that our geometric bound serves as a highly predictive surrogate for actual performance degradation, achieving Spearman's $\rho > 0.9$ across quantization formats and fine-tuning protocols, establishing linear geometric alignment as a robust, compute-efficient proxy for model evaluation.

    \item \textbf{Compute-Efficient Performance Estimation via Control Variates.} By leveraging the high geometric alignment ($\Omega \approx 1$) between target and proxy models, our framework guarantees minimal variance in risk estimation via Popoviciu's inequality. We demonstrate that full-benchmark performance (e.g., 14{,}000 MMLU tasks) can be accurately forecast from a 500-sample calibration set, reducing evaluation compute by over 95\% with strict statistical guarantees.
\end{enumerate}

\section{Related Work}

\paragraph{Representational Similarity Metrics.}
Comparing the internal representations of neural networks is a long-standing problem.
SVCCA~\cite{raghu2017svcca} projects activations onto their top singular vectors before computing canonical correlations, while CKA~\cite{kornblith2019similarity} measures alignment through kernel-based Hilbert--Schmidt independence.
Both metrics rely on Frobenius-norm statistics, which conflate rotational misalignment with scale differences and provide no formal connection to downstream task performance: two models can exhibit high CKA yet diverge significantly in functional risk, or vice versa.
The \emph{Linear Representation Hypothesis}~\cite{moschella2022relative, park2023linear} offers a structural explanation---models trained on similar data converge to linearly alignable manifolds---but existing work uses this insight only for post-hoc interpretability.
Our framework makes this hypothesis \emph{constructive}: by formulating alignment as an Orthogonal Procrustes problem and measuring discrepancy via the nuclear norm ($\Omega$), we derive a provably tighter decomposition that \emph{exactly} separates scale mismatch ($\Delta\rho$) from shape mismatch ($1-\Omega$) and directly bounds the risk gap $|\mathcal{R}_T - \mathcal{R}_P|$.

\paragraph{Post-Training Quantization and Its Evaluation.}
Post-training quantization (PTQ) has become the dominant approach for deploying LLMs under memory constraints.
GPTQ~\cite{frantar2022gptq} uses approximate second-order information to minimize layer-wise reconstruction error, AWQ~\cite{lin2024awq} preserves salient weight channels identified by activation magnitudes, and round-to-nearest methods in GGUF and BitsAndBytes~\cite{dettmers2022gpt3} offer simpler but less controlled compression.
A common bottleneck across all these methods is \emph{quality assessment}: determining how much a quantized variant has degraded requires running full perplexity or accuracy evaluations on large benchmarks, which can be as expensive as the quantization process itself.
Existing diagnostics are limited to indirect proxies such as layer-wise reconstruction loss or weight quantization error, neither of which accounts for the nonlinear accumulation of errors through the full network.
PRISM addresses this gap by providing a \emph{generation-free} geometric diagnostic: our bound decomposes quantization-induced degradation into scale collapse ($\Delta\rho$, capturing activation clipping from outlier channels~\cite{xiao2023smoothquant}) and structural distortion ($1-\Omega$), both computable from a single forward pass on a small calibration set without any label or generation.

\paragraph{Catastrophic Forgetting in Continual and Parameter-Efficient Fine-Tuning.}
When LLMs are sequentially fine-tuned---whether through full SFT or parameter-efficient methods such as LoRA~\cite{hu2022lora}---they risk catastrophic forgetting of pre-training knowledge.
Classical remedies operate in weight space: EWC~\cite{kirkpatrick2017overcoming} penalizes changes to important parameters via a diagonal Fisher approximation, while replay-based methods retain a buffer of old data.
These weight-space constraints are rigid: they penalize any parameter movement equally regardless of whether the underlying feature geometry is preserved.
Our framework offers a complementary, \emph{representation-space} perspective.
We monitor forgetting through the geometric drift of the feature manifold ($\Omega$, $\Delta\rho$) relative to the frozen base model, which naturally permits global rotations and rescalings that do not affect downstream risk.
Notably, under standard LoRA fine-tuning where the prediction head is frozen ($H_T = H_P$), our bound isolates forgetting entirely in the backbone geometry, providing a label-free diagnostic that decouples manifold distortion from head-specific effects.

\paragraph{Performance Forecasting for Foundation Models.}
The prohibitive cost of evaluating LLM variants has motivated several forecasting approaches.
Scaling laws~\cite{kaplan2020scaling, hoffmann2022training} predict aggregate loss as a power-law function of compute and data, but cannot capture variant-specific degradation (e.g., the effect of a particular quantization format on a specific benchmark).
Weak-to-strong generalization~\cite{burns2023weak} explores using smaller models to supervise larger ones, but targets label generation rather than risk estimation.
PRISM introduces a distinct paradigm: \emph{Geometric Control Variates}.
Rather than extrapolating from scaling trends, we exploit the fact that a geometrically aligned proxy ($\Omega \approx 1$) produces per-sample losses that are highly correlated with those of the target.
Our unified bound guarantees that the variance of the loss difference $\ell_T(x) - \ell_P(x)$ is controlled by the geometric terms, enabling accurate estimation of full-benchmark performance from a small calibration set with strict statistical guarantees via Popoviciu's inequality.



\section{A Geometric Framework for Proxy Performance Estimation}
\label{sec:geometric_foundation}

We derive a rigorous upper bound on the risk discrepancy $|\mathcal{R}_T - \mathcal{R}_P|$ between a target model $T$ and a proxy model $P$, grounded in their representational geometry. We first formalize the backbone-plus-linear-head architecture, then state our main theorem linking geometric alignment to functional risk, and finally extend the bound to autoregressive generation.

\subsection{Problem Setup}
\label{subsec:problem_setup}

\paragraph{The Backbone-Plus-Linear-Head Architecture.}
We consider two models sharing the same architecture: a \emph{Target Model} $T$ (e.g., a full-precision base model) and a \emph{Proxy Model} $P$ (e.g., its quantized or fine-tuned variant), both with hidden dimension $d$. Each model $M \in \{T, P\}$ decomposes as:
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:model_decomp}
f_M(x) = \mathrm{softmax}\!\big(\phi_M(x) \cdot H_M\big),
\end{equation}
where:
\begin{itemize}
    \item $\phi_M: \mathcal{X} \to \mathbb{R}^{d}$ is the \emph{feature extractor} (Transformer backbone), mapping an input $x$ to a $d$-dimensional feature vector $z_M = \phi_M(x)$;
    \item $H_M \in \mathbb{R}^{d \times V}$ is the \emph{prediction head} (\texttt{lm\_head}), a linear projection onto the vocabulary space of size $V$. We denote its column vectors (token embeddings) as $h_{M,1}, \ldots, h_{M,V} \in \mathbb{R}^d$;
    \item $\mathrm{softmax}(\cdot)$ converts the logit vector $v_M = z_M \cdot H_M \in \mathbb{R}^V$ into a probability distribution over tokens.
\end{itemize}

\paragraph{Risk via Cross-Entropy.}
We measure model quality through the cross-entropy loss. For a data distribution $\mathcal{D}$ over input-label pairs $(x, y)$, the \emph{risk} of model $M$ is:
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:risk_def}
\mathcal{R}_M = \mathbb{E}_{(x,y) \sim \mathcal{D}}\!\Big[\ell\!\big(\phi_M(x) \cdot H_M,\; y\big)\Big],
\end{equation}
where the cross-entropy loss is defined as:
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:ce_def}
\ell(v, y) = -\log\!\big(\mathrm{softmax}(v)_y\big) = -v_y + \log\!\sum_{j=1}^{V} e^{v_j},
\end{equation}
with $v = \phi_M(x) \cdot H_M \in \mathbb{R}^V$ the logit vector and $y$ the index of the ground-truth token.

\paragraph{The Linear Alignment Hypothesis.}
Following the Linear Representation Hypothesis~\cite{moschella2022relative, park2023linear}, we posit that models within the same lineage---such as a base model and its quantized or fine-tuned variant---learn feature manifolds related by a near-isometric linear transformation. Formally, there exists an orthogonal alignment map $W \in \mathcal{O}(d) = \{W \in \mathbb{R}^{d \times d} \mid W^\top W = I_d\}$ such that:
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:alignment_hypothesis}
\phi_T(x) \approx \phi_P(x)\, W, \quad \forall x \in \mathcal{X}.
\end{equation}
The orthogonality constraint ensures that $W$ preserves inner products and norms ($\|z_P W\|_2 = \|z_P\|_2$), thereby isolating genuine geometric misalignment from trivial scale effects.

\paragraph{Notation.}
Given $n$ evaluation samples, we collect features into matrices $Z_T, Z_P \in \mathbb{R}^{n \times d}$ with rows $z_{T,i} = \phi_T(x_i)$ and $z_{P,i} = \phi_P(x_i)$. We define:
\begin{itemize}
    \item $\rho_M = \frac{1}{\sqrt{n}}\|Z_M\|_F$: the root-mean-square (RMS) feature scale of model $M$;
    \item $\Sigma_P = \frac{1}{n} Z_P^\top Z_P \in \mathbb{R}^{d \times d}$: the (uncentered) empirical feature covariance of the proxy.
\end{itemize}


\subsection{The Unified Risk Bound}
\label{subsec:unified_bound}

We first introduce our primary alignment metric, then present the exact geometric decomposition and the main theorem.

\begin{definition}[Procrustes Similarity $\Omega$]
\label{def:omega}
The Procrustes Similarity $\Omega(Z_T, Z_P) \in [0, 1]$ measures the structural alignment between two feature manifolds via the nuclear norm of their cross-covariance matrix:
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:omega_def}
\Omega(Z_T, Z_P) := \frac{\| Z_T^\top Z_P \|_*}{\| Z_T \|_F \| Z_P \|_F},
\end{equation}
where $\|\cdot\|_*$ denotes the nuclear norm (sum of singular values). $\Omega = 1$ if and only if the feature manifolds are perfectly aligned up to orthogonal transformation; $\Omega = 0$ indicates complete misalignment.
\end{definition}

The nuclear norm arises naturally from the Orthogonal Procrustes problem: finding the best rotation to align two point clouds. The following proposition shows that the resulting alignment residual decomposes \emph{exactly} into two interpretable terms.

\begin{proposition}[Exact Geometric Decomposition]
\label{prop:exact_decomposition}
The minimum feature alignment error under orthogonal transformation admits the exact equality:
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:exact_equality}
\min_{W \in \mathcal{O}(d)} \frac{1}{n} \| Z_T - Z_P W \|_F^2
\;=\;
\underbrace{(\rho_T - \rho_P)^2}_{\text{\rm Scale Mismatch } (\Delta\rho)^2}
\;+\;
\underbrace{2 \rho_T \rho_P \big( 1 - \Omega \big)}_{\text{\rm Shape Mismatch}}.
\end{equation}
The optimal alignment is $W^* = V U^\top$, where $Z_T^\top Z_P = U \Sigma V^\top$ is the compact SVD.
\end{proposition}

\noindent This identity is exact, not an approximation: minimizing feature discrepancy is mathematically equivalent to maximizing $\Omega$ while matching scales $\rho$. The full derivation is given in Appendix~\ref{app:procrustes}.

We now combine this geometric decomposition with a Lipschitz analysis of the cross-entropy loss and a covariance-adjusted head bound to obtain our main result.

\begin{theorem}[Unified Risk Bound]
\label{thm:unified_bound}
Let $T$ and $P$ be target and proxy models with the backbone-plus-linear-head architecture (Eq.~\ref{eq:model_decomp}), and let $W^* \in \mathcal{O}(d)$ be the optimal Procrustes alignment. Then the risk discrepancy satisfies:
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:unified_bound_eq}
\begin{aligned}
|\mathcal{R}_T - \mathcal{R}_P| \;\;\le\;\; & \underbrace{K_{\mathrm{feat}} \sqrt{ (\rho_T - \rho_P)^2 + 2\rho_T \rho_P \big( 1 - \Omega(Z_T, Z_P) \big) }}_{\text{\rm Geometric Alignment Error}} \\
& + \underbrace{K_{\mathrm{pred}} \cdot \| \Sigma_P^{1/2} (W^* H_T - H_P) \|_F}_{\text{\rm Covariance-Aware Head Error}},
\end{aligned}
\end{equation}
where the Lipschitz constants are derived from the cross-entropy structure (Appendix~\ref{app:kfeat}):
\begin{itemize}
    \item $K_{\mathrm{feat}} = \max_{j,k} \|h_{T,j} - h_{T,k}\|_2$: the maximum pairwise distance between column vectors (token embeddings) of $H_T$, governing the sensitivity of the loss to feature perturbations;
    \item $K_{\mathrm{pred}} \le \sqrt{2}$: the Lipschitz constant of cross-entropy with respect to logit perturbations.
\end{itemize}
\end{theorem}

The proof (Appendix~\ref{app:detailed_proofs}) proceeds in three steps:
\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{Risk Decomposition} (Appendix~\ref{app:risk_decomp}): A triangle inequality introduces an intermediate ``hybrid'' model that uses the proxy's aligned features with the target's head, splitting $|\mathcal{R}_T - \mathcal{R}_P|$ into a feature alignment term $\delta$ and a head discrepancy term $\gamma$.
    \item \textbf{Bounding $\delta$ via Procrustes} (Appendix~\ref{app:procrustes}): The Lipschitz property of cross-entropy with respect to features (with constant $K_{\mathrm{feat}}$ derived from the gradient $\nabla_z \ell$) combined with Jensen's inequality and Proposition~\ref{prop:exact_decomposition} yields the geometric alignment error.
    \item \textbf{Bounding $\gamma$ via Covariance Projection} (Appendix~\ref{app:head_bound}): The head discrepancy is bounded by projecting the head misalignment $\Delta H = W^* H_T - H_P$ onto the data covariance $\Sigma_P$, ensuring that only the \emph{active subspace} of the feature distribution contributes to the bound.
\end{enumerate}


\subsection{Geometric Interpretation}
\label{subsec:interpretation}

Theorem~\ref{thm:unified_bound} reveals three sufficient conditions for a proxy to reliably estimate target risk:

\paragraph{Scale Matching ($\rho_T \approx \rho_P$).}
The models must operate at similar activation magnitudes. In post-training quantization, aggressive bit-width reduction clips activation outliers~\cite{dettmers2022gpt3, xiao2023smoothquant}, shrinking $\rho_Q < \rho_F$. Our bound identifies this \emph{scale collapse} as a primary and independently measurable source of degradation.

\paragraph{Shape Alignment ($\Omega \to 1$).}
The feature manifolds must preserve their relational structure up to rotation. A drop in $\Omega$ indicates that the relative arrangement of token representations has been corrupted---what we term \emph{structural distortion}. Critically, $\Omega$ is defined via the nuclear norm, which provides the tightest possible bound for the Procrustes alignment residual; Frobenius-based metrics such as CKA conflate rotational and scale effects and yield provably looser bounds (Appendix~\ref{app:tightness}).

\paragraph{Head Coherence ($\|\Sigma_P^{1/2}(W^* H_T - H_P)\|_F \to 0$).}
The prediction heads must agree on the aligned features, but only within the active subspace of the data covariance $\Sigma_P$. Misalignment in directions orthogonal to the data manifold is permissible, as it does not affect predictions. Notably, under standard LoRA fine-tuning~\cite{hu2022lora} where the \texttt{lm\_head} is frozen ($H_T = H_P$), this term vanishes entirely, isolating all performance change in the backbone geometry ($\Delta\rho$ and $\Omega$).


\subsection{Extension to Autoregressive Generation}
\label{subsec:ar_extension}

The framework above treats each evaluation as a single feature-to-loss mapping. We now show that it extends naturally to sequence-level autoregressive generation, unifying both point-wise classification and language modeling under a single bound.

\paragraph{Autoregressive Risk.}
For a sequence $(c, y)$ drawn from $\mathcal{D}$, where $c$ denotes the context (prompt) and $y = (y_1, \ldots, y_{|y|})$ the target continuation, the autoregressive risk is:
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:ar_risk}
\mathcal{R}_M^{\mathrm{AR}} = \mathbb{E}_{(c, y) \sim \mathcal{D}} \left[ \frac{1}{|y|} \sum_{t=1}^{|y|} \ell\!\big(\phi_M(c, y_{<t}) \cdot H_M,\; y_t\big) \right],
\end{equation}
where $\phi_M(c, y_{<t}) \in \mathbb{R}^d$ is the feature vector at position $t$, encoding the context $c$ and all preceding target tokens $y_{<t}$.

\paragraph{From Tokens to Matrices.}
Under teacher forcing, all token-level features $\{\phi_M(c, y_{<t})\}_{t=1}^{|y|}$ are extracted in a single forward pass---each token contributes an independent feature-loss pair to the average. Collecting all $N = \sum_{(c,y)} |y|$ token-level features into a single matrix $Z_M^{\mathrm{AR}} \in \mathbb{R}^{N \times d}$, we apply Theorem~\ref{thm:unified_bound} directly:

\begin{corollary}[Autoregressive Risk Bound]
\label{cor:ar_bound}
Let $Z_T^{\mathrm{AR}}, Z_P^{\mathrm{AR}} \in \mathbb{R}^{N \times d}$ be the token-level feature matrices collected under teacher forcing. Then:
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:ar_bound}
|\mathcal{R}_T^{\mathrm{AR}} - \mathcal{R}_P^{\mathrm{AR}}| \;\le\; K_{\mathrm{feat}} \sqrt{(\rho_T^{\scriptscriptstyle\mathrm{AR}} - \rho_P^{\scriptscriptstyle\mathrm{AR}})^2 + 2\,\rho_T^{\scriptscriptstyle\mathrm{AR}}\,\rho_P^{\scriptscriptstyle\mathrm{AR}}\!\big(1 - \Omega^{\mathrm{AR}}\big)} + K_{\mathrm{pred}} \,\big\|\Sigma_P^{\mathrm{AR}\,1/2}(W^* H_T - H_P)\big\|_F,
\end{equation}
where $\rho^{\mathrm{AR}}$, $\Omega^{\mathrm{AR}}$, and $\Sigma_P^{\mathrm{AR}}$ are computed over the token-level matrices $Z^{\mathrm{AR}}$.
\end{corollary}

\paragraph{Unification of Evaluation Paradigms.}
The autoregressive formulation (Eq.~\ref{eq:ar_risk}) subsumes all standard LLM evaluation protocols by varying the partition between context $c$ and target $y$:
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Multiple-Choice Classification} (e.g., MMLU, ARC): $c$ is the full prompt, $y$ is a single answer token (\texttt{A}/\texttt{B}/\texttt{C}/\texttt{D}). The risk reduces to point-wise classification with one feature per question, evaluated via logit scoring.
    \item \textbf{Language Modeling} (e.g., WikiText, C4): $c$ is empty or a rolling prefix, $y$ is a contiguous text chunk. The risk captures the standard next-token prediction loss across the full sequence.
    \item \textbf{Short-Horizon Generation} (e.g., TriviaQA, SQuAD): $c$ is the question prompt, $y$ is the answer span (typically 1--5 tokens). The loss is evaluated strictly over the answer tokens.
\end{itemize}
In all cases, the same geometric bound applies---only the feature extraction protocol differs. For multiple-choice tasks, we extract the feature of the \emph{final prompt token}; for language modeling and generation, $Z^{\mathrm{AR}}$ comprises all target-position features across evaluation sequences.

\section{Applications: From Compression to Cross-Family Transfer}
\label{sec:applications}

The PRISM framework and the Unified Risk Discrepancy Bound (Theorem~\ref{thm:unified_bound}) provide a generic mechanism to estimate the functional divergence between any two models sharing a semantic lineage.
To demonstrate the universality of this geometric perspective, we instantiate the framework across a spectrum of application scenarios.
We organize these applications by the increasing complexity of the geometric relationship between the Proxy ($P$) and the Target ($T$), explicitly characterizing the dimensions ($d_P, d_T$) and the alignment map ($W$):

\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{The Identity Regime ($d_P = d_T, W = I$):}
    In the most constrained setting, the proxy and target share the same architecture and coordinate basis. The alignment map $W$ degenerates to the identity matrix. We apply this to:
    \begin{itemize}
        \item \textbf{Quantization Quality Estimation (Sec.~\ref{subsec:quantization}):} Detecting feature collapse in low-precision models purely via geometric distortion.
    \end{itemize}

    \item \textbf{The Rotational Regime ($d_P = d_T, W \in \mathcal{O}(d)$):}
    Here, models maintain the same dimensionality but undergo global basis rotations due to training dynamics. The alignment $W$ is an orthogonal matrix ($W^\top W = I$). We apply this to:
    \begin{itemize}
        \item \textbf{Monitoring Catastrophic Forgetting (Sec.~\ref{subsec:forgetting}):} Tracking the geometric drift of a model away from its pre-training manifold.
        \item \textbf{Out-of-Distribution Detection (Sec.~\ref{subsec:ood}):} Using the breakdown of the learned rotation $W$ as a signal for anomaly detection.
    \end{itemize}

    \item \textbf{The Projective Regime ($d_P \neq d_T, W \in \mathbb{R}^{d_P \times d_T}$):}
    In the general setting, models differ in width. $W \in \mathbb{R}^{d_P \times d_T}$ acts as an \emph{isometric embedding} (semi-orthogonal map) that aligns the lower-dimensional manifold into the higher-dimensional space. The Procrustes metric $\Omega$ remains valid via the nuclear norm of the rectangular cross-covariance matrix. We apply this to:
    \begin{itemize}
        \item \textbf{Cross-Scale Model Merging (Sec.~\ref{subsec:merging}):} Transferring optimal merging coefficients from a small proxy ($d_P < d_T$) to a large target.
        \item \textbf{Generalization Forecasting (Sec.~\ref{subsec:generalization_forecasting}):} Estimating the performance of massive fine-tuned models using lightweight proxies.
        \item \textbf{Cross-Family Capability Transfer (Sec.~\ref{subsec:cross_family_transfer}):} Analyzing capability alignment between entirely disjoint families (e.g., Llama to Gemma).
    \end{itemize}
\end{enumerate}

This progression, from identity to rotation to projection, illustrates that linear geometric alignment is not merely a heuristic, but a rigorous invariant connecting the entire lifecycle of foundation models.

% 5.1 Quantization Quality Estimation
% -------------------------------------------------------------------------
\subsection{Quantization Quality Estimation}
\label{subsec:quantization}

Deploying Large Language Models (LLMs) often necessitates quantization (e.g., from FP16 to INT4). Traditionally, assessing the quality of a quantized model relative to its full-precision counterpart requires expensive perplexity evaluations.
Our framework allows for an efficient, \emph{geometry-based estimation} of quantization degradation without running full generation tasks.

\paragraph{Geometric Instantiation ($T \to F, P \to Q$).}
We instantiate our general framework by designating the full-precision model as the Target ($T = \theta_F$) and the quantized model as the Proxy ($P = \theta_Q$).
In this setting, the architectural dimensions match ($d_T = d_P$), but the representation manifolds differ due to numerical precision limits.
Two simplifying conditions specific to quantization typically hold:
\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{Identity Alignment ($W = I$):} Unlike model merging where features may be rotated, standard Post-Training Quantization (PTQ) introduces element-wise numerical noise but preserves the coordinate basis. Thus, the optimal alignment map $W$ degenerates to the identity matrix $I$.
    \item \textbf{Head Stability:} Modern quantization protocols (e.g., GPTQ, AWQ) often retain the prediction head in high precision (FP16) to avoid logits collapse. Consequently, $H_Q \approx H_F$, causing the head discrepancy term $\| \Sigma_Q^{1/2} (H_F - H_Q) \|_F$ to vanish or become negligible.
\end{enumerate}

\paragraph{The Quantization Bound.}
Under these conditions, the Unified Bound (Theorem~\ref{thm:unified_bound}) simplifies significantly. The risk gap is dominated purely by the geometric deformation of the feature manifold:
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:quant_bound}
|\mathcal{R}_F - \mathcal{R}_Q| \;\;\lessapprox\;\; K_{\mathrm{feat}} \sqrt{ \underbrace{(\rho_F - \rho_Q)^2}_{\text{\rm Scale Collapse}} + \underbrace{2\rho_F \rho_Q \big(1 - \Omega(Z_F, Z_Q)\big)}_{\text{\rm Structural Distortion}} }.
\end{equation}

\paragraph{Implication: Detecting Feature Collapse.}
This derivation establishes the Procrustes Similarity $\Omega$ and RMS scale difference $\Delta \rho$ as powerful diagnostics:
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Scale Collapse ($\Delta \rho$):} Aggressive quantization (e.g., INT2) often clips activation outliers, shrinking the effective dynamic range ($\rho_Q < \rho_F$). Our bound identifies this as a primary source of risk amplification.
    \item \textbf{Structural Distortion ($\Omega$):} A drop in $\Omega$ indicates that the relative geometry between tokens has been corrupted—a phenomenon known as \emph{feature collapse}. 
\end{itemize}
By monitoring Eq.~(\ref{eq:quant_bound}) on a calibration set, practitioners can rank quantization configurations purely from feature statistics.

% 5.2 Geometric Monitoring of Catastrophic Forgetting
% -------------------------------------------------------------------------
\subsection{Geometric Monitoring of Catastrophic Forgetting}
\label{subsec:forgetting}

In sequential fine-tuning or continual learning, models frequently suffer from \emph{catastrophic forgetting}, where performance on the pre-training distribution ($\mathcal{D}_{\mathrm{pre}}$) degrades as the model adapts to a new task.
We propose monitoring the \emph{geometric drift} to mitigate this.

\paragraph{Geometric Instantiation ($T \to \theta_0, P \to \theta_t$).}
We instantiate our framework by defining the frozen base model as the Target ($T = \theta_0$) and the currently evolving model at step $t$ as the Proxy ($P = \theta_t$).
Accordingly, the feature matrices are denoted as $Z_0$ and $Z_t$. Unlike the quantization case, the optimal alignment $W$ is non-identity ($W \neq I$), as deep networks often undergo global feature rotations during adaptation.

\paragraph{Geometric Stability Bound.}
Applying the Unified Bound (Theorem~\ref{thm:unified_bound}), the forgetting magnitude (risk divergence on $\mathcal{D}_{\mathrm{pre}}$) is decomposed into:
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:forgetting_bound}
\begin{aligned}
\text{Forgetting}(t) \;\;\le\;\; & \underbrace{K_{\mathrm{feat}} \sqrt{ (\rho_0 - \rho_t)^2 + 2\rho_0 \rho_t \big( 1 - \Omega(Z_0, Z_t) \big) }}_{\text{\rm Backbone Geometric Drift}} \\
& + \underbrace{K_{\mathrm{pred}} \cdot \| \Sigma_t^{1/2} (W H_0 - H_t) \|_F}_{\text{\rm Head Divergence}}.
\end{aligned}
\end{equation}

\paragraph{Why Monitor Geometry?}
Equation~(\ref{eq:forgetting_bound}) reveals two sources of risk.
In Parameter-Efficient Fine-Tuning (PEFT) methods like LoRA, the head discrepancy is typically constrained by the low-rank update structure or explicitly frozen (where $H_t \approx H_0$, minimizing the second term).
However, even with constrained weights, the \emph{feature manifold} can undergo significant topological distortion (low $\Omega$) due to the deep non-linear accumulation of updates in the backbone.
This suggests that \textbf{Backbone Geometric Drift} is the primary driver of catastrophic forgetting when the head is stable.

\paragraph{The $\Omega$-Regularizer.}
Motivated by this, we propose a regularization strategy that explicitly targets the geometric term. We maximize the Procrustes Similarity on a small replay buffer:
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:omega_reg}
\mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{total}} = \mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{task}}(\theta_t) + \lambda \cdot \underbrace{\big(1 - \Omega(Z_0, Z_t)\big)}_{\mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{geom}}}.
\end{equation}
Unlike $L_2$ penalties on weights (e.g., EWC), $\mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{geom}}$ is permissive of global rotations (absorbed by $W$) and scale shifts. It allows the model to reorganize parameters for the new task, provided the intrinsic relational geometry of the pre-training knowledge is preserved.


% =========================================================================
\subsection{Out-of-Distribution (OOD) Detection via Geometric Consistency}
\label{subsec:ood}

Finally, we leverage the Linear Representation Hypothesis for anomaly detection. Our framework implies that Proxy and Target models form a \emph{consistent geometric view} only for In-Distribution (ID) data.

\paragraph{The Consistency Hypothesis.}
For an input $x \sim \mathcal{D}_{\mathrm{ID}}$, our theory guarantees minimal feature alignment error: $\| \phi_T(x) - \phi_P(x)W \|_2 \approx 0$.
However, for an anomaly $x' \sim \mathcal{D}_{\mathrm{OOD}}$, the shared semantic structure breaks down, causing the linear mapping $W$ (learned on ID data) to fail.

\paragraph{Detection Protocol.}
We define the \emph{Geometric Consistency Score} $s(x)$ as the alignment residual:
\begin{equation}
s(x) := \| \phi_T(x) - \phi_P(x)W \|_2.
\end{equation}
Crucially, our risk bound (Theorem~\ref{thm:unified_bound}) states that prediction risk is low \emph{only} when this term is small. Therefore, a high $s(x)$ serves as a theoretical indicator of unreliable prediction.
Unlike entropy-based methods that require the large model's logits, this method allows a small proxy to audit the large model's reliability purely through feature consistency.


% =========================================================================
\subsection{Cross-Scale Coefficient Transfer in Model Merging}
\label{subsec:merging}

We apply the framework to \emph{Model Merging}, specifically transferring optimal merging coefficients from a small proxy to a large target.

\paragraph{Problem Formulation.}
Consider merging a base model $\theta_{T,0}$ with a task vector $\Delta_T$, modulated by a scalar $\alpha$. The target parameter is $\theta_T(\alpha) = \theta_{T,0} + \alpha \Delta_T$. Tuning $\alpha$ on the target is expensive.
We propose searching for the optimal $\hat{\alpha}$ on a proxy model $\theta_P(\alpha) = \theta_{P,0} + \alpha \Delta_P$ and transferring it to the target.

\paragraph{Transfer Regret Bound.}
Let $\hat{\alpha}_P$ be the optimal coefficient found on the proxy. We utilize our Unified Bound (Theorem~\ref{thm:unified_bound}) to bound the regret on the target model.
Assuming geometric alignment holds uniformly across the search path $\alpha \in [0,1]$, the performance gap is bounded by:
\begin{equation}
\mathcal{R}_T(\hat{\alpha}_P) - \mathcal{R}_T(\alpha_T^*) \le 2 \left( K_{\mathrm{feat}} \sqrt{(\Delta \rho)^2 + 2\rho_T\rho_P(1-\Omega)} + \epsilon_{\mathrm{head}} \right) + 2\xi,
\end{equation}
where $\xi$ is the estimation error on the proxy.
This implies that if the Task Vectors $\Delta_T$ and $\Delta_P$ induce geometrically isomorphic trajectories ($\Omega \approx 1$), the "sweet spot" $\alpha$ is scale-invariant. This provides a rigorous guarantee for using small models to tune hyper-parameters for massive foundation models.

% 5.3 Generalization Forecasting for Fine-tuned Models
% -------------------------------------------------------------------------
\subsection{Generalization Forecasting via Geometric Control Variates}
\label{subsec:generalization_forecasting}

Finally, we address the evaluation bottleneck of massive fine-tuned models.
Let $\theta_T$ be the computation-heavy Target and $\theta_P$ be the lightweight Proxy.
Evaluating $\theta_T$ on the full test distribution $\mathcal{D}_{\mathrm{test}}$ is computationally expensive.
We propose using the Proxy as a \emph{Control Variate} to estimate the Target's generalization risk with minimal compute.

\paragraph{Theoretical Foundation: Variance Reduction via Unified Alignment.}
The reliability of using a proxy for risk forecasting depends not on the correlation of risks, but on the \emph{variance of the risk difference} $\Delta \ell(x) = \ell_T(x) - \ell_P(x)$.
Standard heuristics assume this variance is low without proof. Our PRISM framework provides the rigorous condition.
Recall our Unified Bound (Theorem~\ref{thm:unified_bound}) limits the expected magnitude of the functional discrepancy:
\begin{equation}
\mathbb{E}[\|f_T(x) - f_P(x)\|] \le \underbrace{K_{\mathrm{feat}}\sqrt{(\rho_T - \rho_P)^2 + 2\rho_T\rho_P(1-\Omega)}}_{\text{Geometric Alignment Error}} + \underbrace{K_{\mathrm{pred}}\|\Sigma_P^{1/2}(WH_T - H_P)\|_F}_{\text{Covariance-Aware Head Error}}.
\end{equation}
Crucially, a tight bound on the absolute magnitude of the difference implies a tight bound on its variance (by Popoviciu's inequality on bounded variables).
Thus, minimizing \emph{both} the feature misalignment ($1-\Omega$, $\Delta \rho$) and head covariance error guarantees that $\Delta \ell(x)$ is highly concentrated. This justifies treating the error as a stable "systematic bias" rather than random noise.

\paragraph{The Control Variate Estimator.}
Leveraging this variance suppression, we construct an unbiased estimator for the Target risk using a small calibration set $\mathcal{D}_{\mathrm{cal}}$:
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:cv_estimator}
\hat{\mathcal{R}}_T(\mathcal{D}_{\mathrm{test}}) = \mathcal{R}_P(\mathcal{D}_{\mathrm{test}}) + \underbrace{ \left( \mathcal{R}_T(\mathcal{D}_{\mathrm{cal}}) - \mathcal{R}_P(\mathcal{D}_{\mathrm{cal}}) \right) }_{\text{Calibrated Geometric Bias } \hat{\Delta}}.
\end{equation}
Here, $\mathcal{R}_P(\mathcal{D}_{\mathrm{test}})$ acts as the low-variance baseline (from the proxy), and $\hat{\Delta}$ corrects for the structural offset.

\paragraph{Protocol: The Geometric Validity Gate.}
To ensure rigor, we do not apply Eq.~(\ref{eq:cv_estimator}) blindly. We use the theoretical terms as a \emph{validity gate} to reject unreliable proxies:
\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{Measure Geometry:} On $\mathcal{D}_{\mathrm{cal}}$, compute the feature alignment $\Omega(Z_T, Z_P)$ and head consistency $\|\Sigma_P^{1/2}(WH_T - H_P)\|_F$.
    \item \textbf{Verify Stability:}
    \begin{itemize}
        \item \textbf{Pass:} If both terms are below a threshold $\tau$, the Unified Bound guarantees low variance. The empirical bias $\hat{\Delta}$ is reliable. Proceed with forecasting.
        \item \textbf{Reject:} If $\Omega$ is low or heads are misaligned, the bound is loose. The error variance is theoretically uncontrolled, making the small-sample bias estimate $\hat{\Delta}$ untrustworthy. Fallback to full evaluation.
    \end{itemize}
\end{enumerate}
This approach transforms the PRISM bound from a passive metric into an active criterion for sample-efficient evaluation, ensuring valid uncertainty quantification.

% 5.4 Cross-Family Capability Transfer
% -------------------------------------------------------------------------
\subsection{Cross-Family Capability Transfer}
\label{subsec:cross_family_transfer}

In our most general application, we consider the transferability of performance across completely heterogeneous systems.
Let $P$ and $T$ be two arbitrary models from distinct families (e.g., $P$ is Llama-3-8B, $T$ is Gemma-7B), parameterized by disjoint spaces $\theta_P$ and $\theta_T$.
We analyze the performance on a fixed dataset $\mathcal{D}$.
The central question is: \emph{If a dataset is "solvable" for the Proxy, under what conditions can we guarantee it is solvable for the Target?}

\paragraph{Formulation: The Transfer Inequality.}
Unlike the previous sections where models shared an architectural lineage, here $f_P$ and $f_T$ function as distinct distinct topological observers.
However, our Unified Bound (Theorem~\ref{thm:unified_bound}) remains valid provided a linear map $W$ exists.
We rewrite the bound to express the Target's risk as being strictly constrained by the Proxy's risk plus the geometric cost:
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:transfer_bound}
\mathcal{R}_T(\mathcal{D}) \;\;\le\;\; \mathcal{R}_P(\mathcal{D}) + \underbrace{K \cdot \sqrt{ (\Delta \rho)^2 + 2\rho_T \rho_P (1 - \Omega) } + \epsilon_{\mathrm{head}}}_{\text{Geometric Transfer Cost } \mathcal{C}_{\mathrm{geo}}}.
\end{equation}

\paragraph{Analysis: Performance Guarantee via Alignment.}
Equation~(\ref{eq:transfer_bound}) provides a rigorous condition for \emph{Capability Transfer}.
Assume the Proxy performs well on the dataset (i.e., $\mathcal{R}_P(\mathcal{D}) \approx 0$).
The performance of the Target is not guaranteed by architecture alone, but is bounded by the \emph{geometric isomorphism} $\Omega$:
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Aligned Regime ($\Omega \to 1$):} The Geometric Transfer Cost $\mathcal{C}_{\mathrm{geo}}$ vanishes. We can theoretically guarantee that $\mathcal{R}_T$ is low. This implies that "knowledge" or "capability" on this dataset is transferable between Llama and Gemma simply because they structure the data manifold identically.
    \item \textbf{Misaligned Regime ($\Omega \ll 1$):} The cost $\mathcal{C}_{\mathrm{geo}}$ is large. Even if Llama solves the task perfectly, the upper bound on Gemma is loose. This mathematically explains why some capabilities (e.g., specific coding styles) fail to transfer between model families despite similar parameter counts—they rely on divergent geometric structures.
\end{itemize}

\paragraph{Operational Utility: Architecture-Agnostic Validation.}
This analysis enables a powerful validation protocol.
If we observe that a Target model fails on a dataset where a Proxy succeeds ($\mathcal{R}_T \gg \mathcal{R}_P$), we can inspect $\Omega$.
\begin{itemize}
    \item If $\Omega$ is high, the failure is likely due to the \textbf{Head} ($\epsilon_{\mathrm{head}}$) or a specific bug, as the backbone features contain the necessary information.
    \item If $\Omega$ is low, the failure is fundamental; the Target's backbone has not learned the necessary semantic topology.
\end{itemize}
Thus, PRISM provides a mechanism to decouple "feature quality" from "model performance" across arbitrary architectures.

\section{Detailed Proofs and Derivations}
\label{app:detailed_proofs}

This appendix provides the complete, self-contained proof of the Unified Risk Bound (Theorem~\ref{thm:unified_bound}). We begin from the cross-entropy loss definition and proceed step by step through the risk decomposition, the Procrustes analysis, and the covariance-adjusted head bound.

\subsection{Step 1: Risk Decomposition via Triangle Inequality}
\label{app:risk_decomp}

\paragraph{Setup.}
Recall that the risk of model $M$ is $\mathcal{R}_M = \mathbb{E}_{(x,y)}[\ell(\phi_M(x) \cdot H_M, y)]$, where $\ell$ is the cross-entropy loss (Eq.~\ref{eq:ce_def}). Given the optimal orthogonal alignment $W^* \in \mathcal{O}(d)$ that maps proxy features toward target features (i.e., $\phi_T(x) \approx \phi_P(x) W^*$), we introduce an intermediate \emph{hybrid risk}:
\begin{equation}
\mathcal{R}_{P \to T} := \mathbb{E}_{(x,y)}\!\Big[\ell\!\big(\phi_P(x)\, W^* \cdot H_T,\; y\big)\Big].
\end{equation}
This hybrid term evaluates the \emph{target's head} $H_T$ on the \emph{proxy's aligned features} $\phi_P(x) W^*$, serving as a bridge between $\mathcal{R}_T$ and $\mathcal{R}_P$.

\paragraph{Decomposition.}
By the triangle inequality on absolute values:
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:app_triangle}
|\mathcal{R}_T - \mathcal{R}_P| \;\le\; \underbrace{\big|\mathcal{R}_T - \mathcal{R}_{P \to T}\big|}_{\text{Feature Alignment Error } \delta} \;+\; \underbrace{\big|\mathcal{R}_{P \to T} - \mathcal{R}_P\big|}_{\text{Head Discrepancy } \gamma}.
\end{equation}
The first term $\delta$ measures how much the \emph{features} differ (through the same head $H_T$); the second term $\gamma$ measures how much the \emph{heads} differ (on the same aligned features). We bound each term separately in the following subsections.


\subsection{Step 2: Bounding the Feature Alignment Error $\delta$}
\label{app:kfeat}

\paragraph{Lipschitz Property of Cross-Entropy with Respect to Features.}
We bound $\delta$ by showing that the cross-entropy loss is Lipschitz continuous in the feature vector $z$. Define the composition $g_T(z, y) := \ell(z \cdot H_T, y)$. Our goal is to find the tightest constant $K_{\mathrm{feat}}$ such that:
\begin{equation}
|g_T(z_1, y) - g_T(z_2, y)| \le K_{\mathrm{feat}} \|z_1 - z_2\|_2, \quad \forall z_1, z_2, y.
\end{equation}

\paragraph{Computing the Gradient.}
The logit vector is $v = z \cdot H_T \in \mathbb{R}^V$, and the cross-entropy loss is $\ell(v, y) = -v_y + \log \sum_{j=1}^{V} e^{v_j}$. The gradient with respect to the logits is:
\begin{equation}
\frac{\partial \ell}{\partial v} = \hat{p} - e_y,
\end{equation}
where $\hat{p} = \mathrm{softmax}(v) \in \mathbb{R}^V$ and $e_y$ is the one-hot vector at index $y$. Applying the chain rule to obtain the gradient with respect to features:
\begin{equation}
\nabla_z \ell = H_T (\hat{p} - e_y) = \sum_{j=1}^{V} (\hat{p}_j - \delta_{jy})\, h_{T,j},
\end{equation}
where $h_{T,j}$ denotes the $j$-th column of $H_T$ (the embedding vector for token $j$).

\paragraph{Simplex Polarization.}
We now derive a tight bound on $\|\nabla_z \ell\|_2$ by exploiting the probability simplex constraint $\sum_j \hat{p}_j = 1$. Expanding the gradient for true class $y$:
\begin{equation}
\nabla_z \ell = (\hat{p}_y - 1)\, h_{T,y} + \sum_{j \neq y} \hat{p}_j\, h_{T,j}.
\end{equation}
Since $1 - \hat{p}_y = \sum_{j \neq y} \hat{p}_j$, we can rewrite:
\begin{equation}
\nabla_z \ell = -\Big(\sum_{j \neq y} \hat{p}_j\Big) h_{T,y} + \sum_{j \neq y} \hat{p}_j\, h_{T,j} = \sum_{j \neq y} \hat{p}_j\, (h_{T,j} - h_{T,y}).
\end{equation}
This reveals that the gradient is a \emph{convex combination} of the pairwise differences between incorrect-class embeddings and the correct-class embedding. Taking the norm:
\begin{equation}
\|\nabla_z \ell\|_2 \le \sum_{j \neq y} \hat{p}_j \|h_{T,j} - h_{T,y}\|_2 \le \max_{j \neq y} \|h_{T,j} - h_{T,y}\|_2 \cdot \underbrace{\sum_{j \neq y} \hat{p}_j}_{\le\, 1}.
\end{equation}
Taking the maximum over all possible true classes $y$:
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:kfeat_tight}
K_{\mathrm{feat}} = \max_{j,k} \|h_{T,j} - h_{T,k}\|_2.
\end{equation}

\paragraph{Remark.} This bound depends on the \emph{relative distances} between token embeddings, not their absolute magnitudes. A uniform shift $H_T \to H_T + c\mathbf{1}^\top$ does not change $K_{\mathrm{feat}}$. A naive Cauchy--Schwarz bound gives $K_{\mathrm{feat}}^{\mathrm{naive}} = \sqrt{2}\|H_T\|_2$, which is substantially looser.

\paragraph{Applying the Lipschitz Bound.}
Using the Lipschitz property with $z_1 = \phi_T(x)$ and $z_2 = \phi_P(x) W^*$:
\begin{equation}
\begin{aligned}
\delta &= \big|\mathcal{R}_T - \mathcal{R}_{P \to T}\big| = \Big|\mathbb{E}\big[g_T(\phi_T(x), y) - g_T(\phi_P(x) W^*, y)\big]\Big| \\
&\le \mathbb{E}\Big[|g_T(\phi_T(x), y) - g_T(\phi_P(x) W^*, y)|\Big] \\
&\le K_{\mathrm{feat}} \cdot \mathbb{E}_x\!\big[\|\phi_T(x) - \phi_P(x) W^*\|_2\big].
\end{aligned}
\end{equation}

To connect this expectation to the Procrustes decomposition, we apply Jensen's inequality ($\mathbb{E}[\|X\|] \le \sqrt{\mathbb{E}[\|X\|^2]}$):
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:delta_bound}
\delta \le K_{\mathrm{feat}} \cdot \sqrt{\mathbb{E}_x\!\big[\|\phi_T(x) - \phi_P(x) W^*\|_2^2\big]} = K_{\mathrm{feat}} \cdot \sqrt{\frac{1}{n}\|Z_T - Z_P W^*\|_F^2}.
\end{equation}



\subsection{Step 3: Exact Geometric Decomposition via Procrustes Analysis}
\label{app:procrustes}

We now derive the closed-form solution for the Orthogonal Procrustes problem and prove Proposition~\ref{prop:exact_decomposition}.

\paragraph{The Optimization Problem.}
We seek the orthogonal matrix $W^*$ that minimizes the alignment residual:
\begin{equation}
W^* = \arg\min_{W \in \mathcal{O}(d)} \|Z_T - Z_P W\|_F^2.
\end{equation}

\paragraph{Expansion.} Expanding the squared Frobenius norm:
\begin{equation}
\begin{aligned}
\|Z_T - Z_P W\|_F^2 &= \operatorname{Tr}\!\big[(Z_T - Z_P W)^\top (Z_T - Z_P W)\big] \\
&= \|Z_T\|_F^2 + \|Z_P W\|_F^2 - 2\operatorname{Tr}(Z_T^\top Z_P W).
\end{aligned}
\end{equation}
Since $W$ is orthogonal, it preserves the Frobenius norm: $\|Z_P W\|_F^2 = \operatorname{Tr}(W^\top Z_P^\top Z_P W) = \operatorname{Tr}(Z_P^\top Z_P) = \|Z_P\|_F^2$, where we used the cyclic property of trace and $W^\top W = I_d$. Therefore:
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:procrustes_expand}
\|Z_T - Z_P W\|_F^2 = \|Z_T\|_F^2 + \|Z_P\|_F^2 - 2\operatorname{Tr}(Z_T^\top Z_P W).
\end{equation}
The first two terms are constants independent of $W$. Minimization thus reduces to:
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:procrustes_max}
W^* = \arg\max_{W \in \mathcal{O}(d)} \operatorname{Tr}(Z_T^\top Z_P W).
\end{equation}

\paragraph{SVD Solution.}
Let $Z_T^\top Z_P = U \Sigma V^\top$ be the singular value decomposition, where $U, V \in \mathbb{R}^{d \times d}$ are orthogonal and $\Sigma = \mathrm{diag}(\sigma_1, \ldots, \sigma_d)$ with $\sigma_i \ge 0$. Substituting:
\begin{equation}
\operatorname{Tr}(Z_T^\top Z_P W) = \operatorname{Tr}(U \Sigma V^\top W) = \operatorname{Tr}(\Sigma V^\top W U) = \operatorname{Tr}(\Sigma R),
\end{equation}
where $R = V^\top W U$. Since $W \in \mathcal{O}(d)$ and $U, V \in \mathcal{O}(d)$, we have $R \in \mathcal{O}(d)$. The problem becomes:
\begin{equation}
\max_{R \in \mathcal{O}(d)} \operatorname{Tr}(\Sigma R) = \max_{R \in \mathcal{O}(d)} \sum_{i=1}^{d} \sigma_i R_{ii}.
\end{equation}
Since $\sigma_i \ge 0$ and $|R_{ii}| \le 1$ for any orthogonal $R$, this is maximized when $R_{ii} = 1$ for all $i$, i.e., $R = I_d$, which yields $V^\top W^* U = I_d$ and thus:
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:W_optimal}
W^* = V U^\top.
\end{equation}
The maximum value is:
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:max_trace}
\max_{W \in \mathcal{O}(d)} \operatorname{Tr}(Z_T^\top Z_P W) = \sum_{i=1}^{d} \sigma_i = \|Z_T^\top Z_P\|_*,
\end{equation}
where $\|\cdot\|_*$ denotes the nuclear norm.

\paragraph{Substitution and Decomposition.}
Substituting Eq.~(\ref{eq:max_trace}) into Eq.~(\ref{eq:procrustes_expand}) and dividing by $n$:
\begin{equation}
\min_{W \in \mathcal{O}(d)} \frac{1}{n}\|Z_T - Z_P W\|_F^2 = \frac{1}{n}\|Z_T\|_F^2 + \frac{1}{n}\|Z_P\|_F^2 - \frac{2}{n}\|Z_T^\top Z_P\|_*.
\end{equation}
Recalling $\rho_M = \frac{1}{\sqrt{n}}\|Z_M\|_F$ (so $\frac{1}{n}\|Z_M\|_F^2 = \rho_M^2$) and the definition of $\Omega$ (Eq.~\ref{eq:omega_def}):
\begin{equation}
\frac{1}{n}\|Z_T^\top Z_P\|_* = \frac{\|Z_T^\top Z_P\|_*}{\|Z_T\|_F\|Z_P\|_F} \cdot \frac{\|Z_T\|_F\|Z_P\|_F}{n} = \Omega \cdot \rho_T \rho_P.
\end{equation}
Therefore:
\begin{equation}
\min_{W \in \mathcal{O}(d)} \frac{1}{n}\|Z_T - Z_P W\|_F^2 = \rho_T^2 + \rho_P^2 - 2\,\Omega\,\rho_T\rho_P.
\end{equation}
Adding and subtracting $2\rho_T\rho_P$ completes the square:
\begin{equation}
= (\rho_T^2 - 2\rho_T\rho_P + \rho_P^2) + 2\rho_T\rho_P - 2\,\Omega\,\rho_T\rho_P = \underbrace{(\rho_T - \rho_P)^2}_{\text{Scale Mismatch}} + \underbrace{2\rho_T\rho_P(1 - \Omega)}_{\text{Shape Mismatch}}.
\end{equation}
This completes the proof of Proposition~\ref{prop:exact_decomposition}. \qed

\paragraph{Assembling the Feature Bound.}
Combining Eq.~(\ref{eq:delta_bound}) with Proposition~\ref{prop:exact_decomposition}:
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:delta_final}
\delta \le K_{\mathrm{feat}} \sqrt{(\rho_T - \rho_P)^2 + 2\rho_T\rho_P(1 - \Omega)}.
\end{equation}



\subsection{Step 4: Bounding the Head Discrepancy $\gamma$}
\label{app:head_bound}

\paragraph{Lipschitz Property with Respect to Logits.}
The head discrepancy measures how much the predictions differ when two different heads operate on the same aligned features. Let $z = \phi_P(x) W^*$ be the aligned proxy feature. Then:
\begin{equation}
\gamma = \big|\mathcal{R}_{P \to T} - \mathcal{R}_P\big| = \Big|\mathbb{E}\!\big[\ell(z \cdot H_T, y) - \ell(z \cdot H_P, y)\big]\Big|.
\end{equation}
To bound this, we use the Lipschitz property of cross-entropy with respect to \emph{logits}. The gradient of $\ell$ with respect to $v$ is $\nabla_v \ell = \hat{p} - e_y$, with norm:
\begin{equation}
\|\nabla_v \ell\|_2 = \|\hat{p} - e_y\|_2.
\end{equation}
The worst case occurs when $\hat{p}$ places all mass on an incorrect class: $\hat{p}_j = 1$ for some $j \neq y$. Then $\|\hat{p} - e_y\|_2 = \sqrt{1^2 + (-1)^2} = \sqrt{2}$. Thus $K_{\mathrm{pred}} \le \sqrt{2}$.

\paragraph{Deriving the Covariance Projection.}
Define the head misalignment matrix $\Delta H = W^* H_T - H_P \in \mathbb{R}^{d \times V}$. The logit difference for a single sample is $z \cdot \Delta H$, and:
\begin{equation}
\begin{aligned}
\gamma &\le \mathbb{E}\!\big[|\ell(z \cdot H_T, y) - \ell(z \cdot H_P, y)|\big] \\
&\le K_{\mathrm{pred}} \cdot \mathbb{E}_z\!\big[\|z \cdot \Delta H\|_2\big].
\end{aligned}
\end{equation}
Applying Jensen's inequality on the concave square root:
\begin{equation}
\big(\mathbb{E}[\|z \cdot \Delta H\|_2]\big)^2 \le \mathbb{E}\!\big[\|z \cdot \Delta H\|_2^2\big].
\end{equation}
Expanding the squared norm using the trace operator:
\begin{equation}
\begin{aligned}
\mathbb{E}\!\big[\|z \cdot \Delta H\|_2^2\big] &= \mathbb{E}\!\Big[\operatorname{Tr}\!\big(\Delta H^\top z^\top z\, \Delta H\big)\Big] \\
&= \operatorname{Tr}\!\Big(\Delta H^\top \underbrace{\mathbb{E}[z^\top z]}_{\Sigma_P} \Delta H\Big) \\
&= \|\Sigma_P^{1/2} \Delta H\|_F^2.
\end{aligned}
\end{equation}
Taking the square root:
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:gamma_final}
\gamma \le K_{\mathrm{pred}} \cdot \|\Sigma_P^{1/2}(W^* H_T - H_P)\|_F.
\end{equation}

\paragraph{Interpretation.} The covariance weighting $\Sigma_P^{1/2}$ projects the head error onto the \emph{active subspace} of the data. If the heads disagree only in directions orthogonal to the feature distribution (the null space of $\Sigma_P$), this disagreement has zero cost---it does not affect any prediction. This is strictly tighter than the spectral bound $\gamma \le K_{\mathrm{pred}} \|W^* H_T - H_P\|_2 \cdot \mathbb{E}[\|z\|_2]$, which assumes worst-case alignment between features and head error.


\subsection{Step 5: Assembling the Unified Bound}
\label{app:assembly}

Combining the risk decomposition (Eq.~\ref{eq:app_triangle}), the feature bound (Eq.~\ref{eq:delta_final}), and the head bound (Eq.~\ref{eq:gamma_final}):
\begin{equation}
\begin{aligned}
|\mathcal{R}_T - \mathcal{R}_P| &\le \delta + \gamma \\
&\le K_{\mathrm{feat}} \sqrt{(\rho_T - \rho_P)^2 + 2\rho_T\rho_P(1 - \Omega)} + K_{\mathrm{pred}} \cdot \|\Sigma_P^{1/2}(W^* H_T - H_P)\|_F.
\end{aligned}
\end{equation}
This completes the proof of Theorem~\ref{thm:unified_bound}. \qed



\section{Tightness of Nuclear Norm over Frobenius Norm}
\label{app:tightness}

We show that the nuclear-norm-based Procrustes Similarity $\Omega$ yields a strictly tighter alignment residual than any Frobenius-based metric (such as CKA).

\paragraph{Statement.}
Define the Frobenius-based similarity $\Omega_F := \|Z_T^\top Z_P\|_F / (\|Z_T\|_F \|Z_P\|_F)$. For any feature matrices $Z_T, Z_P$:
\begin{equation}
\Omega_F \le \Omega,
\end{equation}
with equality if and only if the cross-covariance matrix $Z_T^\top Z_P$ has rank 1 (i.e., all singular values are equal to one nonzero value).

\paragraph{Proof.}
Let $\sigma_1, \ldots, \sigma_d$ be the singular values of $Z_T^\top Z_P$. Then:
\begin{equation}
\|Z_T^\top Z_P\|_F = \sqrt{\sum_i \sigma_i^2}, \qquad \|Z_T^\top Z_P\|_* = \sum_i \sigma_i.
\end{equation}
By the Cauchy--Schwarz inequality applied to the vectors $(\sigma_1, \ldots, \sigma_d)$ and $(1, \ldots, 1)$:
\begin{equation}
\|Z_T^\top Z_P\|_F = \sqrt{\sum_i \sigma_i^2} \le \sum_i \sigma_i = \|Z_T^\top Z_P\|_*,
\end{equation}
since $\|\mathbf{x}\|_1 \ge \|\mathbf{x}\|_2$ for any non-negative vector. Dividing both sides by $\|Z_T\|_F \|Z_P\|_F$ gives $\Omega_F \le \Omega$.

\paragraph{Consequence for Risk Bounding.}
The alignment residual using $\Omega_F$ is:
\begin{equation}
(\rho_T - \rho_P)^2 + 2\rho_T\rho_P(1 - \Omega_F) \ge (\rho_T - \rho_P)^2 + 2\rho_T\rho_P(1 - \Omega).
\end{equation}
Therefore, substituting $\Omega_F$ into the unified bound produces a \emph{strictly looser} upper bound on risk discrepancy. The nuclear norm $\Omega$ is the tightest possible metric because it exactly solves the Procrustes optimization---any other norm-based similarity either underestimates the alignment or fails to correspond to a well-defined geometric minimization.
