\begin{abstract}
Comparing Large Language Model (LLM) variants---quantized checkpoints, fine-tuned snapshots, LoRA adaptations---faces a diagnostic gap: benchmarks report only an overall accuracy score that flags whether a variant has degraded but cannot say \emph{why}, and existing representational similarity metrics (e.g., CKA) add no formal link to downstream performance, reducing all geometric drift to a single opaque number.
We propose \textbf{PRISM} (Proxy Risk Inference via Structural Mapping), a framework that bounds the cross-entropy risk gap between a target model and its proxy along three independently measurable axes, each computable from a single forward pass: a scale mismatch $\Delta\rho$ and a shape mismatch $1-\Omega$ obtained jointly from an exact Orthogonal Procrustes decomposition---with $\Omega$ a nuclear-norm similarity strictly tighter than Frobenius surrogates such as CKA---and a head-discrepancy term weighted by where the data actually lives.
We instantiate the framework for post-training quantization (GPTQ, GGUF, BitsAndBytes), where the bound reduces to a pure geometric distortion measure, and for LoRA fine-tuning, where it isolates catastrophic forgetting in the backbone geometry. Because its trace-form variant $\Omega_I$ is differentiable, the same quantity lifts into a training-time \emph{shape regularizer} that empirically suppresses downstream forgetting without impairing target-task learning.
Across four 8B-scale LLM lineages (Llama, Qwen, Ministral, DeepSeek) and five benchmarks, the bound ranks variant-level degradation with strong Spearman correlation under both quantization and LoRA forgetting, and its three axes localize distinct failure modes---shape distortion under low-bit quantization, scale collapse under lineage-specific GPTQ calibration, and head divergence under quantized output embeddings---turning a single-number similarity into a mechanism-level diagnostic whose ranking can be trusted and whose failures can be read off directly.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}

\paragraph{The diagnostic gap in the post-training LLM lifecycle.}
As large language models have moved from research prototypes to production defaults, a new engineering bottleneck has emerged downstream of pre-training.
A single base checkpoint now spawns a range of post-training variants---quantized at multiple bit-widths (GPTQ~\cite{frantar2022gptq}, GGUF, BitsAndBytes~\cite{dettmers2022gpt3}), adapted via LoRA~\cite{hu2022lora} for different downstream tasks, and distilled under different deployment envelopes---each of which must be independently compared, tuned, and iterated on before a deployed model is selected~\cite{grattafiori2024llama, qwen2025qwen3}.
Across these variants, the only signal evaluation routinely provides is an \emph{overall accuracy score} per benchmark---a single number that flags whether a variant has degraded but says nothing about \emph{why}, or which compression knob to turn next---and at this multiplicity, an un-diagnosable accuracy drop forces practitioners into blind trial-and-error rather than targeted correction.
An accuracy drop could equally trace to activation-magnitude collapse from outlier channels, to structural distortion of the feature manifold, or to quantization of the output embedding---three mechanisms each demanding a different remediation.
What the field needs is therefore not a cheaper accuracy number, but a diagnostic whose ranking can be trusted, whose mechanism can be inspected, and whose signal can be trained against.

\paragraph{Similarity scores without risk, without mechanism.}
The natural shortcut is to borrow a representational similarity metric and declare two models ``close enough.''
A decade of internal-representation analysis has produced exactly this---SVCCA~\cite{raghu2017svcca}, linear and kernel CKA~\cite{kornblith2019similarity}, and a broader family of Frobenius-norm statistics---each reducing two activation matrices to a single alignment score.
Yet these scores do not pin down downstream behavior: a quantized model can read as highly similar and still lose accuracy, while a fine-tuned model can read as drifted and still retain task performance~\cite{ding2021grounding, davari2023reliability}, and the recent survey of~\cite{klabunde2023similarity} marks the link between representational and functional similarity as an open problem.
And even where they do correlate, a single-number similarity is no less opaque than the accuracy verdict above---it flags drift without localizing it to an axis that diagnosis could act on.
What is missing is a structural insight that lifts a geometric quantity into a functional risk bound \emph{while preserving the causal decomposition that diagnosis requires}.

\paragraph{What makes LLM geometry analytically tractable.}
That insight exists, and it is structural to modern LLMs.
Every current Transformer language model factors cleanly into a deep non-linear backbone followed by a single linear prediction head (the \texttt{lm\_head}) that projects features onto the vocabulary; there is no additional non-linear classifier between features and logits.
The Linear Representation Hypothesis~\cite{moschella2022relative, park2023linear} further provides that models within a common lineage---a base and its quantized or fine-tuned descendants---learn feature manifolds related by a near-isometric linear map.
These two facts, co-occurring, are what make the geometry formally tractable: the feature-alignment residual under an Orthogonal Procrustes problem can be lifted \emph{exactly} into an upper bound on the cross-entropy risk gap, with no ad-hoc constants and no black-box terms.
It is precisely because LLMs uniquely satisfy this structural recipe---a frozen linear head under standard LoRA, dominant outlier-channel scale collapse under PTQ~\cite{dettmers2022gpt3, xiao2023smoothquant}, and a shared cross-entropy scoring rule across both multiple-choice and generative benchmarks---that we instantiate the framework exclusively on them.
The generality of the method is mathematical; the leverage is architectural.

\paragraph{PRISM: a bound with three actionable axes.}
We propose \textbf{PRISM} (Proxy Risk Inference via Structural Mapping), a framework that turns this structural insight into a bound-certified, mechanism-level diagnostic for LLM variants (Fig.~\ref{fig:prism_concept}).
Starting from the Procrustes alignment residual, we prove an \emph{exact} decomposition of the feature error into two orthogonal quantities: a \emph{scale mismatch} $\Delta\rho$ that isolates activation-magnitude collapse, and a \emph{shape mismatch} $1-\Omega$ that isolates distortion of the feature manifold, with $\Omega$ a nuclear-norm similarity strictly tighter than Frobenius surrogates such as CKA (Appendix~\ref{app:tightness}).
A third term---a head discrepancy, weighted by where the data actually lives---closes the bound.
Because each of these three quantities is individually measurable from a single forward pass, a PRISM failure is not merely a flag but a \emph{direction}: a scale-dominated failure points to outlier-channel clipping and argues for per-channel scaling~\cite{xiao2023smoothquant}; a shape-dominated failure indicts the compression's Hessian approximation and argues for second-order methods such as GPTQ; a head-dominated failure localizes the loss to quantized output embeddings and argues for keeping the head in FP16.
In our primary LLM regimes the optimal alignment is near-identity, so all experiments report a differentiable trace-form surrogate of $\Omega$ (denoted $\Omega_I$) that remains a conservative upper bound on the nuclear form and is autograd-compatible for gradient-based use in training.
Across four 8B-scale LLM lineages (Llama, Qwen, Ministral, DeepSeek) and five benchmarks, the three axes track empirical cross-entropy degradation with strong Spearman rank correlation under both post-training quantization and LoRA fine-tuning, making PRISM a diagnostic whose ordering can be trusted and whose mechanism can be read off directly.

\paragraph{From diagnostic to training signal.}
The same quantity that reads a variant's drift can also prevent it.
Under standard LoRA fine-tuning the prediction head is frozen, the head term of the bound vanishes by construction, and the risk gap is governed entirely by backbone scale and shape drift---a quantity that, in its trace form $\Omega_I$, is smooth in the current features and therefore fully autograd-compatible.
Adding a weighted penalty on $1-\Omega_I$ to the fine-tuning objective therefore lifts the bound from a post-hoc lens into an active geometric constraint against catastrophic forgetting during training.
Empirically, even a weak penalty weight ($\lambda{=}0.1$) eliminates a substantial fraction of downstream forgetting on ARC, MMLU, SQuAD, TriviaQA, and GSM8K without impairing target-task fit; increasing $\lambda$ strengthens preservation monotonically, giving a bound-motivated knob for the forgetting--learning trade-off.
Because the penalty is computed on a 32-sequence reference set disjoint from the fine-tuning data, no labels on the held-out benchmarks are ever needed---geometry alone suffices.

\paragraph{Contributions.}
Our contributions are threefold.
\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{An exact scale--shape decomposition that turns geometric similarity into actionable coordinates.} We prove that the Orthogonal Procrustes residual on a pair of feature matrices decomposes \emph{exactly} into a scale-mismatch arm and a shape-mismatch arm, promoting the two into independently measurable diagnostic coordinates, with the shape arm governed by a nuclear-norm similarity $\Omega$ strictly tighter than Frobenius surrogates such as CKA (Appendix~\ref{app:tightness}). Combined with a covariance-weighted head-discrepancy term, the decomposition lifts Procrustes alignment into a closed-form upper bound on the cross-entropy risk gap (Theorem~\ref{thm:unified_bound}) whose three axes---scale collapse, structural distortion, and head divergence---each carry a distinct operational reading that a single-number similarity cannot provide.

    \item \textbf{A unified framework across the LLM post-training lifecycle, usable both diagnostically and as a training objective.} The bound reduces to a pure geometric distortion measure under post-training quantization (GPTQ, GGUF, BitsAndBytes) and isolates catastrophic forgetting in backbone drift under LoRA with a frozen head; because its trace-form variant $\Omega_I$ is differentiable, the same quantity lifts into a shape regularizer that actively suppresses forgetting during fine-tuning (Sec.~\ref{subsec:shape_reg}).

    \item \textbf{Rank-consistent empirical validation with three-axis failure-mode localization.} Across four 8B-scale LLM lineages (Llama, Qwen, Ministral, DeepSeek) and five benchmarks (ARC, MMLU, SQuAD, TriviaQA, GSM8K), the bound ranks variant-level degradation with strong Spearman correlation under both quantization and LoRA forgetting, and its three axes localize the documented failure modes of each compression family: shape distortion under low-bit quantization, scale collapse under lineage-specific GPTQ calibration, and head divergence under quantized output embeddings. Shape regularization reproduces these rank-consistent gains during training---a mild penalty weight reduces forgetting on every held-out benchmark without impairing target-task learning.
\end{enumerate}

\section{Related Work}

\paragraph{Representational Similarity and Procrustes Shape Metrics.}
A long line of Frobenius-norm similarity statistics---SVCCA~\cite{raghu2017svcca}, linear and kernel CKA~\cite{kornblith2019similarity}---measures geometric resemblance between activation matrices, but empirical audits~\cite{ding2021grounding, davari2023reliability} and the recent survey of~\cite{klabunde2023similarity} establish that these scores do not pin down downstream behavior. The closest mathematical precedent to our formulation is the generalized shape metrics framework~\cite{williams2021generalized}, which defines a proper metric space via the Orthogonal Procrustes distance and whose squared form already implicitly carries the nuclear norm $\|Z_T^\top Z_P\|_*$ that appears in our $\Omega$; a recent decodability analysis~\cite{harvey2024what} uses this shape distance on \emph{whitened} kernel matrices to bound the expected distance between freshly-optimized linear readouts. The Platonic Representation Hypothesis~\cite{huh2024platonic} conjectures shared representational geometry across large models on empirical grounds but stops short of a formal bound, and the Linear Representation Hypothesis~\cite{moschella2022relative, park2023linear} justifies restricting alignments to the orthogonal group. Our framework is complementary: rather than a new similarity or bounding readouts on whitened features, we (a)~split the Procrustes residual into \emph{separately} measurable scale $(\Delta\rho)^2$ and shape $2\rho_T\rho_P(1-\Omega)$ coordinates, (b)~lift it into a cross-entropy risk bound on two models using their \emph{own deployed} prediction heads on raw features, and (c)~add a covariance-weighted head term that vanishes exactly under frozen-head LLM protocols (LoRA, FP16-head PTQ).

\paragraph{Post-Training Quantization and Its Evaluation.}
Post-training quantization is the dominant deployment path for LLMs under memory constraints: GPTQ~\cite{frantar2022gptq} uses approximate second-order information for layer-wise reconstruction, while round-to-nearest methods in GGUF and BitsAndBytes~\cite{dettmers2022gpt3} offer simpler but less controlled compression, and the activation-outlier phenomena that drive their degradation are well documented~\cite{xiao2023smoothquant}. Assessing how much a quantized variant has degraded still requires running full perplexity or accuracy evaluations; existing cheap diagnostics---layer-wise reconstruction loss, weight quantization error---are indirect proxies that do not account for nonlinear error accumulation through the full network. PRISM closes this gap with a generation-free geometric decomposition: scale collapse, structural distortion, and head divergence are each measured from a single forward pass, localizing which axis carries the degradation and hence which remediation (per-channel outlier smoothing, Hessian-aware reconstruction, or FP16-head retention) the variant needs.

\paragraph{Catastrophic Forgetting under Parameter-Efficient Fine-Tuning.}
When LLMs are fine-tuned via low-rank adapters~\cite{hu2022lora} they risk catastrophic forgetting of pre-training knowledge, even though the \texttt{lm\_head} typically remains frozen. Classical weight-space remedies such as EWC~\cite{kirkpatrick2017overcoming} and replay-based methods penalize parameter movement rigidly, ignoring whether the underlying feature geometry is preserved; a recent geometric analysis~\cite{steele2026subspace} localizes LoRA forgetting in \emph{gradient}-subspace angles, fitting the empirical law $F = \alpha(1-\cos^2\theta_{\min}) + \beta$. Our framework shifts the locus to \emph{hidden-state} geometry: we monitor forgetting through drift of the feature manifold ($\Omega_I$, $\Delta\rho$) relative to the frozen base model---a drift that naturally permits global rotations and rescalings with no effect on downstream risk---and, crucially, \emph{bounds} rather than fits the risk gap. Because LoRA's frozen head makes the head-divergence term vanish, this drift becomes a label-free diagnostic directly usable both post-hoc and as a training-time regularizer.

\paragraph{Efficient Evaluation and Performance Forecasting.}
Two existing lines attempt to reduce the cost of evaluating LLM variants: scaling laws~\cite{kaplan2020scaling, hoffmann2022training} predict aggregate loss as a power-law function of compute and data but cannot capture variant-specific degradation, while item-response-theory subsets such as tinyBenchmarks~\cite{polo2024tinybenchmarks} and the ranking-reliability framework of~\cite{perlitz2024efficient} curate representative subsamples that preserve orderings but offer no formal link between representational change and expected accuracy shift; weak-to-strong generalization~\cite{burns2023weak} targets label generation rather than risk estimation. PRISM takes a geometry-grounded route: the bound is computed directly from the model's representational geometry, ranks variants with strong Spearman correlation, and---distinctively---decomposes each variant's degradation into three diagnostic axes, something an accuracy-preserving subset cannot provide.

\section{A Geometric Framework for Proxy Performance Estimation}
\label{sec:geometric_foundation}

We derive a closed-form upper bound on the cross-entropy risk gap $|\mathcal{R}_T - \mathcal{R}_P|$ between a target model and a proxy, grounded entirely in their feature geometry. We first state the setup, then prove an exact decomposition of the bound into three diagnostic axes (scale, shape, head), explain the operational form ($\Omega_I$ at $W{=}I$) used throughout our experiments, extend the bound to autoregressive generation, and finally lift it into a training-time regularizer.

\subsection{Setup}
\label{subsec:problem_setup}

We consider a \emph{Target model} $T$ (e.g., a full-precision base) and a \emph{Proxy model} $P$ (e.g., a quantized or fine-tuned variant) with shared hidden dimension $d$. Each model factors into a Transformer backbone $\phi_M: \mathcal{X} \to \mathbb{R}^d$ followed by a linear prediction head $H_M \in \mathbb{R}^{d \times V}$ (the \texttt{lm\_head}):
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:model_decomp}
f_M(x) = \mathrm{softmax}\!\big(\phi_M(x) \cdot H_M\big), \qquad M \in \{T, P\}.
\end{equation}
The risk of $M$ under data distribution $\mathcal{D}$ is the cross-entropy expectation
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:ce_def}
\mathcal{R}_M \;=\; \mathbb{E}_{(x,y) \sim \mathcal{D}}\!\big[\ell\big(\phi_M(x) H_M,\, y\big)\big], \qquad \ell(v, y) = -v_y + \log \textstyle\sum_{j} e^{v_j}.
\end{equation}
Following the \emph{Linear Representation Hypothesis}~\cite{moschella2022relative, park2023linear}, we assume the two backbones lie in a common lineage: there exists an orthogonal $W \in \mathcal{O}(d)$ such that $\phi_T(x) \approx \phi_P(x)\, W$ for all $x$. On a calibration sample of size $n$, we collect features into $Z_T, Z_P \in \mathbb{R}^{n \times d}$, denote the columns of $H_M$ by $h_{M,1},\ldots,h_{M,V}$, and define the RMS feature scale $\rho_M = \|Z_M\|_F / \sqrt{n}$ and the empirical second-moment matrix $\Sigma_P = Z_P^\top Z_P / n$.


\subsection{The Unified Risk Bound}
\label{subsec:unified_bound}

To bound $|\mathcal{R}_T - \mathcal{R}_P|$ we introduce a hybrid risk $\mathcal{R}_{P \to T} := \mathbb{E}[\ell(\phi_P(x)\,W \cdot H_T, y)]$ that evaluates the target's head on the proxy's aligned features. The triangle inequality splits the gap into a \emph{feature error} $\delta := |\mathcal{R}_T - \mathcal{R}_{P\to T}|$ and a \emph{head error} $\gamma := |\mathcal{R}_{P\to T} - \mathcal{R}_P|$; each admits a closed-form bound whose full derivation is in Appendix~\ref{app:detailed_proofs}.

\paragraph{Feature error $\delta$.}
The cross-entropy loss is Lipschitz in $z$ with constant $K_{\mathrm{feat}} = \max_{j,k} \|h_{T,j} - h_{T,k}\|_2$, the simplex diameter of $H_T$'s token embeddings (Appendix~\ref{app:kfeat}). Minimizing the alignment residual $\|Z_T - Z_P W\|_F^2$ over $W \in \mathcal{O}(d)$ via the classical Orthogonal Procrustes problem yields an \emph{exact identity}---the paper's central mathematical result.

\begin{proposition}[Exact Scale--Shape Decomposition]
\label{prop:exact_decomposition}
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:exact_equality}
\min_{W \in \mathcal{O}(d)} \frac{1}{n} \| Z_T - Z_P W \|_F^2
\;=\;
\underbrace{(\rho_T - \rho_P)^2}_{\text{\rm Scale Mismatch } (\Delta\rho)^2}
\;+\;
\underbrace{2 \rho_T \rho_P \big( 1 - \Omega \big)}_{\text{\rm Shape Mismatch}},
\end{equation}
where $\Omega(Z_T, Z_P)$ is the \emph{Procrustes Similarity} defined via the nuclear norm of the cross-moment:
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:omega_def}
\Omega(Z_T, Z_P) \;:=\; \frac{\| Z_T^\top Z_P \|_*}{\| Z_T \|_F\, \| Z_P \|_F} \;\in [0,\, 1].
\end{equation}
\end{proposition}

The two arms are operationally distinct: the scale term isolates activation-magnitude collapse, the shape term captures structural distortion of the feature manifold. The Procrustes residual itself is classical~\cite{williams2021generalized}; our contribution is this explicit split and its lifting into a functional-risk bound---see Appendix~\ref{app:detailed_proofs} for the relation to the classical shape-metric literature. Combining the identity with the Lipschitz bound gives $\delta \le K_{\mathrm{feat}}\sqrt{(\rho_T - \rho_P)^2 + 2\rho_T\rho_P(1-\Omega)}$, with $\Omega$ strictly tighter than Frobenius-based surrogates such as CKA (Appendix~\ref{app:tightness}).

\paragraph{Head error $\gamma$.}
Writing $\Delta H := W^* H_T - H_P$ for the head misalignment, one has $\gamma \le K_{\mathrm{pred}}\,\|\Sigma_P^{1/2}\Delta H\|_F$ with $K_{\mathrm{pred}} \le \sqrt{2}$ (Appendix~\ref{app:head_bound}). The covariance weighting $\Sigma_P^{1/2}$ ensures that only head misalignment within the \emph{active subspace} of the data contributes.

\begin{theorem}[Unified Risk Bound]
\label{thm:unified_bound}
Let $W^* \in \mathcal{O}(d)$ be the optimal Procrustes alignment. Then
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:unified_bound_eq}
|\mathcal{R}_T - \mathcal{R}_P| \;\le\; \underbrace{K_{\mathrm{feat}} \sqrt{ (\rho_T - \rho_P)^2 + 2\rho_T \rho_P \big(1 - \Omega(Z_T, Z_P)\big) }}_{\text{\rm Geometric Alignment Error}} \;+\; \underbrace{K_{\mathrm{pred}} \,\| \Sigma_P^{1/2} (W^* H_T - H_P) \|_F}_{\text{\rm Second-Moment-Weighted Head Error}}.
\end{equation}
\end{theorem}


\subsection{Three Diagnostic Axes and the Operational Form $\Omega_I$}
\label{subsec:interpretation}

Theorem~\ref{thm:unified_bound} decomposes the risk gap along three \emph{independently measurable} axes, each attached to a distinct failure mode of the proxy.

\paragraph{Scale mismatch $\Delta\rho$.}
Divergence in activation magnitude directly amplifies the feature error. Aggressive bit-width reduction clips activation outliers~\cite{dettmers2022gpt3, xiao2023smoothquant}, shrinking $\rho_P < \rho_T$; the scale axis isolates this \emph{scale collapse}.

\paragraph{Shape mismatch $1-\Omega$.}
The shape term captures distortion of the feature manifold up to rotation. A drop in $\Omega$ signals that the relative arrangement of token representations has been corrupted---what we term \emph{structural distortion}.

\paragraph{Head divergence $\|\Sigma_P^{1/2}(W^* H_T - H_P)\|_F$.}
The head term quantifies how differently the two prediction heads interpret aligned features, weighted by $\Sigma_P$ so that only directions along which the data actually lives can contribute. Under standard LoRA fine-tuning~\cite{hu2022lora} with a frozen \texttt{lm\_head} ($H_T = H_P$), this term vanishes entirely, isolating all performance change in backbone geometry.

\paragraph{Operational form: why $W = I$ throughout this paper.}
All experiments in Sec.~\ref{sec:experiments} and the shape regularizer of Sec.~\ref{subsec:shape_reg} report the identity-alignment \emph{trace specialization}
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:omega_I_def}
\Omega_I(Z_T, Z_P) \;:=\; \frac{\operatorname{Tr}(Z_T^\top Z_P)}{\|Z_T\|_F\,\|Z_P\|_F},
\end{equation}
which sets $W = I$ in the Procrustes decomposition. Three properties justify this operational choice. \textbf{(i)~Structural match.} Both primary instantiations preserve the \texttt{lm\_head} ($H_T = H_P$: frozen under LoRA; typically FP16 under PTQ) and exhibit $W^* \approx I$ in the backbone (exact by construction for PTQ; empirically small rotational drift for LoRA). The $W = I$ choice matches both facts: it gives $\Omega_I \approx \Omega$ in the feature term, and keeps the head term $\|\Sigma_P^{1/2}(W H_T - H_P)\|_F$ at its smallest attainable value---vanishing exactly when $H_T = H_P$, whereas a Procrustes-optimal $W^* \neq I$ would inflate this term even when the two heads are identical. \textbf{(ii)~Safety.} $\Omega_I \le \Omega$ (Appendix~\ref{app:trace_specialization}), so substituting $\Omega_I$ into Theorem~\ref{thm:unified_bound} yields a \emph{conservative} upper bound on the nuclear-form bound---any conclusion drawn from $\Omega_I$ still holds under the tighter form. \textbf{(iii)~Differentiability.} $\Omega_I$ is a smooth function of $Z_T, Z_P$ and back-propagates through standard autograd with no SVD, enabling the training-time regularizer of Sec.~\ref{subsec:shape_reg}. Unless stated otherwise, $\Omega$ in the sequel refers to $\Omega_I$.


\subsection{Extension to Autoregressive Generation}
\label{subsec:ar_extension}

The bound applies unchanged to sequence-level generation. For a sequence $(c, y)$ with context $c$ and target continuation $y = (y_1, \ldots, y_{|y|})$, the autoregressive risk is
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:ar_risk}
\mathcal{R}_M^{\mathrm{AR}} \;=\; \mathbb{E}_{(c, y) \sim \mathcal{D}} \!\left[ \frac{1}{|y|} \sum_{t=1}^{|y|} \ell\!\big(\phi_M(c, y_{<t}) \cdot H_M,\; y_t\big) \right].
\end{equation}
Under teacher forcing, all token-level features $\phi_M(c, y_{<t})$ are extracted in a single forward pass and collected into a matrix $Z_M^{\mathrm{AR}} \in \mathbb{R}^{N \times d}$ (with $N$ the total number of target tokens across the calibration set); Theorem~\ref{thm:unified_bound} then applies directly to $(Z_T^{\mathrm{AR}}, Z_P^{\mathrm{AR}})$, unifying point-wise classification (MMLU, ARC), language modeling (WikiText), and short-horizon QA (SQuAD, TriviaQA) under a single bound. The full corollary is in Appendix~\ref{app:ar_extension}.


\subsection{From Diagnostic to Training: Shape Regularization}
\label{subsec:shape_reg}

Theorem~\ref{thm:unified_bound} is used diagnostically throughout most of our experiments---computed post-hoc to compare trained variants---but the \emph{same} quantity lifts into a training-time penalty when the head term vanishes. Under LoRA fine-tuning with a frozen head, the head term drops out and the risk gap between the base model $\theta_0$ and a checkpoint $\theta_t$ reduces to
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:lora_bound}
|\mathcal{R}_0 - \mathcal{R}_t| \;\le\; K_{\mathrm{feat}}\sqrt{(\rho_0-\rho_t)^2 + 2\rho_0\rho_t(1-\Omega_I)}.
\end{equation}
Because $1-\Omega_I$ enters linearly under the square root and is differentiable in $Z_t$ (property (iii) of Sec.~\ref{subsec:interpretation}), we augment the fine-tuning objective with a PRISM-guided penalty:
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:shape_reg}
L_{\mathrm{total}} \;=\; \underbrace{L_{\mathrm{CE}}\bigl(\theta_t;\,\mathcal{D}_{\mathrm{FT}}\bigr)}_{\text{task loss}} \;+\; \lambda\,\bigl(1 - \Omega_I\!\bigl(Z_0^{\text{ref}},\, Z_t^{\text{ref}}\bigr)\bigr),
\end{equation}
where $Z_0^{\text{ref}}, Z_t^{\text{ref}}$ are base and current feature matrices on a fixed reference set. $Z_0^{\text{ref}}$ is pre-computed once with the frozen base model; $Z_t^{\text{ref}}$ is recomputed every $k$ micro-batches during training, adding negligible wall-clock overhead at 8B scale. The same geometric signal that \emph{diagnoses} backbone drift thereby \emph{prevents} it---an active constraint against catastrophic forgetting whose empirical effect is validated in Sec.~\ref{subsec:shape_reg_exp}.


\section{Applications Across the LLM Post-Training Lifecycle}
\label{sec:applications}

The Unified Risk Bound (Theorem~\ref{thm:unified_bound}) applies to any two models within a common lineage. We instantiate it below in two lifecycle settings---post-training quantization and LoRA fine-tuning with a frozen head---both of which satisfy the near-identity alignment condition discussed in Sec.~\ref{subsec:interpretation}, so the operational trace form $\Omega_I$ applies in each; the two settings differ in which term of the bound carries the risk gap.

\subsection{Quantization Quality Estimation}
\label{subsec:quantization}

We designate the full-precision model as Target $T$ and the quantized variant as Proxy $P$; Theorem~\ref{thm:unified_bound} applies directly. Two PTQ-specific conditions determine which term carries the risk gap. First, standard PTQ introduces element-wise numerical noise that preserves the coordinate basis ($W^* \approx I$), so the feature error reduces to scale collapse $(\Delta\rho)^2$---capturing the activation clipping induced by outlier channels~\cite{dettmers2022gpt3, xiao2023smoothquant}---and structural distortion $1 - \Omega_I$---capturing corruption of the relational geometry between token representations. Second, GPTQ and most BitsAndBytes configurations retain the \texttt{lm\_head} in FP16 and the head-divergence term $\|\Sigma_P^{1/2}(H_T - H_P)\|_F$ vanishes, but several GGUF k-quant tiers (e.g., \texttt{Q2\_K}, \texttt{Q3\_K\_M}) also quantize the output embedding and contribute a non-negligible head discrepancy---which we therefore keep explicit rather than drop. All three terms are generation-free and computable from a single forward pass, letting practitioners rank quantization configurations from feature statistics without decoding.


\subsection{Geometric Monitoring of Catastrophic Forgetting}
\label{subsec:forgetting}

We designate the frozen base model as $T = \theta_0$ and the fine-tuned checkpoint at step $t$ as $P = \theta_t$, and evaluate Theorem~\ref{thm:unified_bound} on tasks \emph{other than} the one being fine-tuned on---this held-out risk is the empirical signature of catastrophic forgetting. Under standard LoRA~\cite{hu2022lora}, low-rank updates are confined to attention and MLP projections while the \texttt{lm\_head} stays frozen ($H_t = H_0$), so the head-divergence term vanishes and forgetting is governed \emph{entirely} by backbone geometric drift $(\Delta\rho, 1-\Omega_I)$---the very quantity that the shape regularizer of Sec.~\ref{subsec:shape_reg} minimizes during training. The result is a label-free diagnostic that decouples manifold distortion from head-specific effects.


\subsection{Other Applications}
\label{subsec:other_applications}

Beyond these two instantiations, Theorem~\ref{thm:unified_bound} supports several additional diagnostic uses that we leave to future empirical validation: (i)~\emph{OOD detection} via both axes of the feature alignment error---the scale discrepancy $|\rho_T(x) - \rho_P(x)|$ at the per-sample or per-batch level \emph{and} the shape residual $\|\phi_T(x) - \phi_P(x) W^*\|_2$---either of which can spike on out-of-distribution inputs, and which together cover activation-magnitude shifts (scale) and breakdowns of the linear alignment hypothesis (shape); (ii)~\emph{cross-scale hyperparameter transfer}, since a small proxy with high $\Omega$ to a large target bounds the regret of transferring its optimal setting; and (iii)~\emph{label-free production monitoring}, since $\Omega$ and $\Delta\rho$ depend only on feature matrices and can serve as online drift signals without ground-truth labels.

\section{Experiments}
\label{sec:experiments}

\subsection{Experimental Setup}
\label{subsec:exp_setup}

\paragraph{Models.}
Our experiments span two regimes with different model coverage. For \textbf{quantization} (Sec.~\ref{subsec:quant_exp}) we evaluate four 8B open-weight models spanning the major current lineages: Llama-3.1-8B~\cite{grattafiori2024llama}, Ministral-3-8B, Qwen3-8B~\cite{qwen2025qwen3}, and DeepSeek-R1-Distill-Llama-8B, giving cross-family evidence that the bound is not a per-lineage artefact; we additionally replicate the same quantization grid on their instruction-tuned counterparts (Llama-3.1-8B-Instruct, Ministral-3-8B-Instruct, Qwen3-8B-Instruct) in Appendix~\ref{app:per_model_tables} to verify that the bound tracks degradation on instruction-tuned checkpoints as well as on base models. For \textbf{fine-tuning forgetting and shape regularization} (Secs.~\ref{subsec:forget_exp}--\ref{subsec:shape_reg_exp}) we restrict the main text to two models---Llama-3.1-8B and Qwen3-8B---with Qwen results in Appendix~\ref{app:qwen_forgetting}. This pair controls for four variables that could otherwise confound a forgetting study: (i)~pre-training corpus (Meta $\sim$15T tokens, English-dominant vs.\ Alibaba $\sim$36T tokens, multilingual), testing cross-lineage robustness of the Linear Representation Hypothesis; (ii)~depth (32 vs.\ 36 layers at fixed hidden dim $d{=}4096$), isolating depth effects on $\Omega_I$; (iii)~vocabulary size ($128{,}256$ vs.\ $151{,}936$), which enters $K_{\mathrm{feat}}=\max_{j,k}\|h_{j}-h_{k}\|_2$ directly and would expose any dependence of the bound on head geometry; and (iv)~tokenizer family (BPE vs.\ byte-level BPE). We exclude DeepSeek-R1-Distill (reasoning-distilled, atypical LoRA dynamics) and Ministral (multimodal architecture with nested language-model backbone) from the forgetting study because their fine-tuning behaviour deviates from standard practice.

\paragraph{Quantization Protocols.}
We test three PTQ families across bit-widths 2--8: \textbf{GGUF} (round-to-nearest, \texttt{Q2\_K} through \texttt{Q8\_0}), \textbf{GPTQ}~\cite{frantar2022gptq} (4-bit, second-order reconstruction), and \textbf{BitsAndBytes}~\cite{dettmers2022gpt3} (INT8, NF4, FP4). This covers the spectrum from basic rounding to calibration-based compensation.

\paragraph{Fine-Tuning Tasks.}
LoRA fine-tuning is performed on two complementary tasks chosen to elicit distinct forgetting profiles. \textbf{TruthfulQA} is a truthfulness-oriented QA set designed to trigger plausible-but-wrong generations, stressing safety-critical factual grounding. \textbf{BBQ} (Bias Benchmark for QA) measures bias-sensitive reasoning through ambiguous social-context questions. The two are complementary---TruthfulQA perturbs factual behaviour, BBQ perturbs reasoning under social bias---so downstream forgetting on general-knowledge benchmarks (ARC, MMLU, SQuAD, TriviaQA, GSM8K) provides a non-trivial stress test of whether PRISM tracks drift across task families, not just within a single domain.

\paragraph{Benchmarks.}
Following the autoregressive risk formulation (Eq.~\ref{eq:ar_risk}), we organize benchmarks by the partition between context $c$ and target $y$:
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Multiple-Choice (MC)} --- MMLU, ARC: $c$ is the full prompt including all choices, $y$ is the answer label token (a single token per question). We extract the final prompt token's feature and compare logit scores over the candidate labels, reducing the task to single-token classification.
    \item \textbf{Language Modeling (LM)} --- WikiText, FineWeb-Edu: $c$ is empty or a rolling prefix, $y$ is a contiguous text chunk. Risk is the standard sequence-level cross-entropy.
    \item \textbf{Short-Horizon Generation (QA)} --- TriviaQA, SQuAD: $c$ is the question prompt, $y$ is the answer span (1--5 tokens). Loss is evaluated strictly over answer tokens.
\end{itemize}
The main quantitative results of Secs.~\ref{subsec:quant_exp}--\ref{subsec:forget_exp} focus on the five MC and QA benchmarks (ARC, MMLU, SQuAD, TriviaQA, GSM8K); the two language-modeling benchmarks WikiText and FineWeb-Edu follow the same pattern and are reported in Appendix~\ref{app:per_model_tables}.

\paragraph{Calibration set size.}
All PRISM quantities and empirical risk gaps are computed on fixed held-out subsets held constant across all variants of a given base model: $N{=}512$ items per benchmark for the quantization regime, and $N{=}256$ items per held-out benchmark for the LoRA forgetting regime (the smaller fine-tuning tasks TruthfulQA and BBQ are evaluated on their full test sets). Four observations justify this protocol.
(i)~\textbf{Fixed-subset design neutralizes subset-selection noise.} Because the \emph{same} $N$-item subset is evaluated by every variant of a model, subset-selection variance enters every $|\Delta\mathcal{R}|$ and every bound estimate equally and therefore does not affect the variant-to-variant \emph{ranking}---the quantity we ultimately report via Spearman correlation.
(ii)~\textbf{Signal dominates sampling noise.} The orderings we rank distinguish variants whose true $|\Delta\mathcal{R}|$ values span one to two orders of magnitude (e.g., Q2\_K vs.\ Q8\_0, Table~\ref{tab:llama_decomposition_main}), a regime in which rank preservation is robust at moderate $N$.
(iii)~\textbf{Pointwise concentration.} The pointwise Lipschitz bound $|\Delta\ell(x)| \le B$ underlying Theorem~\ref{thm:unified_bound} combined with Popoviciu's inequality gives $\mathrm{Var}(\mathcal{R}_T(\mathcal{D}) - \mathcal{R}_P(\mathcal{D})) \le B^2/N$, so the sample-mean estimator is concentrated around the population risk gap whenever $B$ is small---exactly the regime where ranking is tightest.
(iv)~\textbf{Precedent.} Fixed-subset evaluation for variant ranking is standard in efficient LLM evaluation~\cite{polo2024tinybenchmarks, perlitz2024efficient}.

\paragraph{Shape-Regularization Hyperparameters.}
For Sec.~\ref{subsec:shape_reg_exp} we sweep $\lambda \in \{0.0,\, 0.1,\, 0.5\}$, where $\lambda{=}0$ reproduces unregularized LoRA. The reference set used to compute $\Omega_I$ is a fixed held-out batch of $32$ sequences drawn from the base model's pre-training distribution (not from $\mathcal{D}_{\mathrm{FT}}$, so that the penalty and task loss do not directly interfere). $Z_t^{\text{ref}}$ is recomputed every $k{=}8$ micro-steps; checkpoints are saved every 50 training steps, and all analysis figures/tables align to step $300$.

\paragraph{Teacher-Forced Scoring Protocol.}
All risks in this paper are computed in the teacher-forced regime: the prompt $c$ and the target tokens $y$ are concatenated and passed through the model once, and the per-token loss is read off at the target positions. This matches the scoring rules of the benchmarks above by construction---multiple-choice items are ranked by gold-label likelihood, perplexity suites are teacher-forced by definition, and short-answer QA is evaluated on the likelihood of the gold span---so no additional evaluation assumption is introduced. The protocol yields three concrete benefits relevant to variant-level diagnostics.
(a)~\textbf{Runtime.} A single parallel forward pass replaces token-by-token conditional decoding, avoiding the quadratic KV-cache growth and repeated sampling of autoregressive evaluation; for MMLU-scale benchmarks this collapses hours of generation into one pass per model.
(b)~\textbf{Training--evaluation consistency.} Teacher-forced cross-entropy is also the objective used during LoRA fine-tuning in our experiments, so our calibration quantity and the fine-tuning loss live on exactly the same scale, making variant-level drift measurements directly commensurate with training signals---a property that decoding-based metrics (BLEU, pass@$k$) cannot match.
(c)~\textbf{Low variance.} Removing the sampling stage eliminates temperature, top-$k$, and nucleus randomness, producing deterministic per-sample residuals and reproducible variant comparisons under the fixed-subset protocol above.
We do not claim teacher-forced cross-entropy substitutes for generation-based evaluation in open-ended tasks; PRISM is positioned as a rapid variant-ranking signal complementary to full generative evaluation.

Figure~\ref{fig:quant_grid_bound} summarizes the main quantization result: the PRISM bound $\mathcal{B}$ tracks $|\Delta\mathcal{R}|$ with strong rank correlation across four lineages and five benchmarks. Two qualitative patterns hold consistently. First, \emph{bit-width drives the bound monotonically}: Q8 and Q6 GGUF variants sit in the low-$\mathcal{B}$, low-$|\Delta\mathcal{R}|$ corner, while Q2 configurations move into the upper-right region where shape distortion $(1-\Omega_I)$ dominates the feature error. Second, \emph{family ordering is preserved across benchmarks}: for a given bit-width, BitsAndBytes NF4/FP4 and GGUF Q3 cluster together, while GPTQ-4bit is typically positioned one notch lower in $\mathcal{B}$ on Llama/Ministral/DeepSeek, reflecting its second-order Hessian compensation---with a notable exception on Qwen3 that we analyze below. Table~\ref{tab:llama_decomposition_main} reports the per-variant decomposition for Llama-3.1-8B on MMLU and TriviaQA (remaining benchmarks and the other three lineages are in Appendix~\ref{app:per_model_tables}): at the extreme Q2\_K TriviaQA case, $\rho_P$ exceeds $\rho_T$ only mildly ($\Delta\rho\approx 1.3$), but $\Omega_I$ drops to $0.76$, driving the shape-mismatch arm of the bound to dominate. This separation between scale and shape channels, made explicit by our decomposition, is not visible in a single-number similarity metric.

\paragraph{Which axis fails where.}
The decomposition isolates three qualitatively distinct PTQ failure modes in our data, each dominated by a different axis of Theorem~\ref{thm:unified_bound}. \emph{Shape distortion} dominates the majority of aggressive quantization configurations: at Q2 and Q3 across all four families, the shape term $2\rho_T\rho_P(1{-}\Omega_I)$ outweighs the scale term $(\Delta\rho)^2$ by two to three orders of magnitude (e.g., Llama-Q2\_K on MMLU: $24$ vs.~$\sim$$9{,}000$; Ministral-Q2\_K on MMLU: $\sim$$9$ vs.~$\sim$$11{,}000$)---consistent with low-bit PTQ corrupting the relational structure of the feature manifold rather than its global scale. \emph{Scale collapse} is rare but unambiguous when it occurs: Qwen3-8B under GPTQ-4bit halves $\rho_P$ to $162$ from $\rho_T{=}332$ on MMLU and to $133$ from $\rho_T{=}267$ on GSM8K, driving $(\Delta\rho)^2$ past the shape arm by $1.2\text{--}2.3\times$, whereas the same GPTQ-4bit protocol leaves $\rho$ invariant on Llama and DeepSeek ($\Delta\rho<3$ throughout)---a lineage-specific calibration pathology that the scale axis localizes precisely. \emph{Head divergence} isolates a third regime entirely: at Qwen3-Q6\_K and Qwen3-Q8\_0, backbone scale and shape are preserved to within $(\Delta\rho)^2{<}1$ and $\Omega_I{>}0.9995$, yet the quantized output embedding alone contributes $\gamma\approx 60$, making $\gamma$ the sole residual error source. Each dominant axis suggests a different remediation---per-channel outlier smoothing~\cite{xiao2023smoothquant} for scale collapse, Hessian-aware reconstruction for shape distortion, and FP16-head retention for head divergence---turning the bound from a scalar score into a causal diagnostic.

\paragraph{Feature term vs.\ full bound.}
Averaged across the $4\times 5$ grid, the feature-only term $\delta$ attains Spearman $\overline{r_s}(\delta)\!\approx\!\textsc{82.35}$, marginally above the full bound's $\overline{r_s}(\mathcal{B})\!\approx\!\textsc{78.5}$.%
\footnote{Exact per-subplot $r_s$ values are annotated in Fig.~\ref{fig:quant_grid_bound} and in Appendix~\ref{app:feature_only}; aggregate means are reported to one decimal place.}
The small gap is a regime artefact of our PTQ suite: GPTQ and BitsAndBytes keep the \texttt{lm\_head} in FP16 (so $\gamma \equiv 0$ by construction), whereas some GGUF configurations quantize the output embedding and yield $\gamma > 0$. Mixing these two regimes in a single scatter causes the $\gamma$ term to add non-monotone variance in the x-coordinate, slightly diluting rank correlation on $\mathcal{B}$ relative to $\delta$. Both quantities remain theoretically meaningful---$\mathcal{B}$ is the tight upper bound of Theorem~\ref{thm:unified_bound} that the head-free $\delta$ dominates only when $\gamma$ happens to vanish---and confirm that PTQ degradation is dominated by scale collapse $(\Delta\rho)^2$ and shape mismatch $(1-\Omega_I)$, as predicted by Sec.~\ref{subsec:quantization}. The full feature-only $4\times 5$ scatter is reported in Appendix~\ref{app:feature_only}; per-model decomposition tables for Ministral, Qwen3, and DeepSeek are in Appendix~\ref{app:quant_tables}.

Second, \emph{the correlation holds benchmark-by-benchmark}, not only in aggregate: the bound predicts drift on benchmarks (e.g., GSM8K) whose distributions differ substantially from the fine-tuning data, direct evidence that backbone geometry carries the forgetting signal for arbitrary held-out evaluation. \subsection{Ablation: Component-Wise Contribution to Variant Ranking}
\label{subsec:ablation}

\input{tables/quantization/baseline}

Table~\ref{tab:baseline} isolates the marginal ranking contribution of each component of the PRISM bound by progressively adding them. The shape-only similarity $\Omega_I$ already achieves mean $|r_s|{=}0.77$, confirming that the relational geometry of token features alone carries most of the variant-ranking signal. Adding the scale term to form the feature alignment error $\delta_I$ lifts the mean by $+0.054$ to $0.82$ and wins the plurality of cells, making scale collapse the single largest per-component contributor. Adding the covariance-weighted head term to form the full bound $\mathcal{B}_I$ brings the aggregate mean slightly down to $0.79$: because our suite mixes protocols that quantize the output embedding ($\gamma{>}0$ for GGUF k-quant tiers) with protocols that keep it in FP16 ($\gamma{\equiv}0$ for GPTQ and BnB), the $\gamma$ term injects non-monotone variance across the pooled scatter. Two observations keep $\mathcal{B}_I$ the right \emph{default} metric. First, it is the certified upper bound of Theorem~\ref{thm:unified_bound} and is strictly required whenever $\gamma{>}0$; $\delta_I$ alone is not a valid bound in that regime. Second, $\mathcal{B}_I$ has the smallest cross-lineage spread (per-lineage std $\approx 0.04$ vs.\ $0.05$--$0.06$ for $\Omega_I$ and $\delta_I$), so its ranking performance is the most robust across the four model families tested.

\section{Conclusion and Future Work}
\label{sec:conclusion}

We presented PRISM, a unified geometric framework that establishes a rigorous, computable link between representational alignment and functional risk. By formulating alignment as an Orthogonal Procrustes problem and deriving an exact decomposition via the nuclear norm, we obtained a closed-form bound on $|\mathcal{R}_T - \mathcal{R}_P|$ that separates scale collapse ($\Delta\rho$), structural distortion ($1 - \Omega$), and head divergence as independently measurable diagnostic quantities.

We instantiated this framework for two critical LLM lifecycle stages---post-training quantization and catastrophic forgetting during fine-tuning---and showed that evaluating the bound on standard LLM benchmarks yields a low-variance, mechanism-level diagnostic for variant ranking that is commensurate with the fine-tuning objective and whose dominant axis localizes distinct failure modes (shape distortion, scale collapse, or head divergence) to their respective remediations.

\paragraph{Limitations and Future Work.}
Several promising directions remain:
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Dynamic Trajectory Alignment:} Our current formulation validates static (prefill) feature manifolds. Extending to multi-step generation trajectories would enable proxy estimation for long Chain-of-Thought reasoning and agentic workflows.
    \item \textbf{Cross-Lineage Proxy Scenarios:} The current framework targets same-lineage comparisons (a base and its post-training descendants). Extending the alignment step beyond orthogonal Procrustes would naturally support proxy scenarios that bridge different lineages---such as teacher--student distillation---generalizing PRISM to a broader class of model comparisons.
    \item \textbf{Cross-Modal Generalization:} The Linear Representation Hypothesis extends beyond text. Investigating PRISM's applicability to vision transformers and cross-modal models (e.g., CLIP) remains a natural next step.
\end{itemize}
