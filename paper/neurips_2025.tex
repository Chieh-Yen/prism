\begin{abstract}
Comparing Large Language Model (LLM) variants---quantized checkpoints, fine-tuned snapshots, LoRA adaptations---faces a diagnostic gap: benchmarks report only an overall accuracy score that flags whether a variant has degraded but cannot say \emph{why}, and existing representational similarity metrics (e.g., CKA) add no formal link to downstream performance, reducing all geometric drift to a single opaque number.
We propose \textbf{PRISM} (Proxy Risk Inference via Structural Mapping), a framework that bounds the cross-entropy risk gap between a target model and its proxy along three independently measurable axes, each computable from a single forward pass: a scale mismatch $\Delta\rho$ and a shape mismatch $1-\Omega$ obtained jointly from an exact Orthogonal Procrustes decomposition---with $\Omega$ a nuclear-norm similarity strictly tighter than Frobenius surrogates such as CKA---and a head-discrepancy term weighted by where the data actually lives.
We instantiate the framework for post-training quantization (GPTQ, GGUF, BitsAndBytes), where the bound reduces to a pure geometric distortion measure, and for LoRA fine-tuning, where it isolates catastrophic forgetting in the backbone geometry. Because its trace-form variant $\Omega_I$ is differentiable, the same quantity lifts into a training-time \emph{shape regularizer} that empirically suppresses downstream forgetting without impairing target-task learning.
Across four 8B-scale LLM lineages (Llama, Qwen, Ministral, DeepSeek) and five benchmarks, the bound ranks variant-level degradation with strong Spearman correlation under both quantization and LoRA forgetting, and its three axes localize distinct failure modes---shape distortion under low-bit quantization, scale collapse under lineage-specific GPTQ calibration, and head divergence under quantized output embeddings---turning a single-number similarity into a mechanism-level diagnostic whose ranking can be trusted and whose failures can be read off directly.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}

\paragraph{The diagnostic gap in the post-training LLM lifecycle.}
As large language models have moved from research prototypes to production defaults, a new engineering bottleneck has emerged downstream of pre-training.
A single base checkpoint now spawns a months-long pipeline of quantization (GPTQ~\cite{frantar2022gptq}, GGUF, BitsAndBytes~\cite{dettmers2022gpt3}), parameter-efficient adaptation (LoRA~\cite{hu2022lora}), and distillation, producing dozens of intermediate variants that must each be compared, tuned, and iterated on before a deployed model emerges~\cite{grattafiori2024llama, qwen2025qwen3}.
Inside this pipeline, the only signal evaluation routinely provides is an \emph{overall accuracy score} per benchmark---a single number that flags whether a variant has degraded but says nothing about \emph{why}, or which compression knob to turn next---and in a pipeline this long, an un-diagnosable accuracy drop is not a minor inconvenience but a blocker to iteration.
An accuracy drop could equally trace to activation-magnitude collapse from outlier channels, to structural distortion of the feature manifold, or to quantization of the output embedding---three mechanisms each demanding a different remediation.
What the field needs is therefore not a cheaper accuracy number, but a diagnostic whose ranking can be trusted, whose mechanism can be inspected, and whose signal can be trained against.

\paragraph{Similarity scores without risk, without mechanism.}
The natural shortcut is to borrow a representational similarity metric and declare two models ``close enough.''
A decade of internal-representation analysis has produced exactly this---SVCCA~\cite{raghu2017svcca}, linear and kernel CKA~\cite{kornblith2019similarity}, and a broader family of Frobenius-norm statistics---each reducing two activation matrices to a single alignment score.
Yet these scores do not pin down downstream behavior: a quantized model can read as highly similar and still lose accuracy, while a fine-tuned model can read as drifted and still retain task performance~\cite{ding2021grounding, davari2023reliability}, and the recent survey of~\cite{klabunde2023similarity} marks the link between representational and functional similarity as an open problem.
And even where they do correlate, a single-number similarity is no less opaque than the accuracy verdict above---it flags drift without localizing it to an axis that diagnosis could act on.
What is missing is a structural insight that lifts a geometric quantity into a functional risk bound \emph{while preserving the causal decomposition that diagnosis requires}.

\paragraph{What makes LLM geometry analytically tractable.}
That insight exists, and it is structural to modern LLMs.
Every current Transformer language model factors cleanly into a deep non-linear backbone followed by a single linear prediction head (the \texttt{lm\_head}) that projects features onto the vocabulary; there is no additional non-linear classifier between features and logits.
The Linear Representation Hypothesis~\cite{moschella2022relative, park2023linear} further provides that models within a common lineage---a base and its quantized, fine-tuned, or distilled descendants---learn feature manifolds related by a near-isometric linear map.
These two facts, co-occurring, are what make the geometry formally tractable: the feature-alignment residual under an Orthogonal Procrustes problem can be lifted \emph{exactly} into an upper bound on the cross-entropy risk gap, with no ad-hoc constants and no black-box terms.
It is precisely because LLMs uniquely satisfy this structural recipe---a frozen linear head under standard LoRA, dominant outlier-channel scale collapse under PTQ~\cite{dettmers2022gpt3, xiao2023smoothquant}, and a shared cross-entropy scoring rule across both multiple-choice and generative benchmarks---that we instantiate the framework exclusively on them.
The generality of the method is mathematical; the leverage is architectural.

\paragraph{PRISM: a bound with three actionable axes.}
We propose \textbf{PRISM} (Proxy Risk Inference via Structural Mapping), a framework that turns this structural insight into a bound-certified, mechanism-level diagnostic for LLM variants (Fig.~\ref{fig:prism_concept}).
Starting from the Procrustes alignment residual, we prove an \emph{exact} decomposition of the feature error into two orthogonal quantities: a \emph{scale mismatch} $\Delta\rho$ that isolates activation-magnitude collapse, and a \emph{shape mismatch} $1-\Omega$ that isolates distortion of the feature manifold, with $\Omega$ a nuclear-norm similarity strictly tighter than Frobenius surrogates such as CKA (Appendix~\ref{app:tightness}).
A third term---a head discrepancy, weighted by where the data actually lives---closes the bound.
Because each of these three quantities is individually measurable from a single forward pass, a PRISM failure is not merely a flag but a \emph{direction}: a scale-dominated failure points to outlier-channel clipping and argues for per-channel scaling~\cite{xiao2023smoothquant}; a shape-dominated failure indicts the compression's Hessian approximation and argues for second-order methods such as GPTQ; a head-dominated failure localizes the loss to quantized output embeddings and argues for keeping the head in FP16.
In our primary LLM regimes the optimal alignment is near-identity, so all experiments report a differentiable trace-form surrogate of $\Omega$ (denoted $\Omega_I$) that remains a conservative upper bound on the nuclear form and is autograd-compatible for gradient-based use in training.
Across four 8B-scale LLM lineages (Llama, Qwen, Ministral, DeepSeek) and five benchmarks, the three axes track empirical cross-entropy degradation with strong Spearman rank correlation under both post-training quantization and LoRA fine-tuning, making PRISM a diagnostic whose ordering can be trusted and whose mechanism can be read off directly.

\paragraph{From diagnostic to training signal.}
The same quantity that reads a variant's drift can also prevent it.
Under standard LoRA fine-tuning the prediction head is frozen, the head term of the bound vanishes by construction, and the risk gap is governed entirely by backbone scale and shape drift---a quantity that, in its trace form $\Omega_I$, is smooth in the current features and therefore fully autograd-compatible.
Adding a weighted penalty on $1-\Omega_I$ to the fine-tuning objective therefore lifts the bound from a post-hoc lens into an active geometric constraint against catastrophic forgetting during training.
Empirically, even a weak penalty weight ($\lambda{=}0.1$) eliminates a substantial fraction of downstream forgetting on ARC, MMLU, SQuAD, TriviaQA, and GSM8K without impairing target-task fit; increasing $\lambda$ strengthens preservation monotonically, giving a bound-motivated knob for the forgetting--learning trade-off.
Because the penalty is computed on a 32-sequence reference set disjoint from the fine-tuning data, no labels on the held-out benchmarks are ever needed---geometry alone suffices.

\paragraph{Contributions.}
Our contributions are threefold.
\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{An exact scale--shape decomposition that turns geometric similarity into actionable coordinates.} We prove that the Orthogonal Procrustes residual on a pair of feature matrices decomposes \emph{exactly} into a scale-mismatch arm and a shape-mismatch arm, promoting the two into independently measurable diagnostic coordinates, with the shape arm governed by a nuclear-norm similarity $\Omega$ strictly tighter than Frobenius surrogates such as CKA (Appendix~\ref{app:tightness}). Combined with a covariance-weighted head-discrepancy term, the decomposition lifts Procrustes alignment into a closed-form upper bound on the cross-entropy risk gap (Theorem~\ref{thm:unified_bound}) whose three axes---scale collapse, structural distortion, and head divergence---each carry a distinct operational reading that a single-number similarity cannot provide.

    \item \textbf{A unified framework across the LLM post-training lifecycle, usable both diagnostically and as a training objective.} The bound reduces to a pure geometric distortion measure under post-training quantization (GPTQ, GGUF, BitsAndBytes) and isolates catastrophic forgetting in backbone drift under LoRA with a frozen head; because its trace-form variant $\Omega_I$ is differentiable, the same quantity lifts into a shape regularizer that actively suppresses forgetting during fine-tuning (Sec.~\ref{subsec:shape_reg}).

    \item \textbf{Rank-consistent empirical validation with three-axis failure-mode localization.} Across four 8B-scale LLM lineages (Llama, Qwen, Ministral, DeepSeek) and five benchmarks (ARC, MMLU, SQuAD, TriviaQA, GSM8K), the bound ranks variant-level degradation with strong Spearman correlation under both quantization and LoRA forgetting, and its three axes localize the documented failure modes of each compression family: shape distortion under low-bit quantization, scale collapse under lineage-specific GPTQ calibration, and head divergence under quantized output embeddings. Shape regularization reproduces these rank-consistent gains during training---a mild penalty weight reduces forgetting on every held-out benchmark without impairing target-task learning.
\end{enumerate}

\section{Related Work}

\paragraph{Representational Similarity Metrics.}
Comparing the internal representations of neural networks is a long-standing problem. SVCCA~\cite{raghu2017svcca} and linear/kernel CKA~\cite{kornblith2019similarity} provide Frobenius-norm statistics of alignment between activation matrices, but subsequent empirical studies document that neither directly pins down downstream behavior~\cite{ding2021grounding, davari2023reliability}, and the recent survey of~\cite{klabunde2023similarity} explicitly flags the link between representational and functional similarity as an open problem. The closest mathematical precedent to our formulation is the generalized shape metrics framework~\cite{williams2021generalized}, which defines a proper metric space on activation matrices via the Orthogonal Procrustes distance $d_1(Z_T, Z_P) = \min_{Q \in \mathcal{O}} \|Z_T - Z_P Q\|_F$. Expanding $d_1^2$ yields $\|Z_T\|_F^2 + \|Z_P\|_F^2 - 2\|Z_T^\top Z_P\|_*$, so the nuclear norm of the cross-moment appearing in the numerator of our Procrustes Similarity $\Omega$ is already implicit in this classical residual. The \emph{Linear Representation Hypothesis}~\cite{moschella2022relative, park2023linear} provides the structural justification for restricting alignments to the orthogonal group. Our novelty is therefore not a new metric but (i)~an \emph{exact decomposition} of the Procrustes residual into a scale term $(\rho_T - \rho_P)^2$ and a shape term $2\rho_T\rho_P(1-\Omega)$, promoting scale and shape mismatch to \emph{separate, operationally distinct diagnostic coordinates}, and (ii)~the lifting of this residual to a \emph{functional-risk bound} (discussed next).

\paragraph{From Representational Similarity to Functional Bounds.}
A recent line of work has begun to connect representational geometry to task-level behavior. \cite{harvey2024what} show that the Procrustes shape distance on \emph{whitened} kernel matrices two-sidedly bounds the expected $L_2$ distance $\mathbb{E}\|Xw^* - Yv^*\|_2^2$ between \emph{optimal linear readouts} across a distribution of decoding targets (their Prop.~3), quantifying how Procrustes alignment constrains best-case linear decodability. The Platonic Representation Hypothesis~\cite{huh2024platonic} further conjectures, on empirical grounds, that large models converge to a shared representational geometry whose alignment correlates with performance, but stops short of a formal bound. Our framework addresses a complementary and, for LLM evaluation, operationally more direct question: rather than bounding the distance between freshly-optimized linear readouts on whitened features, we bound the cross-entropy risk gap $|\mathcal{R}_T - \mathcal{R}_P|$ between two models \emph{using their own deployed prediction heads} on raw (un-whitened) features. This requires four ingredients absent from the shape-metric literature: (a)~a tight feature-Lipschitz constant for cross-entropy, derived via simplex polarization as the maximum pairwise distance between \texttt{lm\_head} token embeddings (Appendix~\ref{app:kfeat}); (b)~a second-moment-adjusted head-discrepancy term $\|\Sigma_P^{1/2}(W^* H_T - H_P)\|_F$ that vanishes exactly under frozen-head protocols (standard LoRA, FP16-head PTQ); (c)~retention of activation scale as a first-class diagnostic rather than discarding it through whitening; and (d)~instantiations tailored to the LLM lifecycle (post-training quantization, catastrophic forgetting) in which each term acquires an operational reading.

\paragraph{Post-Training Quantization and Its Evaluation.}
Post-training quantization (PTQ) has become the dominant approach for deploying LLMs under memory constraints.
GPTQ~\cite{frantar2022gptq} uses approximate second-order information to minimize layer-wise reconstruction error, while round-to-nearest methods in GGUF and BitsAndBytes~\cite{dettmers2022gpt3} offer simpler but less controlled compression; the activation-outlier phenomena that drive their degradation are well documented~\cite{xiao2023smoothquant}.
A common bottleneck across all these methods is \emph{quality assessment}: determining how much a quantized variant has degraded requires running full perplexity or accuracy evaluations on large benchmarks, which can be as expensive as the quantization process itself.
Existing diagnostics are limited to indirect proxies such as layer-wise reconstruction loss or weight quantization error, neither of which accounts for the nonlinear accumulation of errors through the full network.
PRISM addresses this gap by providing a \emph{generation-free} geometric diagnostic: our bound decomposes quantization-induced degradation into scale collapse ($\Delta\rho$, capturing activation clipping from outlier channels~\cite{xiao2023smoothquant}) and structural distortion ($1-\Omega$), both computable from a single forward pass on a small calibration set without any label or generation.

\paragraph{Catastrophic Forgetting in Continual and Parameter-Efficient Fine-Tuning.}
When LLMs are sequentially fine-tuned---whether through full SFT or parameter-efficient methods such as LoRA~\cite{hu2022lora}---they risk catastrophic forgetting of pre-training knowledge. Classical remedies operate in weight space: EWC~\cite{kirkpatrick2017overcoming} penalizes changes to important parameters via a diagonal Fisher approximation, while replay-based methods retain a buffer of old data. These weight-space constraints are rigid, penalizing any parameter movement equally regardless of whether the underlying feature geometry is preserved. Recent geometric analyses have begun to localize forgetting in subspace structure: \cite{steele2026subspace} shows that LoRA forgetting tracks the minimum principal angle between task \emph{gradient} subspaces via the empirical law $F = \alpha(1-\cos^2\theta_{\min}) + \beta$. Our framework is complementary but shifts the locus from gradient geometry to \emph{hidden-state} geometry: we monitor forgetting through drift of the feature manifold ($\Omega$, $\Delta\rho$) relative to the frozen base model---a drift that naturally permits global rotations and rescalings with no effect on downstream risk---and, crucially, \emph{bounds} rather than fits the risk gap. Under standard LoRA fine-tuning with a frozen prediction head ($H_T = H_P$), our bound isolates forgetting entirely in the backbone geometry, yielding a label-free diagnostic that decouples manifold distortion from head-specific effects.

\paragraph{Efficient Evaluation and Performance Forecasting for Foundation Models.}
Reducing the cost of evaluating LLM variants has motivated two distinct lines of work.
Scaling laws~\cite{kaplan2020scaling, hoffmann2022training} predict aggregate loss as a power-law function of compute and data, but cannot capture variant-specific degradation (e.g., the effect of a particular quantization format on a specific benchmark).
Efficient-evaluation methods---such as tinyBenchmarks~\cite{polo2024tinybenchmarks}, which uses item-response-theory-calibrated subsets of existing benchmarks, and the ranking-reliability framework of~\cite{perlitz2024efficient}---curate small representative item subsets that preserve system orderings, but offer no formal connection between representational change and expected accuracy shift.
Weak-to-strong generalization~\cite{burns2023weak} uses smaller models to supervise larger ones, but targets label generation rather than risk estimation.
PRISM takes a different, geometry-grounded route: rather than extrapolating from scaling trends or sub-sampling benchmark items, we exploit the fact that a geometrically aligned proxy ($\Omega \approx 1$) produces per-sample losses tightly coupled to those of the target.
Combined with the per-sample coupling provided by our bound (Sec.~\ref{subsec:benchmark_calibration}), this allows any benchmark---the full benchmark, not a sub-sample---to serve as its own calibration set via a single forward pass.

\section{A Geometric Framework for Proxy Performance Estimation}
\label{sec:geometric_foundation}

We derive a rigorous upper bound on the risk discrepancy $|\mathcal{R}_T - \mathcal{R}_P|$ between a target model $T$ and a proxy model $P$, grounded in their representational geometry. We first formalize the backbone-plus-linear-head architecture, then state our main theorem linking geometric alignment to functional risk, and finally extend the bound to autoregressive generation.

\subsection{Problem Setup}
\label{subsec:problem_setup}

\paragraph{The Backbone-Plus-Linear-Head Architecture.}
We consider a \emph{Target Model} $T$ (e.g., full-precision) and a \emph{Proxy Model} $P$ (e.g., quantized or fine-tuned variant), both with hidden dimension $d$. Each model $M \in \{T, P\}$ decomposes as:
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:model_decomp}
f_M(x) = \mathrm{softmax}\!\big(\phi_M(x) \cdot H_M\big),
\end{equation}
where $\phi_M: \mathcal{X} \to \mathbb{R}^{d}$ is the \emph{feature extractor} (Transformer backbone), producing a feature vector $z_M = \phi_M(x)$, and $H_M \in \mathbb{R}^{d \times V}$ is the \emph{prediction head} (\texttt{lm\_head}), projecting features onto the vocabulary of size $V$. We denote the columns of $H_M$ (token embeddings) as $h_{M,1}, \ldots, h_{M,V} \in \mathbb{R}^d$.

\paragraph{Risk via Cross-Entropy.}
For a data distribution $\mathcal{D}$ over input-label pairs $(x, y)$, the \emph{risk} of model $M$ is:
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:ce_def}
\mathcal{R}_M = \mathbb{E}_{(x,y) \sim \mathcal{D}}\!\Big[\ell\!\big(\phi_M(x) \cdot H_M,\; y\big)\Big], \quad \text{where} \quad \ell(v, y) = -v_y + \log\!\sum_{j=1}^{V} e^{v_j}
\end{equation}
is the cross-entropy loss, with logits $v = \phi_M(x) \cdot H_M \in \mathbb{R}^V$ and ground-truth index $y$.

\paragraph{The Linear Alignment Hypothesis.}
Following the Linear Representation Hypothesis~\cite{moschella2022relative, park2023linear}, we posit that models within the same lineage learn feature manifolds related by a near-isometric linear transformation: there exists an orthogonal $W \in \mathcal{O}(d) = \{W \in \mathbb{R}^{d \times d} \mid W^\top W = I_d\}$ such that
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:alignment_hypothesis}
\phi_T(x) \approx \phi_P(x)\, W, \quad \forall x \in \mathcal{X}.
\end{equation}

\paragraph{Notation.}
Given $n$ samples, we collect features into $Z_T, Z_P \in \mathbb{R}^{n \times d}$. We define the RMS feature scale $\rho_M = \frac{1}{\sqrt{n}}\|Z_M\|_F$ and the empirical second-moment matrix $\Sigma_P = \frac{1}{n} Z_P^\top Z_P$.


\subsection{The Unified Risk Bound}
\label{subsec:unified_bound}

We derive a computable upper bound on $|\mathcal{R}_T - \mathcal{R}_P|$ in three steps; full proofs are in Appendix~\ref{app:detailed_proofs}.

\paragraph{Step 1: Decomposing the risk gap.}
Introducing an intermediate \emph{hybrid} risk $\mathcal{R}_{P \to T} = \mathbb{E}[\ell(\phi_P(x) W \cdot H_T, y)]$ that evaluates the target's head on the proxy's aligned features, the triangle inequality gives (Appendix~\ref{app:risk_decomp}):
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:risk_decomp}
|\mathcal{R}_T - \mathcal{R}_P| \;\le\; \underbrace{\big|\mathcal{R}_T - \mathcal{R}_{P \to T}\big|}_{\text{Feature alignment error } \delta} \;+\; \underbrace{\big|\mathcal{R}_{P \to T} - \mathcal{R}_P\big|}_{\text{Head discrepancy } \gamma}.
\end{equation}

\paragraph{Step 2: Bounding the feature alignment error $\delta$.}
The cross-entropy loss is Lipschitz in $z$ with constant $K_{\mathrm{feat}} = \max_{j,k} \|h_{T,j} - h_{T,k}\|_2$ (the maximum pairwise distance between token embeddings in $H_T$; see Appendix~\ref{app:kfeat}). Combined with Jensen's inequality, this gives $\delta \le K_{\mathrm{feat}} \sqrt{\frac{1}{n}\|Z_T - Z_P W\|_F^2}$.

To minimize this residual over orthogonal $W$, we solve the classical \emph{Orthogonal Procrustes problem}. Its closed-form solution $W^* = V U^\top$ (from the SVD $Z_T^\top Z_P = U \Sigma V^\top$) yields an \emph{exact} decomposition of the residual:

\begin{proposition}[Exact Geometric Decomposition]
\label{prop:exact_decomposition}
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:exact_equality}
\min_{W \in \mathcal{O}(d)} \frac{1}{n} \| Z_T - Z_P W \|_F^2
\;=\;
\underbrace{(\rho_T - \rho_P)^2}_{\text{\rm Scale Mismatch } (\Delta\rho)^2}
\;+\;
\underbrace{2 \rho_T \rho_P \big( 1 - \Omega \big)}_{\text{\rm Shape Mismatch}},
\end{equation}
where $\Omega(Z_T, Z_P)$ is the \emph{Procrustes Similarity}, defined via the nuclear norm of the cross-moment:
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:omega_def}
\Omega(Z_T, Z_P) := \frac{\| Z_T^\top Z_P \|_*}{\| Z_T \|_F \| Z_P \|_F} \;\in [0,\, 1].
\end{equation}
\end{proposition}

\begin{remark}[Relation to classical Procrustes shape distances]
\label{rem:williams_relation}
The left-hand side of Eq.~(\ref{eq:exact_equality}) is the squared Procrustes size-and-shape distance $d_1^2(Z_T, Z_P)$ studied in the generalized shape metrics framework~\cite{williams2021generalized} and its recent decodability analysis~\cite{harvey2024what}. Expanding $d_1^2$ reveals that the nuclear norm $\|Z_T^\top Z_P\|_*$ appears inside any Procrustes-based similarity; accordingly, our contribution is \emph{not} the Procrustes distance itself. Rather, it is (a)~the explicit split of this residual into the scale term $(\rho_T-\rho_P)^2$ and the shape term $2\rho_T\rho_P(1-\Omega)$, which the shape-metric literature treats jointly as a single descriptive dissimilarity, and (b)~its lifting to a functional-risk bound (Theorem~\ref{thm:unified_bound}). Sections~\ref{subsec:quantization} and~\ref{subsec:forgetting} show that the two arms of this split have qualitatively different operational meanings and dominate the risk gap under different LLM lifecycle regimes.
\end{remark}

\noindent Eq.~(\ref{eq:exact_equality}) is an exact identity: minimizing feature discrepancy is equivalent to maximizing $\Omega$ while matching scales $\rho$. The nuclear norm is provably tighter than Frobenius-based metrics such as CKA (Appendix~\ref{app:tightness}). Combining:
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:delta_main}
\delta \;\le\; K_{\mathrm{feat}} \sqrt{(\rho_T - \rho_P)^2 + 2\rho_T \rho_P(1 - \Omega)}.
\end{equation}

\paragraph{Step 3: Bounding the head discrepancy $\gamma$.}
Using the Lipschitz constant of cross-entropy with respect to logits ($K_{\mathrm{pred}} \le \sqrt{2}$) and projecting the head misalignment $\Delta H = W^* H_T - H_P$ onto the empirical second-moment matrix (Appendix~\ref{app:head_bound}):
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:gamma_main}
\gamma \;\le\; K_{\mathrm{pred}} \cdot \|\Sigma_P^{1/2}(W^* H_T - H_P)\|_F.
\end{equation}
The second-moment weighting $\Sigma_P^{1/2}$ ensures that only misalignment within the \emph{active subspace} of the data is penalized.

\paragraph{Main Result.}
Combining Eqs.~(\ref{eq:risk_decomp}), (\ref{eq:delta_main}), and (\ref{eq:gamma_main}):

\begin{theorem}[Unified Risk Bound]
\label{thm:unified_bound}
Let $W^* \in \mathcal{O}(d)$ be the optimal Procrustes alignment. Then:
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:unified_bound_eq}
\begin{aligned}
|\mathcal{R}_T - \mathcal{R}_P| \;\;\le\;\; & \underbrace{K_{\mathrm{feat}} \sqrt{ (\rho_T - \rho_P)^2 + 2\rho_T \rho_P \big( 1 - \Omega(Z_T, Z_P) \big) }}_{\text{\rm Geometric Alignment Error}} \\
& + \underbrace{K_{\mathrm{pred}} \cdot \| \Sigma_P^{1/2} (W^* H_T - H_P) \|_F}_{\text{\rm Second-Moment-Weighted Head Error}}.
\end{aligned}
\end{equation}
\end{theorem}


\subsection{Geometric Interpretation: Three Independent Axes of Drift}
\label{subsec:interpretation}

Theorem~\ref{thm:unified_bound} decomposes the risk gap along three independent axes, each a separately measurable failure mode of the proxy and each attached to a distinct operational signature:

\paragraph{Scale Matching ($\rho_T \approx \rho_P$).}
Divergence in activation magnitude directly amplifies the geometric error term. In post-training quantization, aggressive bit-width reduction clips activation outliers~\cite{dettmers2022gpt3, xiao2023smoothquant}, shrinking $\rho_Q < \rho_F$. Our bound isolates this \emph{scale collapse} as a primary and independently measurable source of degradation.

\paragraph{Shape Alignment ($\Omega \to 1$).}
The shape mismatch term is governed by $\Omega$, which captures how well the relational structure of the feature manifold is preserved up to rotation. A drop in $\Omega$ signals that the relative arrangement of token representations has been corrupted---what we term \emph{structural distortion}. Defined via the nuclear norm, $\Omega$ is strictly tighter than Frobenius-based surrogates such as CKA for the Procrustes alignment residual; the full comparison is deferred to Appendix~\ref{app:tightness}.

\paragraph{Head Coherence ($\|\Sigma_P^{1/2}(W^* H_T - H_P)\|_F \to 0$).}
The head error term quantifies how differently the two prediction heads interpret aligned features, weighted by the empirical second-moment matrix $\Sigma_P$. Misalignment in directions orthogonal to the data manifold does not contribute, as it cannot affect predictions. Notably, under standard LoRA fine-tuning~\cite{hu2022lora} where the \texttt{lm\_head} is frozen ($H_T = H_P$), this term vanishes entirely, isolating all performance change in the backbone geometry ($\Delta\rho$ and $\Omega$).

\paragraph{Operational form used in experiments.}
Although $\Omega$ is the tightest Procrustes-residual similarity, all experiments in Sec.~\ref{sec:experiments} and the shape regularizer of Sec.~\ref{subsec:shape_reg} instead report its identity-alignment \emph{trace specialization}
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:omega_I_def}
\Omega_I(Z_T, Z_P) \;:=\; \frac{\operatorname{Tr}(Z_T^\top Z_P)}{\|Z_T\|_F \, \|Z_P\|_F},
\end{equation}
for two reasons. First, both of our primary instantiations satisfy $W^*\approx I$: post-training quantization preserves the coordinate basis by construction, while LoRA fine-tuning with a frozen head empirically produces only small rotational drift in the backbone. Second, $\Omega_I \le \Omega$ (Appendix~\ref{app:trace_specialization}), so substituting $\Omega_I$ into Theorem~\ref{thm:unified_bound} yields a \emph{conservative upper bound} on the nuclear-form bound---the tight nuclear form remains the reference theoretical statement, while $\Omega_I$ provides an SVD-free, autograd-compatible operational form. Unless stated otherwise, $\Omega$ in the sequel refers to $\Omega_I$.


\subsection{Extension to Autoregressive Generation}
\label{subsec:ar_extension}

The framework above treats each evaluation as a single feature-to-loss mapping. We now show that it extends naturally to sequence-level autoregressive generation, unifying both point-wise classification and language modeling under a single bound.

\paragraph{Autoregressive Risk.}
For a sequence $(c, y)$ drawn from $\mathcal{D}$, where $c$ denotes the context (prompt) and $y = (y_1, \ldots, y_{|y|})$ the target continuation, the autoregressive risk is:
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:ar_risk}
\mathcal{R}_M^{\mathrm{AR}} = \mathbb{E}_{(c, y) \sim \mathcal{D}} \left[ \frac{1}{|y|} \sum_{t=1}^{|y|} \ell\!\big(\phi_M(c, y_{<t}) \cdot H_M,\; y_t\big) \right],
\end{equation}
where $\phi_M(c, y_{<t}) \in \mathbb{R}^d$ is the feature vector at position $t$, encoding the context $c$ and all preceding target tokens $y_{<t}$.

\paragraph{From Tokens to Matrices.}
Under teacher forcing, all token-level features $\{\phi_M(c, y_{<t})\}_{t=1}^{|y|}$ are extracted in a single forward pass---each token contributes an independent feature-loss pair to the average. Collecting all $N = \sum_{(c,y)} |y|$ token-level features into a single matrix $Z_M^{\mathrm{AR}} \in \mathbb{R}^{N \times d}$, we apply Theorem~\ref{thm:unified_bound} directly:

\begin{corollary}[Autoregressive Risk Bound]
\label{cor:ar_bound}
Let $Z_T^{\mathrm{AR}}, Z_P^{\mathrm{AR}} \in \mathbb{R}^{N \times d}$ be the token-level feature matrices collected under teacher forcing. Then:
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:ar_bound}
|\mathcal{R}_T^{\mathrm{AR}} - \mathcal{R}_P^{\mathrm{AR}}| \;\le\; K_{\mathrm{feat}} \sqrt{(\rho_T^{\scriptscriptstyle\mathrm{AR}} - \rho_P^{\scriptscriptstyle\mathrm{AR}})^2 + 2\,\rho_T^{\scriptscriptstyle\mathrm{AR}}\,\rho_P^{\scriptscriptstyle\mathrm{AR}}\!\big(1 - \Omega^{\mathrm{AR}}\big)} + K_{\mathrm{pred}} \,\big\|\Sigma_P^{\mathrm{AR}\,1/2}(W^* H_T - H_P)\big\|_F,
\end{equation}
where $\rho^{\mathrm{AR}}$, $\Omega^{\mathrm{AR}}$, and $\Sigma_P^{\mathrm{AR}}$ are computed over the token-level matrices $Z^{\mathrm{AR}}$.
\end{corollary}


\subsection{Shape Regularization as a Training Objective}
\label{subsec:shape_reg}

Theorem~\ref{thm:unified_bound} is typically used \emph{diagnostically}---computed post-hoc to compare trained variants. The same quantity can instead serve as a \emph{training-time penalty} that actively constrains geometric drift. Under LoRA fine-tuning with a frozen prediction head ($H_T = H_P$), the head term of the unified bound vanishes and the risk gap reduces to
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:lora_bound}
|\mathcal{R}_0 - \mathcal{R}_t| \;\le\; K_{\mathrm{feat}}\sqrt{(\rho_0-\rho_t)^2 + 2\rho_0\rho_t(1-\Omega)},
\end{equation}
where $t$ indexes the current fine-tuning checkpoint relative to the frozen base model $\theta_0$. Because $1-\Omega$ enters linearly under the square root, minimizing it during training directly shrinks the bound. We therefore augment the fine-tuning objective with a PRISM-guided regularizer:
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:shape_reg}
L_{\mathrm{total}} \;=\; \underbrace{L_{\mathrm{CE}}\bigl(\theta_t;\,\mathcal{D}_{\mathrm{FT}}\bigr)}_{\text{task loss}} \;+\; \lambda\,\bigl(1 - \Omega_I\!\bigl(Z_0^{\text{ref}},\, Z_t^{\text{ref}}\bigr)\bigr),
\end{equation}
where $Z_0^{\text{ref}}, Z_t^{\text{ref}}$ are the base and current feature matrices on a fixed reference set and $\Omega_I$ is the trace specialization of Eq.~(\ref{eq:omega_I_def}). $Z_0^{\text{ref}}$ is pre-computed once with the frozen base model; $Z_t^{\text{ref}}$ is recomputed every $k$ micro-batches during training, adding negligible wall-clock overhead at 8B scale.

\paragraph{Why $\Omega_I$ at training time.}
Three properties make $\Omega_I$ strictly preferable to $\Omega$ inside a training loop: (a)~\emph{Differentiability}---$\Omega_I$ is a smooth function of $Z_t$ and back-propagates through standard autograd with no SVD; (b)~\emph{Safety}---$\Omega_I \le \Omega$ (Appendix~\ref{app:trace_specialization}), so $(1-\Omega_I)$ upper-bounds $(1-\Omega)$ and minimizing the former also shrinks the tight nuclear-form bound; (c)~\emph{Regime match}---LoRA with a frozen head keeps the backbone near its base coordinate basis ($W^*\approx I$), making $\Omega_I \approx \Omega$ in practice. This lifts the bound from a post-hoc diagnostic to an active geometric constraint; its empirical effect is validated in Sec.~\ref{subsec:shape_reg_exp}.


\section{Instantiating the Framework}
\label{sec:applications}

The Unified Risk Bound (Theorem~\ref{thm:unified_bound}) provides a general mechanism to estimate functional divergence between any two models. The nature of the geometric relationship between proxy and target determines which terms dominate the bound, giving rise to two distinct regimes:
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{The Near-Identity Regime ($W^* \approx I$):} When the proxy and target share a nearly identical coordinate basis---as in post-training quantization---the optimal Procrustes alignment $W^*$ absorbs only small perturbations introduced by numerical noise. The bound reduces to a pure function of scale collapse ($\Delta\rho$) and structural distortion ($1 - \Omega$), with the head discrepancy vanishing when the prediction head is preserved.
    \item \textbf{The Rotational Regime ($W^* \in \mathcal{O}(d), W^* \not\approx I$):} When training dynamics induce global basis rotations---as in fine-tuning---the full Procrustes alignment is required. The optimal $W^*$ absorbs arbitrary rotations, and $\Omega$ measures the residual geometric drift that rotations alone cannot explain.
\end{itemize}
We instantiate these two regimes below; a pointwise variance analysis that underwrites single-pass variant comparison follows in Sec.~\ref{subsec:benchmark_calibration}.

\subsection{Quantization Quality Estimation}
\label{subsec:quantization}

We designate the full-precision model as Target ($T$) and the quantized variant as Proxy ($P$). Two conditions specific to post-training quantization simplify the bound:
\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{Near-Identity Alignment ($W^* \approx I$):} Standard PTQ introduces element-wise numerical noise that largely preserves the coordinate basis. The optimal Procrustes alignment $W^*$ absorbs the small, non-symmetric perturbations in the cross-moment matrix $Z_F^\top Z_Q$, and the Procrustes Similarity $\Omega$ (Eq.~\ref{eq:omega_def}) remains the correct measure of structural alignment.\footnote{%
    Setting $W = I$ exactly would replace the nuclear norm $\|Z_F^\top Z_Q\|_*$ in the decomposition with $\operatorname{Tr}(Z_F^\top Z_Q)$, which equals the nuclear norm only when $Z_F^\top Z_Q$ is symmetric positive semidefinite---a condition not guaranteed under quantisation noise. Using the full Procrustes solution avoids this issue while yielding the tightest bound. See Appendix~\ref{app:general_W} for a detailed analysis of alternative alignment choices.}
    \item \textbf{Head Stability ($H_Q \approx H_F$):} Modern protocols (GPTQ, and typical GGUF / BitsAndBytes configurations) retain the prediction head in FP16, causing the head discrepancy to vanish.
\end{enumerate}
Under these conditions, the risk gap is dominated purely by the geometric deformation of the feature manifold:
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:quant_bound}
|\mathcal{R}_F - \mathcal{R}_Q| \;\;\lessapprox\;\; K_{\mathrm{feat}} \sqrt{ \underbrace{(\rho_F - \rho_Q)^2}_{\text{\rm Scale Collapse}} + \underbrace{2\rho_F \rho_Q \big(1 - \Omega(Z_F, Z_Q)\big)}_{\text{\rm Structural Distortion}} }.
\end{equation}
This establishes $\Omega$ and $\Delta\rho$ as generation-free diagnostics: \emph{scale collapse} ($\rho_Q < \rho_F$) captures activation clipping from aggressive bit-width reduction, while a drop in $\Omega$ signals corruption of the relational geometry between tokens. Practitioners can rank quantization configurations purely from feature statistics on a small calibration set.


\subsection{Geometric Monitoring of Catastrophic Forgetting}
\label{subsec:forgetting}

We define the frozen base model as Target ($T = \theta_0$) and the model at fine-tuning step $t$ as Proxy ($P = \theta_t$). Unlike quantization---where $W^*$ deviates only slightly from the identity---fine-tuning induces substantial global feature rotations, requiring the full Procrustes alignment. Applying Theorem~\ref{thm:unified_bound}:
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:forgetting_bound}
\begin{aligned}
\text{Forgetting}(t) \;\;\le\;\; & \underbrace{K_{\mathrm{feat}} \sqrt{ (\rho_0 - \rho_t)^2 + 2\rho_0 \rho_t \big( 1 - \Omega(Z_0, Z_t) \big) }}_{\text{\rm Backbone Geometric Drift}} \\
& + \underbrace{K_{\mathrm{pred}} \cdot \| \Sigma_t^{1/2} (W^* H_0 - H_t) \|_F}_{\text{\rm Head Divergence}}.
\end{aligned}
\end{equation}
Under standard LoRA fine-tuning~\cite{hu2022lora}, low-rank updates are applied only to attention and MLP projection matrices ($W_q, W_k, W_v, W_o, W_{\mathrm{up}}, W_{\mathrm{down}}$), while the \texttt{lm\_head} remains frozen ($H_t = H_0$). The head divergence term therefore vanishes, and performance degradation is governed \emph{entirely} by backbone geometric drift ($\Delta\rho$ and $\Omega$). This provides a label-free diagnostic for forgetting that decouples manifold distortion from head-specific effects.


\subsection{Per-Sample Coupling and Single-Pass Variant Comparison}
\label{subsec:benchmark_calibration}

Theorem~\ref{thm:unified_bound} controls the \emph{aggregate} risk gap; variant-level comparison in practice also requires a \emph{per-sample} statement. Before taking expectations, the Lipschitz analysis underlying Thm.~\ref{thm:unified_bound} yields a pointwise bound:
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:pointwise_bound}
|\Delta\ell(x)| \;\le\; K_{\mathrm{feat}} \|\phi_T(x) - \phi_P(x) W^*\|_2 \;+\; K_{\mathrm{pred}} \|\phi_P(x) \cdot \Delta H\|_2 \;\le\; B,
\end{equation}
where $\Delta\ell(x) = \ell_T(x) - \ell_P(x)$ and $B$ is the worst-case per-sample residual on the calibration set. Since $\Delta\ell(x) \in [-B, B]$, Popoviciu's inequality gives $\mathrm{Var}(\Delta\ell) \le B^2$. When $\Omega \approx 1$ and $\Delta\rho \approx 0$, the per-sample residuals are uniformly small, so the signed difference $\mathcal{R}_T(\mathcal{D}) - \mathcal{R}_P(\mathcal{D})$ is a stable, low-variance quantity on any benchmark $\mathcal{D}$: a single forward pass per model over $\mathcal{D}$ yields a reliable variant-level comparison. This underwrites both downstream diagnostics---quantization ranking from $(\Delta\rho, \Omega)$ on a calibration benchmark and a label-free drift signal for catastrophic-forgetting detection---whose evaluation protocol (including the use of teacher-forced cross-entropy, its runtime/variance benefits, and its scope relative to generation-based metrics) is detailed in Sec.~\ref{subsec:exp_setup}.


\subsection{Other Applications}
\label{subsec:other_applications}

Beyond the two primary regimes, Theorem~\ref{thm:unified_bound} supports several additional diagnostic uses that we leave to future empirical validation: (i)~\emph{OOD detection} via the per-sample residual $\|\phi_T(x) - \phi_P(x) W^*\|_2$, which spikes when the linear alignment hypothesis breaks on out-of-distribution inputs; (ii)~\emph{cross-scale hyperparameter transfer}, since a small proxy with high $\Omega$ to a large target bounds the regret of transferring its optimal setting; and (iii)~\emph{label-free production monitoring}, since $\Omega$ and $\Delta\rho$ depend only on feature matrices and can serve as online drift signals without ground-truth labels.

\section{Experiments}
\label{sec:experiments}

\subsection{Experimental Setup}
\label{subsec:exp_setup}

\paragraph{Models.}
Our experiments span two regimes with different model coverage. For \textbf{quantization} (Sec.~\ref{subsec:quant_exp}) we evaluate four 8B open-weight models spanning the major current lineages: Llama-3.1-8B~\cite{grattafiori2024llama}, Ministral-3-8B, Qwen3-8B~\cite{qwen2025qwen3}, and DeepSeek-R1-Distill-Llama-8B, giving cross-family evidence that the bound is not a per-lineage artefact. For \textbf{fine-tuning forgetting and shape regularization} (Secs.~\ref{subsec:forget_exp}--\ref{subsec:shape_reg_exp}) we restrict the main text to two models---Llama-3.1-8B and Qwen3-8B---with Qwen results in Appendix~\ref{app:qwen_forgetting}. This pair controls for four variables that could otherwise confound a forgetting study: (i)~pre-training corpus (Meta $\sim$15T tokens, English-dominant vs.\ Alibaba $\sim$36T tokens, multilingual), testing cross-lineage robustness of the Linear Representation Hypothesis; (ii)~depth (32 vs.\ 36 layers at fixed hidden dim $d{=}4096$), isolating depth effects on $\Omega_I$; (iii)~vocabulary size ($128{,}256$ vs.\ $151{,}936$), which enters $K_{\mathrm{feat}}=\max_{j,k}\|h_{j}-h_{k}\|_2$ directly and would expose any dependence of the bound on head geometry; and (iv)~tokenizer family (BPE vs.\ byte-level BPE). We exclude DeepSeek-R1-Distill (reasoning-distilled, atypical LoRA dynamics) and Ministral (multimodal architecture with nested language-model backbone) from the forgetting study because their fine-tuning behaviour deviates from standard practice.

\paragraph{Quantization Protocols.}
We test three PTQ families across bit-widths 2--8: \textbf{GGUF} (round-to-nearest, \texttt{Q2\_K} through \texttt{Q8\_0}), \textbf{GPTQ}~\cite{frantar2022gptq} (4-bit, second-order reconstruction), and \textbf{BitsAndBytes}~\cite{dettmers2022gpt3} (INT8, NF4, FP4). This covers the spectrum from basic rounding to calibration-based compensation.

\paragraph{Fine-Tuning Tasks.}
LoRA fine-tuning is performed on two complementary tasks chosen to elicit distinct forgetting profiles. \textbf{TruthfulQA} is a truthfulness-oriented QA set designed to trigger plausible-but-wrong generations, stressing safety-critical factual grounding. \textbf{BBQ} (Bias Benchmark for QA) measures bias-sensitive reasoning through ambiguous social-context questions. The two are complementary---TruthfulQA perturbs factual behaviour, BBQ perturbs reasoning under social bias---so downstream forgetting on general-knowledge benchmarks (ARC, MMLU, SQuAD, TriviaQA, GSM8K) provides a non-trivial stress test of whether PRISM tracks drift across task families, not just within a single domain.

\paragraph{Benchmarks.}
Following the autoregressive risk formulation (Eq.~\ref{eq:ar_risk}), we organize benchmarks by the partition between context $c$ and target $y$:
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Multiple-Choice (MC)} --- MMLU, ARC: $c$ is the full prompt including all choices, $y$ is the answer label token (a single token per question). We extract the final prompt token's feature and compare logit scores over the candidate labels, reducing the task to single-token classification.
    \item \textbf{Language Modeling (LM)} --- WikiText, FineWeb-Edu: $c$ is empty or a rolling prefix, $y$ is a contiguous text chunk. Risk is the standard sequence-level cross-entropy.
    \item \textbf{Short-Horizon Generation (QA)} --- TriviaQA, SQuAD: $c$ is the question prompt, $y$ is the answer span (1--5 tokens). Loss is evaluated strictly over answer tokens.
\end{itemize}

\paragraph{Shape-Regularization Hyperparameters.}
For Sec.~\ref{subsec:shape_reg_exp} we sweep $\lambda \in \{0.0,\, 0.1,\, 0.5,\, 1.0\}$, where $\lambda{=}0$ reproduces unregularized LoRA. The reference set used to compute $\Omega_I$ is a fixed held-out batch of $32$ sequences drawn from the base model's pre-training distribution (not from $\mathcal{D}_{\mathrm{FT}}$, so that the penalty and task loss do not directly interfere). $Z_t^{\text{ref}}$ is recomputed every $k{=}8$ micro-steps; checkpoints are saved every 50 training steps, and all analysis figures/tables align to step $300$.

\paragraph{Teacher-Forced Scoring Protocol.}
All risks in this paper are computed in the teacher-forced regime: the prompt $c$ and the target tokens $y$ are concatenated and passed through the model once, and the per-token loss is read off at the target positions. This matches the scoring rules of the benchmarks above by construction---multiple-choice items are ranked by gold-label likelihood, perplexity suites are teacher-forced by definition, and short-answer QA is evaluated on the likelihood of the gold span---so no additional evaluation assumption is introduced. The protocol yields three concrete benefits relevant to variant-level diagnostics.
(a)~\textbf{Runtime.} A single parallel forward pass replaces token-by-token conditional decoding, avoiding the quadratic KV-cache growth and repeated sampling of autoregressive evaluation; for MMLU-scale benchmarks this collapses hours of generation into one pass per model.
(b)~\textbf{Training--evaluation consistency.} Teacher-forced cross-entropy is also the objective used during LoRA fine-tuning in our experiments, so our calibration quantity and the fine-tuning loss live on exactly the same scale, making variant-level drift measurements directly commensurate with training signals---a property that decoding-based metrics (BLEU, pass@$k$) cannot match.
(c)~\textbf{Low variance.} Removing the sampling stage eliminates temperature, top-$k$, and nucleus randomness, producing deterministic per-sample residuals and, together with the per-sample coupling of Sec.~\ref{subsec:benchmark_calibration}, tight confidence intervals for the bound.
We do not claim teacher-forced cross-entropy substitutes for generation-based evaluation in open-ended tasks; PRISM is positioned as a rapid variant-ranking signal complementary to full generative evaluation.

This separation between scale and shape channels, made explicit by our decomposition, is not visible in a single-number similarity metric.

\paragraph{Which axis fails where.}
The decomposition isolates three qualitatively distinct PTQ failure modes in our data, each dominated by a different axis of Theorem~\ref{thm:unified_bound}. \emph{Shape distortion} dominates the majority of aggressive quantization configurations: at Q2 and Q3 across all four families, the shape term $2\rho_T\rho_P(1{-}\Omega_I)$ outweighs the scale term $(\Delta\rho)^2$ by two to three orders of magnitude (e.g., Llama-Q2\_K on MMLU: $24$ vs.~$\sim$$9{,}000$; Ministral-Q2\_K on MMLU: $\sim$$9$ vs.~$\sim$$11{,}000$)---consistent with low-bit PTQ corrupting the relational structure of the feature manifold rather than its global scale. \emph{Scale collapse} is rare but unambiguous when it occurs: Qwen3-8B under GPTQ-4bit halves $\rho_P$ to $162$ from $\rho_T{=}332$ on MMLU and to $133$ from $\rho_T{=}267$ on GSM8K, driving $(\Delta\rho)^2$ past the shape arm by $1.2\text{--}2.3\times$, whereas the same GPTQ-4bit protocol leaves $\rho$ invariant on Llama and DeepSeek ($\Delta\rho<3$ throughout)---a lineage-specific calibration pathology that the scale axis localizes precisely. \emph{Head divergence} isolates a third regime entirely: at Qwen3-Q6\_K and Qwen3-Q8\_0, backbone scale and shape are preserved to within $(\Delta\rho)^2{<}1$ and $\Omega_I{>}0.9995$, yet the quantized output embedding alone contributes $\gamma\approx 60$, making $\gamma$ the sole residual error source. Each dominant axis suggests a different remediation---per-channel outlier smoothing~\cite{xiao2023smoothquant} for scale collapse, Hessian-aware reconstruction for shape distortion, and FP16-head retention for head divergence---turning the bound from a scalar score into a causal diagnostic.

\paragraph{Feature term vs.\ full bound.}
Averaged across the $4\times 5$ grid, the feature-only term $\delta$ attains Spearman $\overline{r_s}(\delta)\!\approx\!\textsc{82.35}$, marginally above the full bound's $\overline{r_s}(\mathcal{B})\!\approx\!\textsc{78.5}$.%
\footnote{Exact per-subplot $r_s$ values are annotated in Fig.~\ref{fig:quant_grid_bound} and in Appendix~\ref{app:feature_only}; aggregate means are reported to one decimal place.}
The small gap is a regime artefact of our PTQ suite: GPTQ and BitsAndBytes keep the \texttt{lm\_head} in FP16 (so $\gamma \equiv 0$ by construction), whereas some GGUF configurations quantize the output embedding and yield $\gamma > 0$. Mixing these two regimes in a single scatter causes the $\gamma$ term to add non-monotone variance in the x-coordinate, slightly diluting rank correlation on $\mathcal{B}$ relative to $\delta$. Both quantities remain theoretically meaningful---$\mathcal{B}$ is the tight upper bound of Theorem~\ref{thm:unified_bound} that the head-free $\delta$ dominates only when $\gamma$ happens to vanish---and confirm that PTQ degradation is dominated by scale collapse $(\Delta\rho)^2$ and shape mismatch $(1-\Omega_I)$, as predicted by Sec.~\ref{subsec:quantization}. Because the head is frozen, all of this signal originates in the backbone---the drift of $\Omega_I(Z_0, Z_t)$ away from $1$ and of $\rho_t$ away from $\rho_0$. Two structural observations follow. First, \emph{different source tasks induce qualitatively different drift geometries}: TruthfulQA fine-tuning drives large shape drift with modest scale change, while BBQ fine-tuning moves both; the decomposition in Eq.~(\ref{eq:lora_bound}) makes this distinction visible, whereas a unified distance would collapse it. \section{Conclusion and Future Work}
\label{sec:conclusion}

We presented PRISM, a unified geometric framework that establishes a rigorous, computable link between representational alignment and functional risk. By formulating alignment as an Orthogonal Procrustes problem and deriving an exact decomposition via the nuclear norm, we obtained a closed-form bound on $|\mathcal{R}_T - \mathcal{R}_P|$ that separates scale collapse ($\Delta\rho$), structural distortion ($1 - \Omega$), and head divergence as independently measurable diagnostic quantities.

We instantiated this framework for two critical LLM lifecycle stages---post-training quantization and catastrophic forgetting during fine-tuning---and showed that evaluating the bound on standard LLM benchmarks yields a low-variance, mechanism-level diagnostic for variant ranking that is commensurate with the fine-tuning objective and whose dominant axis localizes distinct failure modes (shape distortion, scale collapse, or head divergence) to their respective remediations.

\paragraph{Limitations and Future Work.}
Several promising directions remain:
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Dynamic Trajectory Alignment:} Our current formulation validates static (prefill) feature manifolds. Extending to multi-step generation trajectories would enable proxy estimation for long Chain-of-Thought reasoning and agentic workflows.
    \item \textbf{Cross-Modal Generalization:} The Linear Representation Hypothesis extends beyond text. Investigating PRISM's applicability to vision transformers and cross-modal models (e.g., CLIP) remains a natural next step.
\end{itemize}

\section{Proof of the Unified Risk Bound (Theorem~\ref{thm:unified_bound})}
\label{app:detailed_proofs}

We provide the complete proof of Theorem~\ref{thm:unified_bound}, deriving the bound on $|\mathcal{R}_T - \mathcal{R}_P|$ from the cross-entropy loss definition. The proof proceeds through five self-contained steps: risk decomposition via triangle inequality, Lipschitz analysis of cross-entropy with respect to features, exact Procrustes decomposition via SVD, second-moment-adjusted head bound, and final assembly.

\subsection{Step 1: Risk Decomposition via Triangle Inequality}
\label{app:risk_decomp}

\paragraph{Setup.}
Recall that the risk of model $M$ is $\mathcal{R}_M = \mathbb{E}_{(x,y)}[\ell(\phi_M(x) \cdot H_M, y)]$, where $\ell$ is the cross-entropy loss (Eq.~\ref{eq:ce_def}). Given the optimal orthogonal alignment $W^* \in \mathcal{O}(d)$ that maps proxy features toward target features (i.e., $\phi_T(x) \approx \phi_P(x) W^*$), we introduce an intermediate \emph{hybrid risk}:
\begin{equation}
\mathcal{R}_{P \to T} := \mathbb{E}_{(x,y)}\!\Big[\ell\!\big(\phi_P(x)\, W^* \cdot H_T,\; y\big)\Big].
\end{equation}
This hybrid term evaluates the \emph{target's head} $H_T$ on the \emph{proxy's aligned features} $\phi_P(x) W$, serving as a bridge between $\mathcal{R}_T$ and $\mathcal{R}_P$.

\paragraph{Decomposition.}
By the triangle inequality on absolute values:
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:app_triangle}
|\mathcal{R}_T - \mathcal{R}_P| \;\le\; \underbrace{\big|\mathcal{R}_T - \mathcal{R}_{P \to T}\big|}_{\text{Feature Alignment Error } \delta} \;+\; \underbrace{\big|\mathcal{R}_{P \to T} - \mathcal{R}_P\big|}_{\text{Head Discrepancy } \gamma}.
\end{equation}
The first term $\delta$ measures how much the \emph{features} differ (through the same head $H_T$); the second term $\gamma$ measures how much the \emph{heads} differ (on the same aligned features). We bound each term separately in the following subsections.


\subsection{Step 2: Bounding the Feature Alignment Error $\delta$}
\label{app:kfeat}

\paragraph{Lipschitz Property of Cross-Entropy with Respect to Features.}
We bound $\delta$ by showing that the cross-entropy loss is Lipschitz continuous in the feature vector $z$. Define the composition $g_T(z, y) := \ell(z \cdot H_T, y)$. Our goal is to find the tightest constant $K_{\mathrm{feat}}$ such that:
\begin{equation}
|g_T(z_1, y) - g_T(z_2, y)| \le K_{\mathrm{feat}} \|z_1 - z_2\|_2, \quad \forall z_1, z_2, y.
\end{equation}

\paragraph{Computing the Gradient.}
The logit vector is $v = z \cdot H_T \in \mathbb{R}^V$, and the cross-entropy loss is $\ell(v, y) = -v_y + \log \sum_{j=1}^{V} e^{v_j}$. The gradient with respect to the logits is:
\begin{equation}
\frac{\partial \ell}{\partial v} = \hat{p} - e_y,
\end{equation}
where $\hat{p} = \mathrm{softmax}(v) \in \mathbb{R}^V$ and $e_y$ is the one-hot vector at index $y$. Applying the chain rule to obtain the gradient with respect to features:
\begin{equation}
\nabla_z \ell = H_T (\hat{p} - e_y) = \sum_{j=1}^{V} (\hat{p}_j - \delta_{jy})\, h_{T,j},
\end{equation}
where $h_{T,j}$ denotes the $j$-th column of $H_T$ (the embedding vector for token $j$).

\paragraph{Simplex Polarization.}
We now derive a tight bound on $\|\nabla_z \ell\|_2$ by exploiting the probability simplex constraint $\sum_j \hat{p}_j = 1$. Expanding the gradient for true class $y$:
\begin{equation}
\nabla_z \ell = (\hat{p}_y - 1)\, h_{T,y} + \sum_{j \neq y} \hat{p}_j\, h_{T,j}.
\end{equation}
Since $1 - \hat{p}_y = \sum_{j \neq y} \hat{p}_j$, we can rewrite:
\begin{equation}
\nabla_z \ell = -\Big(\sum_{j \neq y} \hat{p}_j\Big) h_{T,y} + \sum_{j \neq y} \hat{p}_j\, h_{T,j} = \sum_{j \neq y} \hat{p}_j\, (h_{T,j} - h_{T,y}).
\end{equation}
This reveals that the gradient is a \emph{convex combination} of the pairwise differences between incorrect-class embeddings and the correct-class embedding. Taking the norm:
\begin{equation}
\|\nabla_z \ell\|_2 \le \sum_{j \neq y} \hat{p}_j \|h_{T,j} - h_{T,y}\|_2 \le \max_{j \neq y} \|h_{T,j} - h_{T,y}\|_2 \cdot \underbrace{\sum_{j \neq y} \hat{p}_j}_{\le\, 1}.
\end{equation}
Taking the maximum over all possible true classes $y$:
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:kfeat_tight}
K_{\mathrm{feat}} = \max_{j,k} \|h_{T,j} - h_{T,k}\|_2.
\end{equation}

\paragraph{Remark.} This bound depends on the \emph{relative distances} between token embeddings, not their absolute magnitudes. A uniform shift $H_T \to H_T + c\mathbf{1}^\top$ does not change $K_{\mathrm{feat}}$. A naive Cauchy--Schwarz bound gives $K_{\mathrm{feat}}^{\mathrm{naive}} = \sqrt{2}\|H_T\|_2$, which is substantially looser.

\paragraph{Applying the Lipschitz Bound.}
Using the Lipschitz property with $z_1 = \phi_T(x)$ and $z_2 = \phi_P(x) W^*$:
\begin{equation}
\begin{aligned}
\delta &= \big|\mathcal{R}_T - \mathcal{R}_{P \to T}\big| = \Big|\mathbb{E}\big[g_T(\phi_T(x), y) - g_T(\phi_P(x) W^*, y)\big]\Big| \\
&\le \mathbb{E}\Big[|g_T(\phi_T(x), y) - g_T(\phi_P(x) W^*, y)|\Big] \\
&\le K_{\mathrm{feat}} \cdot \mathbb{E}_x\!\big[\|\phi_T(x) - \phi_P(x) W^*\|_2\big].
\end{aligned}
\end{equation}

To connect this expectation to the Procrustes decomposition, we apply Jensen's inequality ($\mathbb{E}[\|X\|] \le \sqrt{\mathbb{E}[\|X\|^2]}$):
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:delta_bound}
\delta \le K_{\mathrm{feat}} \cdot \sqrt{\mathbb{E}_x\!\big[\|\phi_T(x) - \phi_P(x) W^*\|_2^2\big]} = K_{\mathrm{feat}} \cdot \sqrt{\frac{1}{n}\|Z_T - Z_P W^*\|_F^2}.
\end{equation}



\subsection{Step 3: Exact Geometric Decomposition via Procrustes Analysis}
\label{app:procrustes}

We now derive the closed-form solution for the Orthogonal Procrustes problem and prove Proposition~\ref{prop:exact_decomposition}.

\paragraph{The Optimization Problem.}
We seek the orthogonal matrix $W^*$ that minimizes the alignment residual:
\begin{equation}
W^* = \arg\min_{W \in \mathcal{O}(d)} \|Z_T - Z_P W\|_F^2.
\end{equation}

\paragraph{Expansion.} Expanding the squared Frobenius norm:
\begin{equation}
\begin{aligned}
\|Z_T - Z_P W\|_F^2 &= \operatorname{Tr}\!\big[(Z_T - Z_P W)^\top (Z_T - Z_P W)\big] \\
&= \|Z_T\|_F^2 + \|Z_P W\|_F^2 - 2\operatorname{Tr}(Z_T^\top Z_P W).
\end{aligned}
\end{equation}
Since $W$ is orthogonal, it preserves the Frobenius norm: $\|Z_P W\|_F^2 = \operatorname{Tr}(W^\top Z_P^\top Z_P W) = \operatorname{Tr}(Z_P^\top Z_P) = \|Z_P\|_F^2$, where we used the cyclic property of trace and $W^\top W = I_d$. Therefore:
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:procrustes_expand}
\|Z_T - Z_P W\|_F^2 = \|Z_T\|_F^2 + \|Z_P\|_F^2 - 2\operatorname{Tr}(Z_T^\top Z_P W).
\end{equation}
The first two terms are constants independent of $W$. Minimization thus reduces to:
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:procrustes_max}
W^* = \arg\max_{W \in \mathcal{O}(d)} \operatorname{Tr}(Z_T^\top Z_P W).
\end{equation}

\paragraph{SVD Solution.}
Let $Z_T^\top Z_P = U \Sigma V^\top$ be the singular value decomposition, where $U, V \in \mathbb{R}^{d \times d}$ are orthogonal and $\Sigma = \mathrm{diag}(\sigma_1, \ldots, \sigma_d)$ with $\sigma_i \ge 0$. Substituting:
\begin{equation}
\operatorname{Tr}(Z_T^\top Z_P W) = \operatorname{Tr}(U \Sigma V^\top W) = \operatorname{Tr}(\Sigma V^\top W U) = \operatorname{Tr}(\Sigma R),
\end{equation}
where $R = V^\top W U$. Since $W \in \mathcal{O}(d)$ and $U, V \in \mathcal{O}(d)$, we have $R \in \mathcal{O}(d)$. The problem becomes:
\begin{equation}
\max_{R \in \mathcal{O}(d)} \operatorname{Tr}(\Sigma R) = \max_{R \in \mathcal{O}(d)} \sum_{i=1}^{d} \sigma_i R_{ii}.
\end{equation}
Since $\sigma_i \ge 0$ and $|R_{ii}| \le 1$ for any orthogonal $R$, this is maximized when $R_{ii} = 1$ for all $i$, i.e., $R = I_d$, which yields $V^\top W^* U = I_d$ and thus:
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:W_optimal}
W^* = V U^\top.
\end{equation}
The maximum value is:
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:max_trace}
\max_{W \in \mathcal{O}(d)} \operatorname{Tr}(Z_T^\top Z_P W) = \sum_{i=1}^{d} \sigma_i = \|Z_T^\top Z_P\|_*,
\end{equation}
where $\|\cdot\|_*$ denotes the nuclear norm.

\paragraph{Substitution and Decomposition.}
Substituting Eq.~(\ref{eq:max_trace}) into Eq.~(\ref{eq:procrustes_expand}) and dividing by $n$:
\begin{equation}
\min_{W \in \mathcal{O}(d)} \frac{1}{n}\|Z_T - Z_P W\|_F^2 = \frac{1}{n}\|Z_T\|_F^2 + \frac{1}{n}\|Z_P\|_F^2 - \frac{2}{n}\|Z_T^\top Z_P\|_*.
\end{equation}
Recalling $\rho_M = \frac{1}{\sqrt{n}}\|Z_M\|_F$ (so $\frac{1}{n}\|Z_M\|_F^2 = \rho_M^2$) and the definition of $\Omega$ (Eq.~\ref{eq:omega_def}):
\begin{equation}
\frac{1}{n}\|Z_T^\top Z_P\|_* = \frac{\|Z_T^\top Z_P\|_*}{\|Z_T\|_F\|Z_P\|_F} \cdot \frac{\|Z_T\|_F\|Z_P\|_F}{n} = \Omega \cdot \rho_T \rho_P.
\end{equation}
Therefore:
\begin{equation}
\min_{W \in \mathcal{O}(d)} \frac{1}{n}\|Z_T - Z_P W\|_F^2 = \rho_T^2 + \rho_P^2 - 2\,\Omega\,\rho_T\rho_P.
\end{equation}
Adding and subtracting $2\rho_T\rho_P$ completes the square:
\begin{equation}
= (\rho_T^2 - 2\rho_T\rho_P + \rho_P^2) + 2\rho_T\rho_P - 2\,\Omega\,\rho_T\rho_P = \underbrace{(\rho_T - \rho_P)^2}_{\text{Scale Mismatch}} + \underbrace{2\rho_T\rho_P(1 - \Omega)}_{\text{Shape Mismatch}}.
\end{equation}
This completes the proof of Proposition~\ref{prop:exact_decomposition}. \qed

\paragraph{Assembling the Feature Bound.}
Combining Eq.~(\ref{eq:delta_bound}) with Proposition~\ref{prop:exact_decomposition}:
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:delta_final}
\delta \le K_{\mathrm{feat}} \sqrt{(\rho_T - \rho_P)^2 + 2\rho_T\rho_P(1 - \Omega)}.
\end{equation}



\subsection{Step 4: Bounding the Head Discrepancy $\gamma$}
\label{app:head_bound}

\paragraph{Lipschitz Property with Respect to Logits.}
The head discrepancy measures how much the predictions differ when two different ``effective heads'' ($W^* H_T$ vs.\ $H_P$) operate on the same proxy features. Let $z = \phi_P(x)$ denote the (unaligned) proxy feature. Then:
\begin{equation}
\gamma = \big|\mathcal{R}_{P \to T} - \mathcal{R}_P\big| = \Big|\mathbb{E}\!\big[\ell(z \cdot W^* H_T, y) - \ell(z \cdot H_P, y)\big]\Big|.
\end{equation}
To bound this, we use the Lipschitz property of cross-entropy with respect to \emph{logits}. The gradient of $\ell$ with respect to $v$ is $\nabla_v \ell = \hat{p} - e_y$, with norm:
\begin{equation}
\|\nabla_v \ell\|_2 = \|\hat{p} - e_y\|_2.
\end{equation}
Since $\hat{p}$ lies on the probability simplex, $\|\hat{p}\|_2^2 \le \|\hat{p}\|_1^2 = 1$, and therefore $\|\hat{p} - e_y\|_2^2 = \|\hat{p}\|_2^2 - 2\hat{p}_y + 1 \le 2 - 2\hat{p}_y \le 2$. The supremum $\sqrt{2}$ is approached as the model's predicted distribution concentrates on an incorrect class ($\hat{p}_y \to 0$). Thus $K_{\mathrm{pred}} \le \sqrt{2}$.

\paragraph{Deriving the Second-Moment Projection.}
Define the head misalignment matrix $\Delta H = W^* H_T - H_P \in \mathbb{R}^{d \times V}$. The logit difference for a single sample is $z \cdot \Delta H = \phi_P(x)(W^* H_T - H_P)$, and:
\begin{equation}
\begin{aligned}
\gamma &\le \mathbb{E}\!\big[|\ell(z \cdot W^* H_T, y) - \ell(z \cdot H_P, y)|\big] \\
&\le K_{\mathrm{pred}} \cdot \mathbb{E}_z\!\big[\|z \cdot \Delta H\|_2\big].
\end{aligned}
\end{equation}
Applying Jensen's inequality on the concave square root:
\begin{equation}
\big(\mathbb{E}[\|z \cdot \Delta H\|_2]\big)^2 \le \mathbb{E}\!\big[\|z \cdot \Delta H\|_2^2\big].
\end{equation}
Expanding the squared norm using the trace operator:
\begin{equation}
\begin{aligned}
\mathbb{E}\!\big[\|z \cdot \Delta H\|_2^2\big] &= \mathbb{E}\!\Big[\operatorname{Tr}\!\big(\Delta H^\top z^\top z\, \Delta H\big)\Big] \\
&= \operatorname{Tr}\!\Big(\Delta H^\top \underbrace{\mathbb{E}[z^\top z]}_{\Sigma_P} \Delta H\Big) \\
&= \|\Sigma_P^{1/2} \Delta H\|_F^2.
\end{aligned}
\end{equation}
Taking the square root:
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:gamma_final}
\gamma \le K_{\mathrm{pred}} \cdot \|\Sigma_P^{1/2}(W^* H_T - H_P)\|_F.
\end{equation}

\paragraph{Interpretation.} The second-moment weighting $\Sigma_P^{1/2}$ projects the head error onto the \emph{active subspace} of the data. If the heads disagree only in directions orthogonal to the feature distribution (the null space of $\Sigma_P$), this disagreement has zero cost---it does not affect any prediction. This is strictly tighter than the spectral bound $\gamma \le K_{\mathrm{pred}} \|W^* H_T - H_P\|_2 \cdot \mathbb{E}[\|z\|_2]$, which assumes worst-case alignment between features and head error.


\subsection{Step 5: Assembling the Unified Bound}
\label{app:assembly}

Combining the risk decomposition (Eq.~\ref{eq:app_triangle}), the feature bound (Eq.~\ref{eq:delta_final}), and the head bound (Eq.~\ref{eq:gamma_final}):
\begin{equation}
\begin{aligned}
|\mathcal{R}_T - \mathcal{R}_P| &\le \delta + \gamma \\
&\le K_{\mathrm{feat}} \sqrt{(\rho_T - \rho_P)^2 + 2\rho_T\rho_P(1 - \Omega)} + K_{\mathrm{pred}} \cdot \|\Sigma_P^{1/2}(W^* H_T - H_P)\|_F.
\end{aligned}
\end{equation}
This completes the proof of Theorem~\ref{thm:unified_bound}. \qed



\section{Tightness of Nuclear Norm over Frobenius Norm}
\label{app:tightness}

We show that the nuclear-norm-based Procrustes Similarity $\Omega$ yields a strictly tighter alignment residual than its Frobenius-based counterpart $\Omega_F$, and clarify the relationship to CKA.

\paragraph{Statement.}
Define the Frobenius-based similarity $\Omega_F := \|Z_T^\top Z_P\|_F / (\|Z_T\|_F \|Z_P\|_F)$. For any feature matrices $Z_T, Z_P$:
\begin{equation}
\Omega_F \le \Omega,
\end{equation}
with equality if and only if the cross-moment matrix $Z_T^\top Z_P$ has at most one nonzero singular value (i.e., rank $\le 1$).

\paragraph{Proof.}
Let $\sigma_1, \ldots, \sigma_d$ be the singular values of $Z_T^\top Z_P$. Then:
\begin{equation}
\|Z_T^\top Z_P\|_F = \sqrt{\sum_i \sigma_i^2}, \qquad \|Z_T^\top Z_P\|_* = \sum_i \sigma_i.
\end{equation}
By the Cauchy--Schwarz inequality applied to the vectors $(\sigma_1, \ldots, \sigma_d)$ and $(1, \ldots, 1)$:
\begin{equation}
\|Z_T^\top Z_P\|_F = \sqrt{\sum_i \sigma_i^2} \le \sum_i \sigma_i = \|Z_T^\top Z_P\|_*,
\end{equation}
since $\|\mathbf{x}\|_1 \ge \|\mathbf{x}\|_2$ for any non-negative vector. Dividing both sides by $\|Z_T\|_F \|Z_P\|_F$ gives $\Omega_F \le \Omega$.

\paragraph{Consequence for Risk Bounding.}
The alignment residual using $\Omega_F$ is:
\begin{equation}
(\rho_T - \rho_P)^2 + 2\rho_T\rho_P(1 - \Omega_F) \ge (\rho_T - \rho_P)^2 + 2\rho_T\rho_P(1 - \Omega).
\end{equation}
Therefore, substituting $\Omega_F$ into the unified bound produces a \emph{strictly looser} upper bound on risk discrepancy. The nuclear norm $\Omega$ is the tightest possible metric because it exactly solves the Procrustes optimization---any other norm-based similarity applied to the same alignment residual either underestimates the alignment or fails to correspond to a well-defined geometric minimization.

\paragraph{Relation to CKA.}
Linear CKA~\cite{kornblith2019similarity} is defined as $\mathrm{CKA}(Z_T, Z_P) = \|Z_T^\top Z_P\|_F^2 / (\|Z_T^\top Z_T\|_F \cdot \|Z_P^\top Z_P\|_F)$, which differs from $\Omega_F^2 = \|Z_T^\top Z_P\|_F^2 / (\|Z_T\|_F^2 \|Z_P\|_F^2)$ in its denominator. The two denominators are related by the inequality $\|Z_M^\top Z_M\|_F \le \|Z_M\|_F^2 = \operatorname{Tr}(Z_M^\top Z_M)$ (since $\|A\|_F \le \operatorname{Tr}(A)$ for any positive semidefinite $A$, by Cauchy--Schwarz on the eigenvalues), which gives $\mathrm{CKA} \ge \Omega_F^2$. Hence CKA can overestimate alignment relative to $\Omega_F$, and it is not directly substitutable into our Procrustes residual. More fundamentally, CKA is not derived from any alignment optimization and therefore lacks the operational meaning of $\Omega$, which exactly solves the Orthogonal Procrustes problem.

\section{Extension to General Orthogonal Alignments}
\label{app:general_W}

The main text (Theorem~\ref{thm:unified_bound}) derives the risk bound using the Procrustes-optimal alignment $W^*$, which yields the \emph{tightest possible feature bound} via the nuclear norm. Here we present the more general formulation where $W$ is an arbitrary orthogonal matrix, and discuss two important specializations: the identity alignment ($W = I$) and the joint optimization over both feature and head terms. We relegate this material to the appendix because: (i)~the Procrustes solution $W^*$ is the natural, canonical choice that minimizes the feature alignment residual with a closed-form solution; (ii)~in the two primary application regimes of this paper---post-training quantization ($W^* \approx I$) and frozen-head fine-tuning ($\gamma = 0$ for any $W$)---alternative choices of $W$ yield negligible practical differences; and (iii)~the general formulation introduces a $\delta$--$\gamma$ trade-off that complicates interpretation without improving the bound in these regimes.

\subsection{The General Bound for Arbitrary $W$}
\label{app:general_bound}

The exact geometric decomposition (Proposition~\ref{prop:exact_decomposition}) generalizes to any $W \in \mathcal{O}(d)$. Expanding the squared Frobenius norm and using the orthogonality of $W$:
\begin{equation}
\frac{1}{n} \| Z_T - Z_P W \|_F^2
\;=\;
(\rho_T - \rho_P)^2
\;+\;
2 \rho_T \rho_P \big( 1 - \Omega_W \big),
\end{equation}
where $\Omega_W(Z_T, Z_P)$ is the \emph{alignment-dependent similarity}:
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:omega_W_def}
\Omega_W(Z_T, Z_P) \;:=\; \frac{\operatorname{Tr}(Z_T^\top Z_P\, W)}{\|Z_T\|_F \, \|Z_P\|_F}.
\end{equation}
This yields a family of risk bounds parameterized by $W$:
\begin{equation}
|\mathcal{R}_T - \mathcal{R}_P| \;\le\; \underbrace{K_{\mathrm{feat}} \sqrt{(\rho_T - \rho_P)^2 + 2\rho_T\rho_P(1 - \Omega_W)}}_{\delta(W)} + \underbrace{K_{\mathrm{pred}} \|\Sigma_P^{1/2}(W H_T - H_P)\|_F}_{\gamma(W)}.
\end{equation}
The Procrustes solution $W^*$ minimizes $\delta(W)$ alone, yielding $\Omega_{W^*} = \Omega$ (the nuclear-norm similarity). However, $W^*$ enters the head term $\gamma(W^*)$, which can be inflated when the rotation is applied to a nearly preserved head ($H_T \approx H_P$). This creates a fundamental trade-off: $W^*$ gives the tightest feature bound but may worsen the head bound, while $W = I$ preserves head coherence at the cost of a looser feature bound.

\subsection{The Trace Specialization ($W = I$)}
\label{app:trace_specialization}

Setting $W = I$, the alignment-dependent similarity reduces to the \emph{trace similarity}:
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:omega_tr}
\Omega_{\mathrm{tr}} \;:=\; \Omega_I \;=\; \frac{\operatorname{Tr}(Z_T^\top Z_P)}{\|Z_T\|_F\,\|Z_P\|_F}.
\end{equation}

\paragraph{Ordering of Similarities.}
Since the Procrustes solution maximizes $\operatorname{Tr}(Z_T^\top Z_P W)$ over $\mathcal{O}(d)$:
\begin{equation}
\operatorname{Tr}(Z_T^\top Z_P) \;\le\; \|Z_T^\top Z_P\|_*,
\end{equation}
which gives $\Omega_{\mathrm{tr}} \le \Omega$. Equality holds if and only if $W^* = I$, i.e., $Z_T^\top Z_P$ is symmetric positive semidefinite.

\paragraph{When does $\Omega_{\mathrm{tr}} = \Omega$?}
Let $Z_T^\top Z_P = U\Sigma V^\top$ be the SVD. Then $\Omega_{\mathrm{tr}} = \Omega$ iff $VU^\top = I$, i.e., $U = V$, which holds when $Z_T^\top Z_P$ is symmetric. In post-training quantization with mild noise, $Z_F^\top Z_Q \approx Z_F^\top Z_F$ is approximately SPSD, so $\Omega_{\mathrm{tr}} \approx \Omega$. As quantization becomes more aggressive, asymmetric perturbations cause $\Omega_{\mathrm{tr}}$ to fall below $\Omega$, and this gap reflects genuine asymmetric distortion that a rotation-invariant metric would mask.

\paragraph{Full Bound under $W = I$.}
The identity alignment yields:
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:bound_identity}
|\mathcal{R}_T - \mathcal{R}_P|
\;\le\;
K_{\mathrm{feat}} \sqrt{(\rho_T - \rho_P)^2 + 2\rho_T\rho_P(1 - \Omega_{\mathrm{tr}})}
\;+\;
K_{\mathrm{pred}}\,\|\Sigma_P^{1/2}(H_T - H_P)\|_F.
\end{equation}
The head term reduces to $\|H_T - H_P\|$ without rotational contamination, which vanishes when the prediction head is preserved (as in quantization with FP16 heads, or frozen-head LoRA).

\paragraph{Relation to CKA.}
Both $\Omega_{\mathrm{tr}}$ and the Frobenius similarity $\Omega_F$ (Appendix~\ref{app:tightness}) are bounded above by $\Omega$ (since $\operatorname{Tr}(M) \le \|M\|_*$ and $\|M\|_F \le \|M\|_*$), but $\Omega_{\mathrm{tr}}$ and $\Omega_F$ do not have a fixed ordering relative to each other. Linear CKA avoids explicit rotational alignment like $\Omega_{\mathrm{tr}}$, but uses the Frobenius norm rather than the trace, and lacks a direct operational interpretation as an alignment residual.


\subsection{Joint Optimization on the Stiefel Manifold}
\label{app:joint_opt}

The tightest bound over all orthogonal alignments requires solving:
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:joint_obj}
W_{\mathrm{opt}} = \arg\min_{W \in \mathcal{O}(d)}
\Big[
\underbrace{K_{\mathrm{feat}} \sqrt{\tfrac{1}{n}\|Z_T - Z_P W\|_F^2}}_{\delta(W)}
\;+\;
\underbrace{K_{\mathrm{pred}}\,\|\Sigma_P^{1/2}(W H_T - H_P)\|_F}_{\gamma(W)}
\Big].
\end{equation}
Unlike the standard Procrustes problem (which minimizes $\delta$ alone and admits the closed-form $W^* = VU^\top$), the addition of $\gamma(W)$ introduces a quadratic dependence on $W$ through the head term, related to the \emph{Weighted Orthogonal Procrustes Problem} (WOPP)~\cite{gower2004procrustes}.

\paragraph{Why No Closed-Form Solution Exists.}
The feature term $\delta(W)$ is concave in $\operatorname{Tr}(Z_T^\top Z_P W)$, while $\gamma(W)$ is convex in $W$. Their sum is neither convex nor concave on $\mathcal{O}(d)$, precluding a simple variational characterization.

\paragraph{Special Case: Isotropic Features.}
If $\Sigma_P \approx \lambda I$, the head term becomes independent of $W$ and the joint objective reduces to a standard Procrustes problem. However, the isotropic assumption is unrealistic for LLM representations, which exhibit well-documented anisotropy~\cite{ethayarajh2019contextual, dettmers2022gpt3}.

\paragraph{Numerical Solution.}
For the general case, $W_{\mathrm{opt}}$ can be approximated via Riemannian gradient descent on the Stiefel manifold, but this entangles scale, shape, and head components, destroying the clean physical interpretation.

\paragraph{Practical Irrelevance for This Paper's Regimes.}
In the two primary regimes:
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Quantization ($H_T \approx H_P$):} The head term is negligible, so $W_{\mathrm{opt}} \approx W^*$ and the Procrustes solution suffices.
    \item \textbf{LoRA ($H_T = H_P$):} The head term vanishes for any $W$, so the joint problem reduces to standard Procrustes.
\end{itemize}
The Riemannian formulation becomes relevant only when comparing models with \emph{both} rotated features and divergent heads (e.g., full-parameter SFT). We leave this regime to future work.
